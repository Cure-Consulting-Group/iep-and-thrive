### Math Block (9:20–10:20)

**Objective**: Students will model division as grouping (measurement division) using concrete manipulatives, understanding that division can mean making groups of a certain size and counting how many groups. (IEP Goal: Math Computation — solve division problems within 100 using models.)

**Materials**:
- Two-color counters (20+ per student)
- Division mats (use the reverse side — or print a new version with one "Total" circle and a "Group Size" box)
- Snap cubes (for building equal groups)
- Dry-erase boards + markers
- Chart paper: update "Division" anchor chart — add "Grouping" alongside "Sharing"
- Word problem cards — Set B: grouping problems (8 cards, laminated)

#### Warm-Up: Multiplication Fact Fluency (5 min)
- **"Around the World"** style: Two students stand. Teacher shows a multiplication flash card (x2, x5, x10). First to answer correctly moves to the next student. Loser sits down.
- Cover 10–12 facts. Keep it encouraging — emphasize speed is growing, not competition.

#### Direct Instruction (15 min) — CRA Level: Concrete

1. **Connect to Yesterday (3 min)**:
   - *"Yesterday we learned division as SHARING — you know the total and you know how many groups, and you figure out how many go in each group."*
   - Write on the board: 12 ÷ 3 = 4 → "12 shared among 3 groups = 4 in each group."
   - *"Today we'll learn a SECOND way to think about division: GROUPING."*

2. **Model Grouping (6 min)**:
   - New story: *"I have 12 nails. I need to put them into bags of 4. How many bags can I fill?"*
   - *"This time, I know the TOTAL and I know the GROUP SIZE. I need to find out how many groups I can make."*
   - Model with counters: Place 12 counters on the mat. *"I'm going to scoop out groups of 4."*
     - Scoop 4 → "That's 1 bag."
     - Scoop 4 → "That's 2 bags."
     - Scoop 4 → "That's 3 bags."
     - *"How many bags did I fill?"* (3)
   - Record: **12 ÷ 4 = 3** → "12 put into groups of 4 = 3 groups."
   - *"SAME equation as yesterday (12 ÷ 4 = 3) but a DIFFERENT picture in our heads."*

3. **Compare the Two Types (6 min)**:
   - Draw a T-chart on the anchor chart:
     | Sharing (Partitive) | Grouping (Measurement) |
     |---|---|
     | I know the # of groups | I know the group size |
     | I find: how many in each group | I find: how many groups |
     | "Share 12 among 3 friends" | "Put 12 into bags of 4" |
   - Guided try: *"I have 20 counters. Make groups of 5. How many groups?"*
   - Students scoop groups of 5 from their 20 counters. Count groups: 4.
   - **20 ÷ 5 = 4** → "20 put into groups of 5 = 4 groups."
   - Repeat: *"Now take 18 counters. Make groups of 3."* (18 ÷ 3 = 6)

#### Guided Practice (15 min)

- **"How Many Groups?" Partner Activity**:
- Teacher presents 6 problems. Students work with counters, scooping groups of the specified size:
  1. 10 ÷ 2 = ___ (groups of 2 from 10)
  2. 15 ÷ 5 = ___ (groups of 5 from 15)
  3. 12 ÷ 6 = ___ (groups of 6 from 12)
  4. 20 ÷ 10 = ___ (groups of 10 from 20)
  5. 16 ÷ 4 = ___ (groups of 4 from 16)
  6. 24 ÷ 8 = ___ (stretch — groups of 8 from 24)

- After each: student writes the equation and draws a quick grouping diagram (circles with dots inside) on the dry-erase board.
- **Key question**: *"Is this a sharing problem or a grouping problem? How do you know?"*

#### Independent Practice (15 min)

- **Word Problem Cards — Set B (Grouping)**: Students solve 4–5 problems independently.
  - Card 1: "A builder has 20 tiles. He puts 5 tiles in each row. How many rows can he make?" (20 ÷ 5 = 4)
  - Card 2: "There are 12 chairs. The teacher puts 4 chairs at each table. How many tables are there?" (12 ÷ 4 = 3)
  - Card 3: "Sam has 18 blocks. He stacks them in towers of 3. How many towers does he make?" (18 ÷ 3 = 6)
  - Card 4: "A library has 15 new books. They are put on shelves with 5 books per shelf. How many shelves are used?" (15 ÷ 5 = 3)
  - Card 5 (challenge — mixed): "A carpenter has 24 screws. She uses 6 screws for each shelf. How many shelves can she build? If she then shares the shelves equally between 2 rooms, how many shelves per room?" (24 ÷ 6 = 4 shelves; 4 ÷ 2 = 2 per room — two-step)

- Students must label each problem as "sharing" or "grouping" AND draw a diagram.

#### Closure (5 min)
- **Quick Sort**: Teacher reads 4 mini-problems aloud. Students hold up a card labeled "S" (sharing) or "G" (grouping):
  - "8 cookies shared among 2 kids." (S)
  - "8 cookies, 4 per bag — how many bags?" (G)
  - "12 pencils for 3 cups." (S or G depending on interpretation — discuss!)
  - "15 blocks in stacks of 5." (G)
- *"Division has two faces — sharing and grouping. The equation looks the same, but the STORY is different. Tomorrow we'll connect both to multiplication."*

#### Data Collection
- Guided practice: record accuracy (/6) per student
- Word problem cards: collect, score (/5), note whether students correctly identified sharing vs. grouping
- Observational: Who transitioned smoothly between sharing and grouping models? Who conflated them?

#### Differentiation
- **Group A (Below level)**: Use only groups of 2 and 5 (most familiar from skip counting). Provide "grouping mats" with pre-drawn circles in the target group size. Word problems use simple numbers (totals ≤15). Skip the two-step Card 5.
- **Group B (On level)**: Full lesson as described. All 5 cards including the mixed problem.
- **Group C (Above level)**: After guided practice, challenge: *"Write your OWN grouping word problem using a total of 30. Trade with a partner and solve."* Introduce: *"If 12 ÷ 4 = 3, what multiplication fact does that remind you of?"* (Bridge to tomorrow.)

#### Part 1: OG Lesson — Vowel Teams ou/ow (/ow/ sound) + R-Controlled or (10:30–11:05)

**Objective**: Students will read and spell words containing the vowel teams ou (house) and ow (cow) for the /ow/ sound, and the r-controlled vowel or (fork), distinguishing ow-/oh/ (snow) from ow-/ow/ (cow). (IEP Goal: Decoding — vowel teams and r-controlled vowels.)

**Materials**:
- OG phonogram cards: full deck + new cards (ou, ow with /ow/ notation, or)
- Red word cards: enough, laugh
- Sand/salt trays
- Dry-erase boards + markers
- Decodable word list for ou/ow/or (printed)
- Highlighters
- "Switch Hitter" anchor chart for ow

**OG Lesson Sequence**:

1. **Phonogram Review Drill (4 min)**:
   - Full deck review including yesterday's oi, oy, ar.
   - Extra pause on ow card: *"Remember, we learned ow says /oh/ in Week 2 — like in 'snow.' Today it has a SECOND sound."*

2. **New Concept: ou and ow for /ow/ (12 min)**:
   - *"Today's sound is /ow/ — like when you stub your toe: OW!"*
   - Hold up **ou** card: *"o-u says /ow/ — like in house, cloud, loud. Key word: house."*
   - Hold up **ow** card: *"o-w can ALSO say /ow/ — like in cow, town, down. Key word: cow."*
   - **The "Switch Hitter" — ow**: Create anchor chart:
     - ow says /oh/ → snow, grow, flow, yellow, window (learned Week 2)
     - ow says /ow/ → cow, how, now, town, brown, crowd, power
     - *"How do you know which sound? You have to try BOTH and see which one makes a real word. That's called being a flexible reader."*
   - **Position patterns**:
     - ou is usually in the MIDDLE: house, cloud, ground, mouse, found, sound, shout
     - ow for /ow/ can be at the END or MIDDLE: cow, plow, how, now, town, brown, crowd, flower, tower, power
   - **Multisensory**: Sky-write "ou" saying /ow/. Then "ow" saying /ow/. Sand tray practice: write "ou" then "ow" three times each.
   - **Sorting activity (quick)**: Teacher reads 8 words. Students write either "ou" or "ow" on dry-erase boards:
     - cloud (ou), brown (ow), shout (ou), town (ow), round (ou), plow (ow), mouth (ou), flower (ow)

3. **New Concept: R-Controlled or (6 min)**:
   - Hold up **or** card: *"Our second r-controlled vowel: or says /or/ — like in fork, corn, store. Key word: fork."*
   - Write on chart paper:
     - **or**: fork, corn, storm, short, sport, order, morning, corner
   - **Arm tapping**: Practice with 2-syllable or words: morning (mor-ning), corner (cor-ner), order (or-der), forest (for-est).
   - Compare ar and or: *"ar says /ar/ like car. or says /or/ like fork. Different vowels, but both are controlled by that bossy r."*

4. **Word Reading Practice (7 min)**:
   - Decodable word list:
     - **ou words**: out, loud, found, round, ground, cloud, south, mouth, scout, proud
     - **ow words (/ow/)**: owl, how, plow, crowd, tower, power, flower, towel, growl, frown
     - **or words**: for, sort, born, porch, north, sport, corner, morning
     - **Mixed (all Week 3 so far)**: coin, star, joy, house, fork, brown, point, barn, cloud, torn
   - Students read with a partner — "ping pong reading" (alternate words).
   - Teacher pulls 2–3 students individually for a quick accuracy check on the mixed list.

5. **Dictation (3 min)**:
   - Words: **loud**, **town**, **corn**, **found**, **sport**, **crowd**
   - Sentence: **"The loud crowd stood in the storm."**
   - Students write on dry-erase boards. Check and correct.

6. **Red Words: enough, laugh (3 min)**:
   - **enough**: Write on red card. Underline "ough" — *"Another 'ough' word! This time it says /ŭf/. So we have thought (/aw/), through (/oo/), and now enough (/ŭf/). Three different sounds for the same letters!"*
   - **laugh**: Write on red card. Underline "augh" — *"This says /laf/. The 'augh' part sounds like /af/. Tricky!"*
   - Say, spell, write, say — 3 repetitions each.
   - Quick review all 4 red words so far: thought, through, enough, laugh. Flash cards — students spell on boards.

**Data Collection**:
- Word reading accuracy per pattern (ou, ow, or) for students individually checked
- Dictation score (/6 words + /1 sentence)
- Switch hitter success: When reading ow words, did students try both sounds? Note who defaulted to only /oh/ or only /ow/
- Red word accuracy: who can spell all 4 from memory?

#### Part 2: Narrative Writing — Story Planning (11:05–11:30)

**Objective**: Students will plan a narrative story using a graphic organizer, selecting a character, setting, and problem for their chosen story seed. (IEP Goal: Written Expression — plan a narrative with story elements.)

**Materials**:
- Story planning graphic organizer (printed — sections: Character, Setting, Problem, Events/What Happens, Solution)
- Story seed brainstorms from yesterday
- Mentor text excerpt: a short published narrative with clear character, setting, problem, solution (e.g., an excerpt from *Knuffle Bunny* by Mo Willems or *Sheila Rae, the Brave* by Kevin Henkes)
- Chart paper: "Story Elements" anchor chart
- Sticky notes (for moveable event planning)

**Lesson**:

1. **Mini-Lesson: Story Elements (10 min)**:
   - *"Every story — whether it's a book, a movie, or the story you're going to write — has the same building blocks."*
   - Introduce the **Story Elements anchor chart**:
     - **Character**: Who is the story about? What are they like?
     - **Setting**: Where and when does the story take place?
     - **Problem**: What goes wrong? What does the character want or need?
     - **Solution**: How is the problem fixed?
   - **Mentor text read-aloud** (5 min): Read the short narrative excerpt. After reading, ask:
     - *"Who is the character?"*
     - *"Where does it take place?"*
     - *"What was the problem?"*
     - *"How was it solved?"*
   - Fill in a sample story map on chart paper with the class responses.
   - *"See? Every story follows this structure. Now YOU'RE going to plan YOUR story the same way."*

2. **Story Planning with Graphic Organizer (12 min)**:
   - Students pull out their story seed brainstorms from yesterday.
   - Distribute the story planning graphic organizer.
   - *"Look at the seed you starred. Now let's build it into a plan."*
   - Guide step by step (give 2–3 minutes per section):
     - **Character**: *"Who is your story about? Is it you? Someone else? Write their name and 2–3 words to describe them."*
     - **Setting**: *"Where does your story happen? When — morning, afternoon, summer, winter? Be specific — 'the backyard on a hot day' is better than 'outside.'"*
     - **Problem**: *"What goes wrong? What does the character want that they can't have? What's the challenge?"*
   - *"We'll plan Events and Solution tomorrow — for now, the CHARACTER, SETTING, and PROBLEM are your foundation."*
   - Circulate and conference with 3–4 students: *"Tell me about your character. What's the problem in your story? That sounds like a great setting — can you add one more detail?"*

3. **Share (3 min)**:
   - 2 volunteers share their Character, Setting, and Problem with the class.
   - Class gives a "thumbs up" if they can picture the setting and understand the problem.

**Data Collection**:
- Collect graphic organizers — check completeness (3/3 sections filled)
- Note quality of problem: Is it specific and story-worthy, or vague? (Conference targets for Wednesday)
- Note students who wrote more than required vs. those who wrote minimally

**Differentiation**:
- **Below level**: Provide a modified organizer with sentence frames:
  - "My character is ___ . They are ___ (describing word)."
  - "My story happens at ___ during ___ ."
  - "The problem is ___ ."
  - Teacher or aide conferences with each below-level student to verbally plan before writing.
- **On level**: Complete the graphic organizer as described.
- **Above level**: Add to the organizer: sketch a quick picture of the setting, list 3 events that happen, and begin thinking about the solution. *"Can you also think about what your character FEELS when the problem happens?"*

### Exploration & Enrichment (12:00–1:00)

**Activity**: Architecture — How Buildings Tell Stories

**Enrichment Category**: Cultural Exploration (Tuesday theme)

**Overview**: Students explore how architecture reflects culture, history, and purpose. They examine images of diverse buildings from around the world, discuss what the designs communicate, and sketch their own "dream building" that tells a story about them or their community.

**Materials**:
- Printed images or slideshow: 8–10 diverse buildings (examples: a mosque, a log cabin, a skyscraper, a lighthouse, a school, a treehouse, a castle, a Long Island home, the NYC Public Library, a Lenape longhouse)
- Drawing paper (large, 11x17 if available)
- Colored pencils, markers, crayons
- "Building Observation Sheet" (printed — What do you notice? What is this building for? What story does it tell?)
- Rulers (for straight lines in drawings)
- Architecture vocabulary cards: foundation, structure, design, column, arch, roof, window, entrance

**Schedule**:

1. **Introduction: "Every Building Has a Story" (10 min)**:
   - Show 3–4 building images. For each, ask:
     - *"What do you NOTICE about this building?"* (Accept all observations)
     - *"What do you think this building is FOR?"* (Function)
     - *"What STORY does this building tell? What does it say about the people who built it?"* (Culture, values)
   - Example discussion: A lighthouse — *"It's tall and stands alone near water. It was built to keep ships safe. It tells us the people who lived here cared about protecting sailors."*
   - Introduce vocabulary: foundation, structure, design. *"These are words builders use — and writers use them too. Stories have structure, just like buildings."*

2. **Building Observation Gallery (10 min)**:
   - Post 6–8 building images around the room (gallery walk style).
   - Students walk with a partner and their "Building Observation Sheet." At each image, they write 1–2 observations.
   - *"Look at the shapes, the materials, the size. Think about WHY the builder made those choices."*
   - After 8 minutes, gather the group. Quick share: *"Which building surprised you? Which one would you want to visit?"*

3. **Design Your Dream Building (25 min)**:
   - *"Now YOU are the architect. Design a building that tells a story about YOU or about something you care about."*
   - Prompt ideas:
     - A school designed for YOUR perfect learning day
     - A community center for your neighborhood
     - A museum of things you love
     - A house for your family that shows what matters to you
   - Students sketch on large paper. Encourage:
     - Label at least 3 parts of the building
     - Include a title for the building
     - Write 2–3 sentences on the back: "My building is ___ . I designed it this way because ___ ."
   - **Reading connection**: Use at least one r-controlled vowel word in your labels (e.g., porch, garden, corner, floor, barn, art room).
   - **Math connection**: *"How many windows does your building have? If you divided them equally on 4 walls, how many per wall?"*

4. **Share & Discuss (5 min)**:
   - 3–4 students share their designs and explain the "story" their building tells.
   - Audience practices active listening (from morning SEL).
   - Photos taken for portfolio.

5. **Closing Circle (10 min)**:
   - *"Today we saw that buildings — like stories — have structure, a purpose, and a message. This week, you're building STORIES in writing and building MATH understanding too."*
   - Self-monitoring checklist review.
   - Preview tomorrow: *"Tomorrow is our STEM challenge — you're going to engineer a bridge that can hold weight. Start thinking about strong shapes and designs."*
   - Dismissal at 1:00.

**Academic Connections**:
- **Reading/Writing**: Vocabulary development (structure, design, foundation). Descriptive writing on the building explanation. Connection to narrative setting — *"Your story needs a detailed setting, just like an architect needs a detailed plan."*
- **Math**: Counting, symmetry, division of building features (windows, doors).
- **Comprehension**: Observation and inference — *"What can you figure out about a place by looking at it carefully?"*
