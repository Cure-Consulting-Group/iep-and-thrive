### Math Block (9:20–10:20)

**Objective**: Students will solve multiplication and division word problems using two schemas — equal groups and comparison — selecting the correct operation and representing with bar models. (IEP Goal: Math Problem Solving — solve 1-step word problems involving multiplication/division with ≥75% accuracy, showing work with a model.)

**Materials**:
- Word problem schema anchor charts (2 new charts: "Equal Groups" and "Comparison")
- Bar model/tape diagram template (printed)
- Dry-erase boards + markers
- Counters (available for students who need concrete verification)
- Word problem cards — Set D: mixed equal groups and comparison (10 cards, color-coded)
- "Problem Type Sort" mat (2 columns: Equal Groups / Comparison)

#### Warm-Up: Fact Family Speed Round (5 min)
- Teacher writes a product on the board. Students write as many fact family equations as they can in 30 seconds.
  - Round 1: 12 (3x4=12, 4x3=12, 12÷3=4, 12÷4=3; also 2x6=12, 6x2=12, 12÷2=6, 12÷6=2)
  - Round 2: 20 (4x5, 2x10, etc.)
  - Round 3: 15 (3x5, 5x3, etc.)
- *"Numbers can have MORE than one fact family. 12 has lots of families — it's a popular number!"*

#### Direct Instruction (15 min) — Word Problem Schemas

1. **Schema 1: Equal Groups (7 min)**:
   - **Anchor chart — "Equal Groups Problems"**:
     - You know: the NUMBER of groups AND the SIZE of each group → Multiply
     - You know: the TOTAL and the NUMBER of groups → Divide (find group size — sharing)
     - You know: the TOTAL and the SIZE of each group → Divide (find number of groups — grouping)
   - **Model problem (multiply)**: *"A builder buys 5 boxes of nails. Each box has 10 nails. How many nails in all?"*
     - Draw bar model: 5 sections, each labeled 10. Total = ?
     - 5 x 10 = 50 nails.
   - **Model problem (divide — sharing)**: *"A builder has 20 nails and 4 toolboxes. She puts the same number in each toolbox. How many per toolbox?"*
     - Draw bar model: total bar = 20, divided into 4 sections. Each section = ?
     - 20 ÷ 4 = 5 nails per toolbox.
   - **Model problem (divide — grouping)**: *"A builder has 20 nails. She puts 5 in each toolbox. How many toolboxes does she fill?"*
     - Draw bar model: total = 20, each section = 5. How many sections?
     - 20 ÷ 5 = 4 toolboxes.
   - *"See? Same numbers — different QUESTIONS. The bar model helps you SEE what you know and what you need to find."*

2. **Schema 2: Comparison (8 min)**:
   - **Anchor chart — "Comparison Problems"**:
     - "__ times as many" → Multiply
     - "How many times more?" → Divide
   - **Model problem (multiply)**: *"Marcus built 4 towers. Nia built 3 TIMES as many towers. How many towers did Nia build?"*
     - Draw comparison bar model: Marcus = 1 bar labeled 4. Nia = 3 bars of the same size.
     - 4 x 3 = 12 towers.
   - **Model problem (divide)**: *"Marcus built 4 towers. Nia built 12 towers. How many TIMES as many towers did Nia build?"*
     - Draw comparison bar model: Marcus = short bar (4). Nia = long bar (12). *"How many of Marcus's bars fit inside Nia's?"*
     - 12 ÷ 4 = 3 times as many.
   - Key language: *"Times as many" is the signal for comparison problems.*
   - Practice identifying: teacher reads 3 mini-problems, students hold up "EG" (equal groups) or "C" (comparison):
     - "5 bags, 3 apples each — how many apples?" (EG)
     - "Sam has 4 cards. Jake has 3 times as many." (C)
     - "18 stickers shared among 6 friends." (EG)

#### Guided Practice (15 min)

- **"Problem Type Sort" Activity (8 min)**: Students work in pairs with 8 word problem cards. They must:
  1. Read each problem
  2. Place it on the correct side of the sort mat: Equal Groups or Comparison
  3. Write the equation on their dry-erase board

  - Problem cards:
    - EG: "3 shelves with 6 books each. Total?" (3 x 6 = 18)
    - EG: "15 blocks split into 5 equal piles. How many per pile?" (15 ÷ 5 = 3)
    - C: "Ana has 2 dogs. Kai has 5 times as many dogs." (2 x 5 = 10)
    - EG: "24 chairs, 4 per table. How many tables?" (24 ÷ 4 = 6)
    - C: "Team A scored 3 goals. Team B scored 4 times as many." (3 x 4 = 12)
    - EG: "4 bags of 10 marbles. Total?" (4 x 10 = 40)
    - C: "Luis read 6 books. Mia read 18 books. How many times as many?" (18 ÷ 6 = 3)
    - EG: "20 cookies shared among 10 friends." (20 ÷ 10 = 2)

- **Bar Model Practice (7 min)**: Choose 3 of the 8 problems and draw a full bar model on the template. Must include:
  - Labeled bars
  - The equation
  - A sentence answering the question

#### Independent Practice (15 min)

- **Word Problem Cards — Set D (Mixed)**: 5 problems. Students solve independently:
  - Card 1 (EG-multiply): "A construction crew builds 3 walls each day for 5 days. How many walls do they build in all?" (3 x 5 = 15)
  - Card 2 (EG-divide): "A plumber has 18 pipes. He puts 6 pipes at each job site. How many job sites does he supply?" (18 ÷ 6 = 3)
  - Card 3 (Comparison-multiply): "Building A has 4 floors. Building B has 5 times as many floors. How many floors does Building B have?" (4 x 5 = 20)
  - Card 4 (EG-divide): "An electrician has 24 light switches. She installs them equally in 4 rooms. How many per room?" (24 ÷ 4 = 6)
  - Card 5 (Comparison-divide + challenge): "A carpenter cut 6 boards on Monday. On Tuesday, she cut 3 times as many. How many did she cut on Tuesday? How many boards total for both days?" (6 x 3 = 18 on Tuesday; 6 + 18 = 24 total — two-step)

  For each: label the problem type (EG or C), draw a bar model, write the equation, answer in a sentence.

#### Closure (5 min)
- **Think-Pair-Share**: *"What is the DIFFERENCE between an equal groups problem and a comparison problem?"*
- Collect 3 responses. Reinforce: *"Equal groups: groups of the same size. Comparison: one quantity is some number of TIMES another."*
- *"Tomorrow we'll use everything we've learned — multiplication, division, fact families, bar models — on our Weekly Probe. It's not a test to worry about. It's a check to see how much we've GROWN."*

#### Data Collection
- Problem Type Sort: record accuracy of sorting (/8) and equation writing
- Bar model templates: collect and check for correct structure, labels, equations
- Word problem cards Set D: score (/5), note problem type identification accuracy
- Key indicator: Can students independently identify the problem type and select the correct operation?

#### Differentiation
- **Group A (Below level)**: Focus on equal groups problems only (comparison is introduced but not required independently). Use counters alongside bar models. Limit numbers: factors and divisors ≤5, totals ≤20. Provide a "clue words" reference card: "each," "per," "every" = equal groups; "times as many" = comparison. Word problems Cards 1, 2, and 4 only.
- **Group B (On level)**: Full lesson as described. All 5 word problem cards.
- **Group C (Above level)**: All 5 cards required. Additional challenge: *"Write your OWN comparison word problem about the building theme. Include the bar model and solution."* Introduce: *"Building C has 24 floors. That's 4 times as many as Building A. How many floors does Building A have?"* (24 ÷ 4 = 6 — missing smaller quantity in comparison).

#### Part 1: OG Lesson — Cumulative Review + Arm Tapping Syllable Division (10:30–11:05)

**Objective**: Students will (1) read words containing all Week 3 phonics patterns with ≥80% accuracy, and (2) use arm tapping to divide and read 2-syllable words containing r-controlled vowels and vowel teams. (IEP Goal: Decoding — vowel teams and r-controlled vowels; syllable division.)

**Materials**:
- OG phonogram cards: full deck
- Red word cards: all 6
- Decodable passage (mixed patterns — r-controlled + vowel teams, 1 per student)
- Dry-erase boards + markers
- "Syllable Type" chart (classroom — updated through Week 3)
- Multisyllabic word cards (2-syllable words with target patterns, 20 cards)
- Arm tapping reference poster

**OG Lesson Sequence**:

1. **Phonogram Drill (4 min)**:
   - Full deck, rapid pace. Students respond chorally.
   - "Switch hitter" ow: students give BOTH sounds.
   - "Triplets" er/ir/ur: teacher holds up each, students respond with /er/ and the key word (her, bird, burn).

2. **Cumulative Word Reading — Pattern Sort (8 min)**:
   - 20 word cards on the table (face down). Students work in pairs.
   - Each pair flips a card, reads the word, and sorts it into the correct pattern category:
     - oi/oy: coil, enjoy, moist, royal
     - ou/ow (/ow/): shout, crowd, bounce, power
     - au/aw: fault, crawl, pause, yawn
     - ar: shark, garden, march
     - or: storm, corner, sport
     - er/ir/ur: fern, shirt, purple
   - After sorting, each pair reads their sorted columns to the other pair at their table. Verify correct placement.
   - Debrief: *"Which patterns were easiest to sort? Which words could trick you?"*

3. **Syllable Division — Arm Tapping Focus (12 min)**:
   - **Review the arm tapping procedure**:
     - Hold your left arm out in front of you.
     - First syllable: tap your left shoulder.
     - Second syllable: tap your inner elbow.
     - Third syllable (if needed): tap your wrist.
     - After tapping, sweep your hand down your arm to blend the syllables together.
   - **Model with 5 words**:
     - "garden" → tap shoulder: "gar-" | tap elbow: "-den" | sweep: "garden"
     - "enjoy" → tap: "en-" | tap: "-joy" | sweep: "enjoy"
     - "turtle" → tap: "tur-" | tap: "-tle" | sweep: "turtle"
     - "morning" → tap: "mor-" | tap: "-ning" | sweep: "morning"
     - "outside" → tap: "out-" | tap: "-side" | sweep: "outside"
   - **Students practice 10 words** (teacher says the word, students arm tap and then write it on dry-erase boards):
     - market, flower, birthday, husband, council, daughter, forty, Thursday, orbit, oyster
   - For each word, after writing, ask: *"What syllable types do you see?"*
     - market: ar (r-controlled) + closed
     - flower: ow (vowel team) + er (r-controlled)
     - birthday: ir (r-controlled) + closed
     - Thursday: ur (r-controlled) + closed
   - *"Arm tapping helps you BREAK words apart so you can read each piece. Then blend them back together."*

4. **Decodable Passage Reading (8 min)**:
   - Passage titled: **"The Farm in the Morning"** (controlled text featuring ar, or, er/ir/ur, oi/oy, ou/ow, au/aw):

     > *Mark woke up before dawn. The birds were chirping in the yard. He put on his shirt and walked out to the barn. The cows were loud. "Good morning," Mark said to the horse. He poured corn into the trough. A girl named Joy came to help. She wore a purple scarf. "I brought my straw hat," Joy said with a laugh. Together they worked through the morning. The farm was their favorite place on earth.*

   - First read: Teacher reads aloud while students follow along. Students circle any words with this week's patterns (using a pencil, lightly).
   - Second read: Students whisper-read to themselves. Teacher circulates and listens to 3–4 students individually.
   - Third read: Partner read — alternate sentences.
   - Comprehension quick check (verbal):
     - *"Who are the characters?"* (Mark and Joy)
     - *"What's the setting?"* (A farm in the morning)
     - *"What happens in the story?"* (They do morning farm chores together)
     - *"Find a word with the /oy/ sound."* (Joy) *"Find an r-controlled ar word."* (Mark, yard, barn, scarf, farm, star)

5. **Red Word Review (3 min)**:
   - Speed round: Flash all 6 red word cards. Students spell on dry-erase boards.
   - *"Tomorrow, these red words will be on the assessment. Let's make sure they're solid."*
   - Students who miss any: trace the word 3 times on the desk with their finger, saying each letter.

**Data Collection**:
- Pattern sort: note accuracy per pair — which patterns caused sorting errors?
- Arm tapping: who tapped independently? Who needed teacher support to identify syllable breaks?
- Decodable passage: record accuracy for individually listened students (words correct/total words)
- Comprehension: who identified story elements quickly? (preview for tomorrow's assessment)
- Red word cumulative accuracy: /6 per student

#### Part 2: Narrative Writing — Dialogue Introduction (11:05–11:30)

**Objective**: Students will learn the punctuation rules for writing dialogue and practice writing 2–3 lines of dialogue for their narrative stories. (IEP Goal: Written Expression — use dialogue with correct punctuation in narrative writing.)

**Materials**:
- "Dialogue Rules" anchor chart (built during lesson)
- Practice sentences for dialogue punctuation (on the board)
- Composition notebooks
- Story planning organizers (for reference)
- Mentor text page showing dialogue (from a familiar book)

**Lesson**:

1. **Mini-Lesson: Dialogue Rules (10 min)**:
   - *"Dialogue is when characters TALK in a story. It's the words inside quotation marks. Dialogue makes stories come ALIVE — instead of telling us what happened, we HEAR the characters."*
   - Show mentor text example with dialogue highlighted. Read it aloud with expression.
   - **Anchor chart — "Dialogue Rules"**:
     - Rule 1: Put the speaker's exact words inside quotation marks. **"Let's go!"**
     - Rule 2: Use a comma before the quotation marks when the speaker tag comes first. **She said, "Let's go!"**
     - Rule 3: Put punctuation (period, !, ?) INSIDE the quotation marks. **"Let's go!"** NOT **"Let's go"!**
     - Rule 4: New speaker = new line/paragraph.
   - **Show the pattern**:
     ```
     "I can't believe it!" said Marcus.
     "Believe what?" asked Nia.
     "The bridge held 20 weights!" Marcus shouted.
     ```
   - *"See how each time a new person talks, we start a new line?"*

2. **Guided Practice: Fix the Dialogue (5 min)**:
   - Write 4 sentences on the board with dialogue errors. Students fix on dry-erase boards:
     1. she said lets go to the park. → **She said, "Let's go to the park."**
     2. I love building things said Tom. → **"I love building things," said Tom.**
     3. "Can you help me" asked Joy. → **"Can you help me?" asked Joy.**
     4. Watch out yelled the boy → **"Watch out!" yelled the boy.**
   - Go through each one. Students share corrections. Discuss what changed and why.

3. **Independent Practice: Write Dialogue for Your Story (8 min)**:
   - *"Open your story plan. Think about a moment in your story when your character TALKS. Maybe they say something to another character. Maybe they shout or whisper or ask a question."*
   - *"Write 2–3 lines of dialogue. Use at least 2 of our 4 rules."*
   - Students write in composition notebooks. Circulate and conference:
     - *"Who is talking? What are they feeling when they say this?"*
     - *"Remember — quotation marks go around the EXACT words the character says."*
     - Check for punctuation placement and new-speaker-new-line rule.

4. **Share (2 min)**:
   - 2 volunteers read their dialogue aloud with expression.
   - Class listens for: quotation marks (we can't hear them, but we can hear the expression), and a new speaker.
   - *"Tomorrow, you'll put your opening hook AND your dialogue together into the first paragraph of your narrative draft."*

**Data Collection**:
- Dialogue correction exercise: score /4 per student
- Notebook check: did students write 2–3 lines of dialogue? Check for:
  - Quotation marks present (yes/no)
  - Punctuation inside quotation marks (yes/no)
  - Speaker tag included (yes/no)
  - New speaker = new line (yes/no)
- Note students who need additional support for tomorrow's drafting session

**Differentiation**:
- **Below level**: Provide a dialogue template:
  - "_____," said _____.
  - "______?" asked _____.
  - "______!" shouted _____.
  - Students fill in the blanks with their character's words. Focus on quotation mark placement only — other punctuation rules can be added later.
- **On level**: Write 2–3 lines of dialogue using the anchor chart rules.
- **Above level**: Write 4+ lines of dialogue between two characters. Include at least one question, one exclamation, and one statement. Challenge: *"Can you show a character's FEELING through what they say, without using a feeling word? Instead of 'I'm sad,' maybe 'I don't want to talk about it.'"*

### Exploration & Enrichment (12:00–1:00)

**Activity**: Budget a Building Project (Life Skills Math)

**Enrichment Category**: Life Skills (Thursday theme)

**Overview**: Student teams are given a building project scenario and a fixed budget. They must choose materials from a price list, calculate costs, and stay within budget. This directly applies multiplication, addition, and division skills from the week in a real-world context.

**Materials**:
- "Building Budget" worksheet (1 per student — includes the scenario, price list, planning space, and total calculation area)
- Play money: bills ($1, $5, $10, $20) and coins (quarters, dimes, nickels, pennies)
- Price list poster (displayed large for all to see):
  | Material | Price |
  |----------|-------|
  | Bricks (pack of 10) | $5 |
  | Wooden beams (each) | $3 |
  | Bag of nails (20 nails) | $2 |
  | Roof tiles (pack of 5) | $4 |
  | Window (each) | $6 |
  | Door (each) | $8 |
  | Paint (1 can) | $3 |
  | Cement bag | $7 |
- Calculators (available for checking, not primary tool)
- Chart paper for group comparison at the end

**Schedule**:

1. **Introduction: "Every Builder Needs a Budget" (8 min)**:
   - *"A budget is a PLAN for money. Real builders can't just buy anything they want — they have a set amount of money and have to make smart choices."*
   - Show the price list. Practice reading it: *"How much does a window cost?"* ($6) *"If I need 4 windows, what's my total cost for windows?"* (4 x $6 = $24)
   - **The scenario**: *"Your team has been hired to build a community clubhouse. Your budget is $50. You need to buy materials to build it. Every clubhouse MUST have at least: 1 door, 2 windows, some bricks, some beams, and a roof. Beyond that, you choose."*
   - Distribute play money: each team gets $50 in bills.

2. **Planning Phase (7 min)**:
   - Teams discuss: *"What do we NEED to buy first (requirements)? How much will that cost? How much is left for extras?"*
   - Students calculate the minimum required cost on their worksheets:
     - 1 door: $8
     - 2 windows: $12
     - At least 1 pack of bricks: $5
     - At least 2 beams: $6
     - At least 1 pack of roof tiles: $4
     - Minimum: $35
     - Remaining budget: $15 for extras
   - *"Now decide as a team: What extras do you want? More windows? Paint? Extra bricks? Remember — you CANNOT go over $50."*

3. **Shopping & Calculating (20 min)**:
   - Teams "shop" by writing their material list on the worksheet and calculating the total cost.
   - They physically count out play money to "pay" for each item.
   - **Math connections embedded**:
     - Multiplication: "3 packs of bricks at $5 each = ?"
     - Addition: running total as items are added
     - Subtraction: "How much budget remains?"
     - Division: "If we have $15 left and windows cost $6, can we afford 3 more windows?" (No — 3 x $6 = $18 > $15)
   - Circulate with guiding questions:
     - *"How much have you spent so far? How do you know?"*
     - *"If you want 3 more cans of paint, how much is that? Can you afford it?"*
     - *"You have $7 left. What can you still buy?"*
     - *"If you bought 4 packs of roof tiles, how many INDIVIDUAL tiles is that?"* (4 x 5 = 20)

4. **Presentations & Comparison (15 min)**:
   - Each team presents:
     - What they bought (reading from their material list)
     - Their total cost
     - How much budget remained (or if they had exactly $0 left — bonus challenge)
   - Record on chart paper: Team name, total spent, items bought, money remaining.
   - **Discussion questions**:
     - *"Which team spent the MOST? The LEAST?"*
     - *"If Team A's total was $48 and Team B's was $42, how much MORE did Team A spend?"* ($6 — comparison subtraction)
     - *"If we combined ALL teams' remaining money, how much would we have?"* (addition)
     - *"Was there a 'right' answer? Or did different teams make different CHOICES?"* (Financial literacy: budgets involve priorities)

5. **Closing Circle (10 min)**:
   - *"Building a budget uses the same math we've been learning: multiplication for costs, division for figuring out how to split things, and addition for totals."*
   - **Writing connection**: *"In your story, your character faces a PROBLEM. Sometimes problems are about not having enough — not enough time, not enough help, not enough money. Your character has to make choices, just like you made choices with your budget."*
   - **Vocabulary from today**: budget, cost, total, remaining, afford, purchase, expense.
   - Self-monitoring checklist review. *"Did you use planning today?"*
   - Preview tomorrow: *"Tomorrow is our assessment day AND our mural day. You'll show what you've learned this week, and we'll create something beautiful together."*
   - Dismissal at 1:00.

**Academic Connections**:
- **Math**: Multiplication (unit price x quantity), addition (running total), subtraction (budget remaining), division (Can I afford it? How many can I buy?), comparison (team results).
- **Reading**: Reading the price list, reading the scenario, following written instructions.
- **Writing**: Writing material lists, explaining choices in complete sentences.
- **Executive Function**: Budgeting IS planning. Making choices within constraints. Prioritizing needs vs. wants.
- **SEL**: Team decision-making, compromise, respecting different priorities.
