### Math Block (9:20–10:20)

**Objective**: Administer Weekly Probe #2 (multiplication/division computation) and Division Concept Check (5 conceptual questions). Assess student growth from Week 2 baseline and readiness for Week 4 content.

**Materials**:
- Weekly Probe #2 — Multiplication/Division Computation (printed, 1 per student):
  - 20 problems, 2-minute timed assessment
  - Mix of multiplication (x0, x1, x2, x5, x10) and division (÷2, ÷5, ÷10, ÷3, ÷4)
- Division Concept Check (printed, 1 per student):
  - 5 questions assessing conceptual understanding (untimed)
- Counters (available for concept check — students choose whether to use)
- Dry-erase boards (for review activity while teacher administers individual checks)
- Student answer recording sheet
- Timer (visual countdown)

#### Pre-Assessment Review (10 min)

- **Multiplication Fact Warm-Up**: Flash 10 multiplication cards. Students write answers on dry-erase boards. This is practice, not scored — loosens them up.
- **Division Strategy Review**: *"When you see a division problem and you're not sure, what can you do?"*
  - Think of the related multiplication fact
  - Draw a picture (groups or sharing)
  - Use skip counting
  - *"You have STRATEGIES. Use them."*

#### Weekly Probe #2: Computation (10 min — 2 min timed + 8 min for setup/collection)

- **Administration**:
  - Distribute probe face-down. *"Do NOT turn over until I say go."*
  - *"You have 2 minutes. Work as quickly and accurately as you can. If you get stuck on one, skip it and come back."*
  - *"Ready? Go."*
  - Start timer. Walk the room silently.
  - At 2 minutes: *"Pencils down. Flip your paper over."*
  - Collect probes.

- **Probe Content** (20 problems):
  1. 5 x 2 = ___
  2. 10 ÷ 5 = ___
  3. 3 x 5 = ___
  4. 12 ÷ 3 = ___
  5. 4 x 10 = ___
  6. 20 ÷ 4 = ___
  7. 2 x 8 = ___
  8. 15 ÷ 5 = ___
  9. 5 x 5 = ___
  10. 18 ÷ 3 = ___
  11. 10 x 3 = ___
  12. 10 ÷ 2 = ___
  13. 4 x 5 = ___
  14. 20 ÷ 10 = ___
  15. 2 x 6 = ___
  16. 24 ÷ 4 = ___
  17. 5 x 6 = ___
  18. 16 ÷ 2 = ___
  19. 1 x 9 = ___
  20. 0 x 7 = ___

#### Division Concept Check (15 min — untimed)

- Distribute concept check. *"This part is NOT timed. Take your time. Show your thinking. You may use counters if you want."*

- **5 Questions**:
  1. **Modeling**: "Draw a picture that shows 12 ÷ 3. Write what your picture means in words." (Looking for: correct grouping or sharing diagram; verbal explanation matches)
  2. **Fact Family**: "Write the complete fact family for 4, 5, and 20." (4x5=20, 5x4=20, 20÷4=5, 20÷5=4)
  3. **Word Problem (equal groups)**: "There are 15 plants. A gardener puts 5 plants in each row. How many rows are there? Draw a bar model and write an equation." (15 ÷ 5 = 3)
  4. **Inverse Relationship**: "Sam knows that 6 x 3 = 18. How does that help him solve 18 ÷ 6? Explain." (Looking for: student articulates that division is the inverse/opposite of multiplication)
  5. **Word Problem (comparison — stretch)**: "Team A built 4 walls. Team B built 3 times as many walls. How many walls did Team B build? Draw a model." (4 x 3 = 12)

- Students work independently. Teacher circulates but does not assist (assessment conditions).
- Collect when finished or after 15 minutes, whichever comes first.

#### Post-Assessment Activity (15 min) — While teacher reviews/organizes probes

- **"Multiplication & Division Game: Fact Family Go Fish"**:
  - Students play in groups of 3–4. Each student gets 6 cards from a fact family card deck.
  - To make a "match," you need all 4 facts from one fact family (e.g., 3x4=12, 4x3=12, 12÷3=4, 12÷4=3).
  - Students ask: "Do you have a fact from the 3, 4, 12 family?"
  - First to collect 2 complete families wins.
  - Purpose: reinforcement, low-stakes practice, social interaction after assessment stress.

#### Closure (5 min)
- *"You worked really hard on those assessments. I'm proud of how you used your strategies."*
- *"Raise your hand if you used a multiplication fact to help you with a division problem."* (Celebrate this!)
- *"Next week, we start FRACTIONS. We'll use fraction strips and pattern blocks — lots of hands-on materials. Division actually helps a LOT with fractions."*

#### Data Collection (score after school)
- Computation probe: # correct / 20, # attempted / 20. Compare to Week 2 probe.
- Concept check: score each question individually:
  - Q1 Modeling: /2 (1 for diagram, 1 for explanation)
  - Q2 Fact Family: /4 (1 per equation)
  - Q3 Word Problem: /3 (1 for bar model, 1 for equation, 1 for answer)
  - Q4 Inverse: /2 (1 for correct answer, 1 for explanation)
  - Q5 Comparison: /3 (1 for model, 1 for equation, 1 for answer)
  - Total: /14

#### Differentiation (Assessment Accommodations)
- **Group A (Below level)**: Extended time (3 minutes instead of 2 for computation probe). Concept check: counters provided and encouraged. For Q5, provide a sentence starter: "Team B built ___ walls because ___." Read questions aloud if needed.
- **Group B (On level)**: Standard administration.
- **Group C (Above level)**: Standard administration. If finished early with concept check, bonus question on back: "Write your own word problem that uses the fact family 6, 4, 24. Include a bar model."

#### Part 1: Reading Assessments — ORF + Phonics Checks (10:30–11:05)

**Structure**: Teacher pulls students individually for ORF probes (2–3 min each). Remaining students complete two written phonics assessments independently, then draft their narrative opening.

**Station A — Individual ORF Pull (Teacher, 1 student at a time)**:

**Objective**: Measure oral reading fluency (words correct per minute) on a grade-level passage. Compare to Week 1 baseline.

**Materials**:
- ORF passage — Grade 3 level (for students reading at/below grade level)
- ORF passage — Grade 4 level (for students reading at/above grade level)
- Timer (1 minute)
- Scoring sheet (mark errors: omissions, substitutions, insertions, self-corrections)

**Administration** (per student, 2–3 min):
- *"I'd like you to read this passage out loud. Do your best reading — not too fast, not too slow. If you get stuck on a word, I'll tell it to you after 3 seconds. Ready? Begin."*
- Start timer when student reads the first word.
- Mark errors on the teacher copy. At 1 minute: *"Stop."*
- Note the last word read. Calculate WCPM (words correct per minute = total words read minus errors).
- If time allows, ask 1–2 comprehension questions about the passage.
- Record: WCPM, accuracy %, self-corrections, error types (vowel team errors? r-controlled errors?).

**Target**: Pull all 8–10 students during the 35-minute block. Average 3 min per student = 10 students in 30 minutes.

**Station B — Independent Phonics Assessments (All students, at desks)**:

**Assessment 1: R-Controlled Vowel Sort (5 min)**:
- Printed worksheet with 10 words in a word bank: shark, fork, fern, bird, church, barn, storm, girl, purple, march
- Three columns labeled: ar, or, er/ir/ur
- Students read each word silently and write it in the correct column.
- Scoring: /10 (1 point per correctly sorted word)

**Assessment 2: Vowel Team Cumulative Check (8 min)**:
- Printed worksheet with 15 words — mix of Week 2 and Week 3 vowel team patterns:
  - Week 2 patterns: rain, beach, boat, play, stream (ai, ea, oa, ay, ea)
  - Week 3 patterns: coin, brown, enjoy, straw, shout, crawl, point, loud, pause, royal (oi, ow, oy, aw, ou, aw, oi, ou, au, oy)
- Task: Read each word. Write the vowel team you see in the word. Use the word in a short phrase or sentence.
  - Example: "coin" → vowel team: oi → "I found a coin."
- Scoring: /15 for correct vowel team identification; phrase/sentence quality noted but not scored.

**Assessment 3: Red Word Spelling Check (3 min)**:
- Teacher (or recorded audio) reads all 6 red words. Students write them from memory.
  - 1. thought  2. through  3. enough  4. laugh  5. bought  6. caught
- Scoring: /6 (must be spelled 100% correctly to earn the point)

**Student Workflow**:
1. Complete R-Controlled Vowel Sort (5 min)
2. Complete Vowel Team Cumulative Check (8 min)
3. Complete Red Word Spelling Check (when teacher announces — whole group, 3 min)
4. When finished with assessments → begin narrative drafting (Part 2)
5. Teacher pulls students one at a time for ORF — student leaves seat quietly when called, returns quietly when done.

#### Part 2: Narrative Writing — Draft Opening Paragraph (11:05–11:30)

**Objective**: Students will draft the opening paragraph of their narrative story, incorporating their chosen hook and at least one line of dialogue. This draft becomes a portfolio piece. (IEP Goal: Written Expression — write a narrative paragraph with a hook, setting, and dialogue.)

**Materials**:
- Composition notebooks
- Story planning organizers (character, setting, problem — from Tuesday)
- Hook selection (from Wednesday — starred in notebook)
- Dialogue practice (from Thursday — in notebook)
- "Narrative Opening Checklist" (printed, 1 per student):
  - [ ] I started with a hook (action, dialogue, or question)
  - [ ] I introduced my character
  - [ ] I described the setting (where and when)
  - [ ] I included at least one line of dialogue with quotation marks
  - [ ] I hinted at the problem
  - [ ] I used at least 5 sentences
- "Transition Words" reference card (first, then, next, after that, suddenly, meanwhile)

**Lesson**:

1. **Mini-Lesson: Putting It All Together (5 min)**:
   - *"All week, you've been building your story piece by piece — like building a house. You chose your story seed (the foundation). You planned your character, setting, and problem (the blueprint). You wrote a hook (the front door). You wrote dialogue (the voices inside). TODAY, you put it all together into your opening paragraph."*
   - Show the checklist. *"Your opening paragraph should have ALL of these things. It doesn't have to be perfect — this is a DRAFT. Drafts are messy. That's okay."*
   - **Quick model**: Teacher writes a 5-sentence opening paragraph on chart paper, thinking aloud:
     - Hook: *"'Don't move!' my mom whispered."* (dialogue hook)
     - Character: *"I froze in the middle of the backyard, holding a bucket of water."*
     - Setting: *"It was a hot Saturday afternoon in July, and the grass was crunchy under my bare feet."*
     - Hint at problem: *"That's when I saw it — the biggest bee I had ever seen, sitting on the edge of my bucket."*
     - Dialogue: *"'Just walk away slowly,' Mom said."*
   - *"See? Hook, character, setting, hint at the problem, dialogue. Five sentences. Your turn."*

2. **Drafting Time (17 min)**:
   - Students write in composition notebooks. Play soft background music.
   - **Teacher circulates and conferences** (when not pulling students for ORF):
     - Quick conferences (2 min each) — target 4–5 students:
       - *"Read me your hook. Does it grab me? Good — keep going."*
       - *"Where is your story set? Can you add one more detail about the setting?"*
       - *"I see you have dialogue — let's check the punctuation together."*
       - *"What's the problem going to be? Can you add a sentence that HINTS at it?"*
   - Students who finish early: re-read using the checklist, add details, or illustrate a scene from their opening.

3. **Share & Celebrate (3 min)**:
   - 2–3 volunteers read their opening paragraphs aloud.
   - Audience response: *"What hooked you? What do you want to know more about?"*
   - *"These drafts go into your portfolio folders. We'll continue developing these stories in Week 4 — adding the middle and end, and descriptive details."*

**Data Collection**:
- Collect narrative drafts (in notebooks or on separate paper if preferred for portfolio).
- Score using a simple rubric:
  | Element | 0 | 1 | 2 |
  |---------|---|---|---|
  | Hook present | No opening hook | Attempted hook | Clear, engaging hook |
  | Character introduced | No character | Named but not described | Named + at least 1 detail |
  | Setting described | No setting | Mentioned but vague | Specific (where + when or sensory detail) |
  | Dialogue included | No dialogue | Dialogue without proper punctuation | Dialogue with quotation marks + punctuation |
  | Problem hinted | No hint of problem | Vague hint | Clear hint that creates curiosity |
  | Length/effort | Fewer than 3 sentences | 3–4 sentences | 5+ sentences |
  | **Total** | **/12** |

**Differentiation**:
- **Below level**: Use the modified planning page as a direct guide. Provide the following frame:
  - "___" said ___. (dialogue hook — fill in)
  - My name is ___. I was ___. (character)
  - It was ___ at ___. (setting)
  - Then ___ happened. (hint at problem)
  - "___," I said. (more dialogue)
  - Student writes in the frame. If they finish, they can add more.
  - Teacher or aide conferences with each below-level writer at least once.
- **On level**: Write using the checklist. Aim for 5–7 sentences.
- **Above level**: Write 7+ sentences. Include at least 2 exchanges of dialogue (back-and-forth between characters). Add a sensory detail (what the character sees, hears, smells, or feels). Challenge: *"Can you end your opening paragraph with a CLIFFHANGER — something that makes the reader desperate to keep reading?"*

### Exploration & Enrichment (12:00–1:00)

**Activity**: Collaborative Mural — Building a Community

**Enrichment Category**: Art & Community (Friday theme)

**Overview**: The entire group collaborates to create a large mural depicting a community they would build together. Each student contributes a section — a building, a park, a person, a feature — and the pieces connect to form a unified community scene. This celebrates collaboration, creativity, and the "Building & Construction" theme while providing a visual reminder of working together.

**Materials**:
- Large mural paper (butcher paper, 6–8 feet long, taped to wall or laid on tables)
- Pencils (for sketching)
- Markers (thick and thin point)
- Tempera paint and brushes (if space/time allows; markers are fine as alternative)
- Colored pencils and crayons
- Collage materials: construction paper, fabric scraps, magazine cutouts
- Glue sticks and tape
- Scissors
- Title banner: "Our Community — Built Together" (pre-lettered by teacher or students)
- Section assignment cards (1 per student — see below)

**Schedule**:

1. **Introduction: "Building a Community Together" (10 min)**:
   - *"All week we've been building things — shelters, stories, bridges, budgets. Today we build something together: a COMMUNITY."*
   - *"A community is a place where people live, work, play, and help each other. If you could build the PERFECT community, what would be in it?"*
   - Brainstorm on chart paper (quick — 3 minutes). Possible responses: school, park, library, houses, hospital, fire station, playground, garden, store, restaurant, art studio, sports field, etc.
   - Show the mural paper. *"This is our community. It needs ALL of those things — and each of you is going to contribute one piece."*
   - **Assign sections** (prepared in advance based on group size):
     - Distribute section assignment cards. Each student gets 1–2 features to draw/create:
       - Student 1: A school
       - Student 2: Houses/apartments
       - Student 3: A park with trees and a playground
       - Student 4: A library
       - Student 5: A garden or farm
       - Student 6: A fire station or hospital
       - Student 7: Roads and sidewalks connecting everything
       - Student 8: People (diverse community members — at least 5 people)
       - Students 9–10: Sky, sun, clouds, birds (background) + community sign/banner
     - *"Your piece connects to everyone else's. That's what makes it a COMMUNITY — every part matters."*
   - **Planning (2 min)**: Quick sketch on scrap paper of what you'll draw. *"Plan first — you know the drill."*

2. **Create (30 min)**:
   - Students work on their assigned sections simultaneously.
   - Teacher circulates:
     - Encourage collaboration: *"Your road needs to connect to her park. Talk to each other about where things go."*
     - Prompt description: *"Tell me about the building you're drawing. What's it made of? Who works there?"*
     - **Literacy connection**: Each student writes a label or short caption for their section directly on the mural (e.g., "Community Library — Free books for everyone!" or "Fire Station #1 — Keeping our community safe").
     - **Vocabulary encouragement**: *"Can you use a word from this week? Maybe 'structure' or 'foundation' or 'design'?"*
   - At 15-minute mark: *"Check in with your neighbors — are your pieces connecting? Do the roads lead somewhere? Is there space for everyone?"*
   - At 25-minute mark: *"5 more minutes! Start finishing your details and writing your labels."*

3. **Gallery Viewing & Reflection (10 min)**:
   - Everyone steps back from the mural. Teacher leads a "gallery viewing":
     - *"Look at what we built TOGETHER. Every piece was made by one of you, but it only becomes a community when all the pieces connect."*
   - Each student gets 30 seconds to point to their contribution and say one sentence about it.
   - **Reflective connections**:
     - *"How is this mural like everything we've learned this week?"*
       - We built words from vowel patterns (oi, oy, ar, or…)
       - We built fact families from multiplication and division
       - We built stories from hooks, characters, settings, and dialogue
       - We built bridges and shelters and budgets
       - And now we built a community — together.
     - *"What if one person hadn't contributed their piece? Would the community be complete?"* (No — every part matters.)
   - Take a group photo with the mural for portfolios and parent communication.

4. **Closing Circle — Week 3 Wrap-Up (10 min)**:
   - **Week celebration**: *"Give yourself a round of applause. This was a BIG week. You learned new vowel patterns, you learned DIVISION, you started writing a STORY, and you built incredible things."*
   - **Self-monitoring checklist review**: Go through each item. Students rate themselves for the week (not just today).
   - **Portfolio update**: Narrative draft goes in the portfolio folder. Mural photo will be added next week.
   - **Parent folder**: Teacher distributes the weekly parent communication (or emails it). Include:
     - Progress monitoring data from today's probes
     - Red words to practice over the weekend (cards in folder)
     - Home practice suggestion: 10 min reading + 5 min fact families
     - Preview of Week 4
   - **Growth mindset close**: *"On Monday, some of you didn't know what division was. Today you solved division word problems with bar models. On Monday, you didn't know what oi or ar said. Today you read a whole passage full of those patterns. THAT is growth."*
   - *"Next week: multisyllabic words — breaking BIG words into pieces. And fractions — breaking numbers into pieces. The theme is 'Taking Things Apart.' Have a great weekend!"*
   - Dismissal at 1:00.

**Academic Connections**:
- **Reading/Writing**: Labeling, captioning, descriptive vocabulary, using Week 3 phonics words in context.
- **Math**: Spatial reasoning, planning sections, counting/dividing space.
- **Executive Function**: Planning (sketch before drawing), self-monitoring (check that your section connects to others), task completion within a time limit.
- **SEL**: Collaboration, contribution, celebrating collective achievement, recognizing that every person's work matters.

#### Step 2: Identify Grouping Adjustments

**Phonics Groups — Questions to Answer**:
- Are any Group A students (still working on Week 2 patterns) now showing ≥80% on vowel teams? → Move to Group B.
- Are any Group B students showing ≥90% on all patterns including r-controlled? → Move to Group C.
- Are any Group B or C students showing <70% on r-controlled? → May need additional support — don't move down, but add targeted r-controlled practice.
- er/ir/ur confusion: Is this widespread or isolated? If widespread → whole-group reteach in Week 4 warm-ups.

**Math Groups — Questions to Answer**:
- Are any Group A students (multiplication focus only) now demonstrating division understanding on the concept check (≥10/14)? → Move to Group B.
- Are any Group B students showing automaticity with facts (≥16/20 on computation probe) AND strong conceptual understanding (≥12/14)? → Move to Group C.
- Division concept Q4 (inverse relationship): Who scored 0? These students need explicit re-teaching of the multiplication-division connection before fractions begin.
- Computation speed: Who attempted <10 problems? May need processing speed accommodations or reduced problem sets.

**Comprehension & Writing**:
- Story elements: Did all students successfully identify character, setting, problem on the decodable passage comprehension checks this week?
- Narrative drafts: Who scored <6/12? These students may need modified expectations for Week 4 narrative continuation (focus on just middle OR end, not both).
- Dialogue: Who still cannot place quotation marks correctly? Small-group reteach in Week 4.

#### Step 3: Document Changes
- Record all grouping changes in the student data tracker.
- Communicate any significant changes to parents in the weekly report.
- Adjust Week 4 lesson materials to match new group compositions.
