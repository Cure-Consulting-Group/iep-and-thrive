### Math Block (9:20–10:20)

**Objective**: Students will identify and write complete fact families for multiplication and division, using bar models and grouping diagrams (representational) to show the relationship between the two operations. (IEP Goal: Math Computation — multiplication/division fact families; Math Problem Solving — word problems with bar models.)

**Materials**:
- Fact family triangle cards (number at each corner of the triangle — product on top, two factors at the bottom)
- Two-color counters (available for students who need to verify concretely)
- Bar model/tape diagram template (printed, 1 per student)
- Dry-erase boards + markers
- Chart paper: "Fact Families" anchor chart + "Bar Model for Division" anchor chart
- Word problem cards — Set C: mixed multiplication/division (8 cards)

#### Warm-Up: Multiplication Fact Fluency (5 min)
- **"Roll and Multiply"**: Each pair has two dice (one standard 1–6, one labeled x2, x5, x10, x1, x0, "choice"). Roll both. Multiply the number by the factor. Write equation on dry-erase board. Do 8 rounds.

#### Direct Instruction (15 min) — CRA Level: Concrete → Representational

1. **Connecting Multiplication and Division (5 min)**:
   - Place 12 counters on the document camera. Arrange into 3 rows of 4.
   - *"What multiplication fact does this show?"* (3 x 4 = 12)
   - *"What if I turn it? Now it's 4 rows of 3."* (4 x 3 = 12)
   - *"What if I take these same 12 counters and share them into 3 groups?"* (12 ÷ 3 = 4)
   - *"And into 4 groups?"* (12 ÷ 4 = 3)
   - *"Same three numbers — 3, 4, 12 — FOUR different facts. That's a fact family."*

2. **Fact Family Triangles (4 min)**:
   - Hold up a fact family triangle card with 12 at the top, 3 and 4 at the bottom corners.
   - *"The BIG number (the product/total) goes on top. The two factors go at the bottom. You can make 4 facts from these 3 numbers."*
   - Show 3 more triangles: (2, 5, 10), (5, 4, 20), (6, 3, 18). For each, students write all 4 facts on dry-erase boards.

3. **Transition to Representational: Bar Models (6 min)**:
   - *"Now let's draw our division instead of building it with counters."*
   - Draw a bar model on chart paper:
     - Long bar labeled "15" (the total)
     - Divided into 3 equal sections, each labeled "?"
     - *"15 divided into 3 groups. Each section is 5."*
   - Draw another:
     - Long bar labeled "20"
     - Divided into 5 equal sections
     - *"20 divided into 5 groups = 4 each."*
   - *"Bar models help us SEE division without needing counters. They're also great for word problems."*
   - Students practice: Draw a bar model for 18 ÷ 3 = 6 on their dry-erase boards. Teacher checks.

#### Guided Practice (15 min)

- **Activity Part 1: "Fact Family Builders" (8 min)**:
  - Each student gets 5 fact family triangle cards:
    - (2, 6, 12)
    - (5, 3, 15)
    - (4, 5, 20)
    - (10, 2, 20)
    - (5, 5, 25) — *"What's special about this one?"* (Square number — only 2 unique facts: 5 x 5 = 25, 25 ÷ 5 = 5)
  - For each triangle, students write all 4 facts (or 2 for the square number) on their dry-erase boards.
  - Teacher circulates. Key question: *"How does knowing 4 x 5 = 20 help you solve 20 ÷ 5?"*

- **Activity Part 2: "Bar Model Practice" (7 min)**:
  - Students draw bar models on the printed template for 3 problems:
    1. 12 ÷ 4 = ___ (draw bar, divide into 4 sections, find the value of each)
    2. 20 ÷ 5 = ___
    3. 15 ÷ 3 = ___
  - For each, also write the complete fact family below the bar model.
  - Circulate and conference. Ensure bars are proportional (sections roughly equal in size).

#### Independent Practice (15 min)

- **Word Problem Cards — Set C (Mixed Mult/Div)**: 5 problems. Students must:
  1. Read the problem
  2. Draw a bar model OR grouping diagram
  3. Write the equation
  4. Write the answer in a sentence
  5. Write a related fact from the same fact family

  - Card 1: "There are 4 shelves in the bookcase. Each shelf holds 5 books. How many books in all?" (4 x 5 = 20; related: 20 ÷ 4 = 5)
  - Card 2: "A builder has 18 bricks. He makes 3 equal stacks. How many bricks in each stack?" (18 ÷ 3 = 6; related: 6 x 3 = 18)
  - Card 3: "Each bridge has 2 towers. There are 5 bridges. How many towers in all?" (5 x 2 = 10; related: 10 ÷ 5 = 2)
  - Card 4: "An architect has 24 windows to place on a building. She puts 6 windows on each floor. How many floors?" (24 ÷ 6 = 4; related: 4 x 6 = 24)
  - Card 5 (challenge): "A construction team builds 3 walls each day. After some days, they have built 15 walls. How many days did they work? If each wall needs 4 beams, how many beams did they use in all?" (15 ÷ 3 = 5 days; 15 x 4 = 60 beams — two-step)

#### Closure (5 min)
- **Exit Ticket** (dry-erase boards): *"Write the complete fact family for 4, 3, 12."* Students hold up boards. Quick scan for accuracy.
- *"How does knowing multiplication help you with division?"* Collect 2–3 responses.
- Update anchor chart: **"Multiplication and division are INVERSE operations — they undo each other. Knowing one helps you solve the other."**
- Preview tomorrow: *"Tomorrow we tackle word problems with BOTH operations — and we start doing division without counters."*

#### Data Collection
- Fact family triangle cards: note accuracy (complete families? correct equations?)
- Bar model template: collect, check for correct structure and proportional sections
- Word problem cards Set C: score (/5), note bar model quality and whether related fact was correct
- Key indicator: Can students articulate HOW multiplication helps with division?

#### Differentiation
- **Group A (Below level)**: Use fact families with x2 and x5 only. Provide counters alongside bar model template — allow students to build with counters first, then draw the bar model to match. Limit word problems to Cards 1–3. For bar models, provide pre-drawn bars that students just partition and label.
- **Group B (On level)**: Full lesson as described. All 5 word problem cards.
- **Group C (Above level)**: Add triangles with larger numbers: (6, 4, 24), (7, 3, 21), (8, 5, 40). Challenge: *"Can you write a fact family for a number that has MORE than one fact family? What about 24?"* (2x12, 3x8, 4x6 — multiple families). Word problem Card 5 required.

#### Part 1: OG Lesson — Vowel Teams au/aw + R-Controlled er/ir/ur (10:30–11:05)

**Objective**: Students will read and spell words containing the vowel teams au (sauce) and aw (draw) and the r-controlled vowels er (her), ir (bird), ur (burn), recognizing that er/ir/ur all produce the same /er/ sound. (IEP Goal: Decoding — vowel teams and r-controlled vowels.)

**Materials**:
- OG phonogram cards: full deck + new cards (au, aw, er, ir, ur)
- Red word cards: bought, caught
- Sand/salt trays
- Dry-erase boards + markers
- Decodable word list for au/aw and er/ir/ur (printed)
- Highlighters (4 colors: au, aw, er/ir/ur as one group, and a mixed review color)
- Syllable type chart (updated to include all 5 types taught so far)

**OG Lesson Sequence**:

1. **Phonogram Review Drill (4 min)**:
   - Full deck through Tuesday's cards. Quick pace.
   - Pause on "switch hitter" ow: students say both sounds — /oh/ and /ow/.

2. **New Concept: au and aw (8 min)**:
   - Hold up **au** card: *"a-u says /aw/ — like in sauce, cause, fault. Key word: sauce."*
   - Hold up **aw** card: *"a-w says /aw/ — like in draw, paw, lawn. Key word: draw."*
   - *"Same sound! Just like oi/oy, these two make the same sound but appear in different positions."*
   - **Position pattern**:
     - au: usually MIDDLE — sauce, cause, fault, haunt, launch, caught, taught, pause, August
     - aw: usually at the END or before n/l — draw, paw, jaw, law, saw, straw, crawl, lawn, dawn, yawn, shawl
   - Write word lists on chart paper. Students read chorally.
   - **Multisensory**: Sky-write "au" saying /aw/. Sand tray: write "aw" three times saying /aw/.
   - Quick sort on dry-erase boards — teacher says a word, students write au or aw:
     - straw (aw), cause (au), crawl (aw), fault (au), dawn (aw), pause (au), jaw (aw), launch (au)

3. **New Concept: er/ir/ur — "The Triplets" (10 min)**:
   - *"Now for the BIG one. Three different spellings — ONE sound. Meet the triplets."*
   - Hold up **er** card: *"er says /er/ — like in her, fern, after. Key word: her."*
   - Hold up **ir** card: *"ir says /er/ — SAME sound — like in bird, first, girl. Key word: bird."*
   - Hold up **ur** card: *"ur says /er/ — SAME sound — like in burn, turn, hurt. Key word: burn."*
   - *"Her, bird, burn — they all have the same /er/ sound in the middle. The tricky part is knowing WHICH spelling to use. For READING, you just need to know the sound. For SPELLING, you often have to memorize which one a word uses."*
   - Write on chart paper in three columns:
     - **er**: her, fern, herd, clerk, perch, after, under, never, water, sister
     - **ir**: bird, first, girl, dirt, shirt, third, stir, twirl, birthday, circle
     - **ur**: burn, turn, hurt, church, nurse, purse, surf, fur, turtle, purple
   - **Memory tip**: *"er is the most common. When in doubt for spelling, try er first. But you have to LOOK at the word and remember which triplet it uses."*
   - **Multisensory**: For each spelling, students tap their arm:
     - er: tap wrist (closest, most common)
     - ir: tap inner elbow
     - ur: tap shoulder
     - Teacher says a word, students tap the correct spot AND say the spelling.
   - **Arm tapping for syllables**: Practice with 2-syllable words: sis-ter, tur-tle, birth-day, af-ter, pur-ple, cir-cle. Students tap left shoulder then right shoulder (or arm tapping spots) for each syllable.

4. **Word Reading Practice (7 min)**:
   - Decodable word list:
     - **au words**: haul, fault, pause, launch, August, because
     - **aw words**: draw, claw, straw, crawl, lawn, yawn
     - **er words**: her, fern, clerk, after, under, water
     - **ir words**: bird, first, girl, stir, shirt, third
     - **ur words**: burn, turn, nurse, church, purple, turtle
     - **Cumulative mixed list (all Week 3 patterns)**: coin, star, house, fork, boy, cloud, corn, joy, straw, fern, bird, turn, point, crawl, sport, girl
   - Partner reading: "whisper read" — read the list to your partner in a whisper voice.
   - Teacher pulls 3–4 students for individual accuracy check on the cumulative mixed list.

5. **Dictation (3 min)**:
   - Words: **draw**, **bird**, **nurse**, **fault**, **stir**, **lawn**
   - Sentence: **"The girl will draw a bird and a fern."**
   - Students write on dry-erase boards.

6. **Red Words: bought, caught (3 min)**:
   - **bought**: Write on red card. Underline "ough" — *"bought — past tense of buy. The 'ough' says /aw/ — just like in 'thought.' bought and thought RHYME."*
   - **caught**: Write on red card. Underline "augh" — *"caught — past tense of catch. The 'augh' says /aw/ — just like in 'laugh'... wait, no! Laugh says /af/ but caught says /aw/. TRICKY. Caught sounds like bought and thought."*
   - Say, spell, write, say — 3 repetitions each.
   - Cumulative red word review (all 6): thought, through, enough, laugh, bought, caught. Flash cards. Students write on boards.

**Data Collection**:
- Word reading: accuracy per pattern (au, aw, er, ir, ur) for individually checked students
- Cumulative mixed list: overall accuracy score — critical data point for Mid-Cycle Check
- Dictation score: /6 words + /1 sentence
- "Triplets" — which spelling do students default to? Note patterns
- Red word cumulative check: /6 for all red words this week

#### Part 2: Narrative Writing — Writing Strong Openings (11:05–11:30)

**Objective**: Students will learn and practice three types of story openings (action, dialogue, question) and draft an opening for their narrative. (IEP Goal: Written Expression — write narrative openings using hooks.)

**Materials**:
- "3 Types of Hooks" anchor chart (pre-made or built during lesson)
- Mentor text openings (3 short excerpts — one of each type)
- Story planning organizers from yesterday
- Composition notebooks
- Chart paper

**Lesson**:

1. **Mini-Lesson: Three Types of Hooks (12 min)**:
   - *"A hook is the very first sentence of your story. Its job is to GRAB the reader and make them want to keep reading. A boring opening = a bored reader."*
   - **Anchor chart — "3 Types of Hooks"**:

   - **Action Hook**: Start in the middle of something happening.
     - Mentor example: *"I ran as fast as my legs could carry me, but the gate was closing."*
     - *"See? We're already IN the story. Something is happening. We want to know — will they make it?"*

   - **Dialogue Hook**: Start with someone talking.
     - Mentor example: *"'Don't touch that!' my mom shouted from across the yard."*
     - *"Someone is speaking. We immediately wonder — touch WHAT? What's going to happen?"*

   - **Question Hook**: Start with a question that makes the reader curious.
     - Mentor example: *"Have you ever made a mistake so big you wished you could disappear?"*
     - *"The reader thinks: yes, I have! I want to know what happened to THIS person."*

   - Write a "boring" opening for comparison: *"One day I went to the park. It was sunny."*
   - *"Which ones make you want to read MORE? The boring one, or the hooks?"*

2. **Guided Practice: Write 3 Hooks (8 min)**:
   - *"Using your story plan from yesterday, try writing ALL THREE types of hooks for your story. Same story, three different openings."*
   - Students write in their composition notebooks:
     - My action hook: ___
     - My dialogue hook: ___
     - My question hook: ___
   - Circulate and support. Prompt stuck students: *"What's the first exciting thing that happens? Start THERE."* or *"What might your character SAY at the beginning?"*

3. **Share & Choose (5 min)**:
   - Partner share: read all 3 hooks to your partner. Partner says which one hooked them the most.
   - Students circle or star the hook they plan to use in their narrative draft.
   - *"Tomorrow, you'll learn how to write dialogue — the way characters talk in a story. If you chose the dialogue hook, you're ahead of the game!"*

**Data Collection**:
- Check notebooks: Did each student write 3 hooks? Rate quality (1 = attempted, 2 = clear hook type, 3 = engaging and well-crafted)
- Note which hook type was most popular and which students struggled with specific types
- Conference notes: who needed significant support?

**Differentiation**:
- **Below level**: Provide sentence starters for each hook type:
  - Action: "___ ran/jumped/grabbed/screamed when ___."
  - Dialogue: "'___!' said ___."
  - Question: "Have you ever ___?"
  - Students may write only 2 of 3 hook types if 3 is overwhelming.
- **On level**: Write all 3 hooks as described.
- **Above level**: Write all 3 hooks, then begin drafting 3–4 sentences AFTER the hook — continuing the story opening. Challenge: *"Can you include a FEELING in your opening? How does your character feel?"*

### Exploration & Enrichment (12:00–1:00)

**Activity**: Bridge-Building STEM Challenge (Load Testing)

**Enrichment Category**: STEM Engineering (Wednesday theme)

**Overview**: Student teams design and build a bridge from provided materials that must span a 12-inch gap between two stacks of books and hold as much weight as possible. This reinforces planning (EF), teamwork (SEL), measurement (math), and descriptive vocabulary (literacy).

**Materials**:
- Per team (teams of 2–3):
  - 30 craft sticks (popsicle sticks)
  - 1 piece of cardboard (6" x 12")
  - 12 inches of masking tape
  - 10 straws
  - 10 paper clips
  - 1 sheet of newspaper
  - 1 small piece of aluminum foil (12" x 12")
- Testing materials:
  - 2 stacks of books (same height, placed 12 inches apart) per testing station
  - Small weights, coins, or small blocks (for load testing)
  - Kitchen scale (to weigh the load if available)
  - Ruler (to measure the 12-inch gap)
- Planning sheets: "Bridge Engineering Plan" (goal, sketch, materials list, steps, prediction: "I think our bridge will hold ___ weights")
- Chart paper: "Strong Shapes in Building" (triangle, arch)

**Schedule**:

1. **The Challenge (8 min, whole group)**:
   - Show the setup: two book stacks, 12 inches apart.
   - *"Your challenge: Build a bridge that goes from HERE to HERE using ONLY the materials at your table. The bridge must hold weight — we'll test by placing small weights on top. The bridge that holds the MOST weight wins."*
   - Quick teaching moment — **strong shapes**:
     - Show a flat piece of paper between the books — it sags and falls.
     - Fold the same paper into an accordion (zigzag) — it holds a book.
     - *"WHY? Folding creates TRIANGLES — the strongest shape in engineering. Arches are strong too."*
     - Show pictures of real bridges: beam bridge, arch bridge, truss bridge (triangles).
   - *"You have 3 minutes to PLAN before you build."*

2. **Planning Phase (5 min)**:
   - Teams complete the "Bridge Engineering Plan" sheet:
     - Sketch your bridge design
     - List which materials you'll use for the base, supports, and surface
     - Write 3 steps
     - Predict: "We think our bridge will hold ___ weights."
   - Remind: *"Use your Plan It steps from this morning!"*

3. **Build Phase (22 min)**:
   - Timer visible. Teams build.
   - Teacher circulates with guiding questions (do NOT solve for them):
     - *"Is your bridge touching the table between the books, or does it span the gap?"*
     - *"What shape are you using for strength?"*
     - *"It collapsed — what can you change? What did you learn?"*
     - *"How are you dividing the work on your team?"*
   - At 10-minute mark: *"Halfway! Check — does your bridge span the gap? Can it hold a pencil? If not, you may need to redesign."*
   - At 18-minute mark: *"4 minutes left! Start finishing and testing on your own."*

4. **Load Testing (10 min)**:
   - Each team brings their bridge to the testing station (or teacher moves between tables).
   - Place bridge across the gap. Begin adding weights one at a time.
   - Count aloud together: *"1... 2... 3..."* Continue until the bridge collapses or bends more than 1 inch.
   - Record each team's result on chart paper: Team name, number of weights held.
   - Celebrate ALL bridges — *"Every team built something that held at least some weight. That's engineering."*
   - **Math connection**: *"If Team A held 12 weights and Team B held 4, how many TIMES more did Team A hold?"* (3 times — multiplicative comparison). *"If we shared all Team A's weights equally into 3 bags, how many per bag?"* (4 — division).

5. **Debrief & Closing Circle (15 min)**:
   - *"What made the strongest bridges strong?"* Collect answers — triangles, thickness, multiple layers, arches.
   - *"What would you do differently?"*
   - *"How did PLANNING help? Did any team change their plan during building? That's okay — engineers iterate."*
   - **Writing connection**: *"Bridges are the MIDDLE of a story — they connect the beginning to the end. Your story opening (hook) connects to the problem. Tomorrow, you'll learn to write DIALOGUE — what your characters SAY — to bridge your story forward."*
   - **Vocabulary from today**: bridge, span, support, load, collapse, structure, design, engineer, test, iterate.
   - Take photos of bridges for portfolios. Clean up materials.
   - Self-monitoring checklist. Dismissal at 1:00.

**Academic Connections**:
- **Math**: Measurement (12-inch gap, counting weights), multiplicative comparison, division in debrief.
- **Reading/Writing**: Descriptive vocabulary (engineering terms), planning/writing connection, metaphor of bridges connecting story elements.
- **Executive Function**: Full planning cycle — plan, build, test, reflect. Iterating when something fails.
- **SEL**: Collaboration, handling frustration when designs fail, celebrating others' success.
