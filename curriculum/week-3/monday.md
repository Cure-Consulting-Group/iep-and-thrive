### Math Block (9:20–10:20)

**Objective**: Students will model division as equal sharing (partitive division) using concrete manipulatives, understanding that division means distributing a total equally among a given number of groups. (IEP Goal: Math Computation — solve division problems within 100 using models.)

**Materials**:
- Two-color counters (20+ per student)
- Division mats (printed — large "Total" circle at top, 2–5 partition sections below)
- Snap cubes (for building/verifying)
- Dry-erase boards + markers (1 per student)
- Chart paper + marker (for anchor chart: "Division = Fair Sharing")
- Word problem cards — Set A: sharing problems (8 cards, laminated)

#### Warm-Up: Multiplication Fact Fluency (5 min)
- **Flash card drill**: x0, x1, x2, x5, x10 facts. Teacher holds up a flash card; students write the answer on dry-erase boards and hold up on the count of three.
- Keep pace brisk — roughly 12–15 facts in 5 minutes.
- Note which facts students hesitate on (record on roster).

#### Direct Instruction (15 min) — CRA Level: Concrete

1. **Set the Context (3 min)**:
   - Tell the story: *"I have 12 building blocks and 3 friends. I want to share them FAIRLY — each friend gets the same amount. How many does each friend get?"*
   - Ask students to predict. Accept all guesses.
   - *"When we share a total equally into groups, that's called DIVISION. Division is fair sharing."*

2. **Model with Manipulatives (6 min)**:
   - Place 12 counters in the "Total" circle on the division mat (use the document camera or model at a student table).
   - Draw 3 circles below (the groups/friends).
   - Distribute one counter at a time into each group, round-robin: *"One for group 1, one for group 2, one for group 3. One for group 1, one for group 2, one for group 3…"* Continue until all 12 are distributed.
   - Count how many in each group: 4.
   - Record on the board: **12 ÷ 3 = 4** — *"12 shared among 3 equals 4 each."*
   - Vocabulary anchor chart entry: **dividend** (the total), **divisor** (number of groups), **quotient** (how many in each group).

3. **Guided Try (6 min)** — Students do the next one:
   - *"You have 15 counters. Share them equally among 5 groups on your division mat."*
   - Students physically distribute counters one at a time. Walk the room.
   - When finished: *"How many in each group?"* (3) *"So 15 divided by 5 equals…?"* (3)
   - Students write on dry-erase boards: **15 ÷ 5 = 3**
   - Repeat with: **20 ÷ 4 = 5** (20 counters, 4 groups)

#### Guided Practice (15 min)

- **"Fair Shares" Partner Activity**: Students work in pairs with counters and division mats.
- Teacher provides 6 problems on the board, one at a time. Students:
  1. Count out the total (counters)
  2. Create the correct number of groups on the mat
  3. Distribute fairly, one at a time
  4. Write the division equation on dry-erase boards

- Problems (progressive difficulty):
  1. 8 ÷ 2 = ___ (easy entry — even split)
  2. 10 ÷ 5 = ___
  3. 18 ÷ 3 = ___
  4. 16 ÷ 4 = ___
  5. 20 ÷ 2 = ___ (larger quotient)
  6. 24 ÷ 6 = ___ (stretch)

- After each problem, one pair shares their mat under the document camera and explains: *"We had ___ total. We shared into ___ groups. Each group got ___."*

- **Key question to ask during circulation**: *"What does the answer (quotient) tell us?"* (How many in EACH group.)

#### Independent Practice (15 min)

- **Word Problem Cards — Set A**: Students work individually on 4–5 sharing word problems.
  - Card 1: "Maya has 12 stickers. She shares them equally among 4 friends. How many stickers does each friend get?" (12 ÷ 4 = 3)
  - Card 2: "A builder has 15 nails. He puts an equal number into 3 toolboxes. How many nails in each toolbox?" (15 ÷ 3 = 5)
  - Card 3: "There are 20 students going on a field trip. They are split into 5 equal teams. How many students are on each team?" (20 ÷ 5 = 4)
  - Card 4: "A baker made 18 cookies. She puts the same number on 6 plates. How many cookies per plate?" (18 ÷ 6 = 3)
  - Card 5 (challenge): "A construction crew has 24 bricks. They divide them equally among workers. Each worker gets 8 bricks. How many workers are there?" (24 ÷ 8 = 3 — missing divisor)

- Students must:
  1. Draw a picture or diagram showing the sharing
  2. Write the division equation
  3. Write a sentence answering the question

- Circulate. Conference with 2–3 students about their diagrams.

#### Closure (5 min)
- **Turn and Talk**: *"In your own words, what does division mean?"* Collect 3 responses.
- Add to the anchor chart: **"Division is fair sharing. We take a total and split it into equal groups."**
- Preview tomorrow: *"Tomorrow we'll learn ANOTHER way to think about division — grouping. Same operation, different way to picture it."*

#### Data Collection
- Record which students successfully modeled 4+ of 6 guided practice problems
- Word problem cards: collect and score (/5, note whether drawing matches equation)
- Observational notes: Who distributed one-at-a-time (procedural) vs. who "just knew" to put 4 in each group (more automatic)?
- Flag students who needed support partitioning into 3+ groups

#### Differentiation
- **Group A (Below level)**: Limit to dividing by 2 and 5 only. Use only 10–12 counters maximum. Provide pre-drawn group circles on the mat. Skip Card 5 (missing divisor).
- **Group B (On level)**: Full lesson as described. All 5 word problem cards.
- **Group C (Above level)**: After guided practice, give additional challenge: "Can you figure out 24 ÷ 3 WITHOUT using counters? What multiplication fact helps you?" Begin connecting division to known multiplication facts.

#### Part 1: OG Lesson — Vowel Teams oi/oy + R-Controlled ar (10:30–11:10)

**Objective**: Students will read and spell words containing the vowel teams oi (coin) and oy (boy) and the r-controlled vowel ar (car), producing the correct sound for each pattern with ≥80% accuracy during guided practice. (IEP Goal: Decoding — vowel teams and r-controlled vowels.)

**Materials**:
- OG phonogram cards: full deck including new cards for oi, oy, ar
- Red word cards: thought, through (introduce 2 today)
- Sand/salt trays (1 per student)
- Dry-erase boards + markers
- Decodable word list (printed, on student desks)
- Highlighters (3 colors: one for oi, one for oy, one for ar)
- Chart paper: "Vowel Teams — Part 2" anchor chart (started today, added to all week)

**OG Lesson Sequence**:

1. **Phonogram Review Drill (5 min)**:
   - Rapid-fire review of all known phonogram cards: consonants, short vowels, digraphs (sh, ch, th, wh, ck), long vowels (a_e, i_e, o_e, u_e), and Week 2 vowel teams (ai/ay, ee/ea, oa/ow-/oh/).
   - Students respond chorally with the sound(s).
   - Pace: approximately 2 seconds per card. Mark hesitations on roster.

2. **New Concept: oi and oy (10 min)**:
   - Hold up the **oi** card. *"This says /oy/ — like in coin, oil, point. When you see o-i together in a word, they make the /oy/ sound. Key word: coin."*
   - Hold up the **oy** card. *"This also says /oy/ — like in boy, toy, joy. Same sound! Key word: boy."*
   - **Position rule**: *"Here's the building rule — oi is usually found in the MIDDLE of a word or syllable. oy is found at the END. Think of it like building a wall: oi goes INSIDE, oy goes on the OUTSIDE."*
   - Write on chart paper:
     - **oi** (middle): coin, join, point, boil, soil, moist, noise
     - **oy** (end): boy, toy, joy, enjoy, destroy, annoy, employ
   - **Multisensory**: Students sky-write "oi" with two fingers while saying /oy/, then sky-write "oy" while saying /oy/. Repeat 3 times each.
   - **Sand tray**: Students write "oi" in sand, then "oy" in sand, saying the sound each time.

3. **New Concept: R-Controlled ar (8 min)**:
   - Hold up the **ar** card. *"When the letter r comes after a vowel, it CHANGES the vowel's sound. The r is bossy — it controls the vowel. ar says /ar/ — like in car, star, park."*
   - *"Is the 'a' in 'car' a short a like in 'cat'? No! The r changed it. That's why we call these r-controlled vowels."*
   - Write on chart paper:
     - **ar**: car, star, park, barn, farm, card, shark, march, garden
   - **Arm tapping preview**: *"We're also going to learn a new trick for breaking apart big words this week. Watch."* Demonstrate arm tapping with "garden": tap left shoulder for "gar-", tap elbow for "-den". *"Two syllables, two taps."*
   - Students practice arm tapping: garden, market, carpet, party (all ar words with 2 syllables).

4. **Word Reading Practice (7 min)**:
   - Decodable word list on desks. Students read each word independently, then check with a partner:
     - **oi words**: oil, coil, join, moist, point, broil, choice
     - **oy words**: boy, joy, ploy, enjoy, royal, loyal, voyage
     - **ar words**: art, cart, dark, mark, sharp, charm, start
     - **Mixed review**: coin, star, toy, farm, boil, yard, enjoy
   - Students highlight: oi words in green, oy words in orange, ar words in blue.
   - Teacher circulates and listens to 3–4 students read the list aloud individually (running record style — note errors).

5. **Dictation (5 min)**:
   - *"I say the word, you repeat it, tap it, then write it on your dry-erase board."*
   - Dictation words:
     1. **join** (oi in the middle)
     2. **joy** (oy at the end)
     3. **park** (ar)
     4. **boil** (oi)
     5. **barn** (ar)
     6. **toy** (oy)
   - Sentence dictation: **"The boy will join us at the park."**
   - Students hold up boards after each word. Teacher gives immediate corrective feedback.

6. **Red Words: thought, through (5 min)**:
   - *"These words don't follow our regular patterns — they're tricky. We call them red words because they have a part that breaks the rules."*
   - **thought**: Write on a red card. Underline "ough" — *"This part says /aw/. The t-h says /th/ and the t at the end is silent in how we naturally say it. Thought — past tense of 'think.'"* Say it, spell it, write it, say it. Repeat 3 times.
   - **through**: Write on a red card. Underline "ough" — *"Same letters, different sound! Here 'ough' says /oo/. Through — like going through a door."* Say it, spell it, write it, say it. Repeat 3 times.
   - *"Both words have 'ough' but they sound different. That's what makes them red words — you have to memorize them."*
   - Practice: Students close eyes, visualize the word, then write it from memory on dry-erase boards. Check. Repeat.

**Data Collection**:
- Phonogram drill: mark hesitations/errors on class roster
- Word reading: note accuracy per pattern (oi, oy, ar) for each student heard
- Dictation: score /6 words + /1 sentence per student
- Red words: note who spelled "thought" and "through" correctly from memory on first try

#### Part 2: Narrative Writing — Brainstorming Story Ideas (11:10–11:30)

**Objective**: Students will generate at least 3 narrative story ideas using a "story seed" list and select one to develop into a full narrative over the week. (IEP Goal: Written Expression — plan a narrative with character, setting, and problem.)

**Materials**:
- "Story Seed" brainstorm handout (printed, 1 per student)
- Composition notebooks (My Summer Learning Journal)
- "Plan It" anchor chart (from morning warm-up)
- Chart paper: "Great Stories Start With Small Moments" (anchor chart)

**Lesson**:

1. **Mini-Lesson: Small Moments (8 min)**:
   - *"This week, we're going to write our own stories. Not BIG stories about saving the world — but small, real moments that matter."*
   - Share an example: *"I'm not going to write about my whole vacation. I'm going to write about the moment I lost my shoe in the ocean and had to hop on one foot across hot sand. THAT'S a story."*
   - **Anchor chart — "Great Stories Start With Small Moments"**:
     - A time you were scared
     - A time you laughed really hard
     - A time something went wrong (and maybe got fixed)
     - A time you tried something new
     - A time you helped someone (or someone helped you)
     - A time you built or made something
   - *"Notice — each one of these is about ONE moment, not a whole day or a whole trip."*

2. **Story Seed Brainstorm (8 min)**:
   - Distribute the "Story Seed" handout. It has 6 prompt categories with space to jot ideas:
     - A time I was surprised: ___
     - A time something broke or went wrong: ___
     - A time I was brave: ___
     - A time I built, made, or created something: ___
     - A time I was with someone I care about: ___
     - A time I learned something the hard way: ___
   - *"You have 6 minutes. Write at LEAST one idea for each box. They can be just a few words — 'the time the dog got out' is enough."*
   - Play soft background music. Circulate and prompt students who are stuck: *"Tell me about a time something funny happened at home. What about a time you were really proud of yourself?"*

3. **Select Your Seed (4 min)**:
   - *"Look at your ideas. Put a star next to the ONE story you want to tell this week. Pick the one that makes you feel the most — happy, excited, nervous, proud."*
   - **Turn and Talk**: Tell your partner which seed you chose and why. Partner listens and says: *"I want to hear that story because ___."*
   - *"Tomorrow, we'll start PLANNING your story — choosing your character, setting, and problem. Today you just needed the idea. That's the foundation — like the foundation of a building."*

**Data Collection**:
- Collect brainstorm handouts — check that each student generated at least 3 ideas
- Note students who struggled to generate ideas (may need more modeling tomorrow)
- Record which students chose which types of seeds (informs conferring later in the week)

**Differentiation**:
- **Below level**: Teacher or aide sits with this group during brainstorming. Verbally prompt: *"Tell me about a time you…"* Scribe their ideas if writing is a barrier. Allow drawing a quick sketch instead of words.
- **On level**: Complete the brainstorm independently. Star one seed.
- **Above level**: After selecting a seed, begin jotting 3–4 sentences about what happened (early drafting start). Challenge: *"Can you think of a way to start your story that grabs the reader's attention?"*

### Exploration & Enrichment (12:00–1:00)

**Activity**: Build a Nature Shelter (Outdoor Engineering)

**Enrichment Category**: Nature & STEM (Monday theme)

**Overview**: Students work in teams of 2–3 to design and build a small shelter (large enough for a stuffed animal or action figure) using only natural materials found outdoors plus string/twine. This connects to the "Building & Construction" theme and reinforces the planning skill introduced in the morning warm-up.

**Materials**:
- Nature materials: sticks, leaves, grass, bark (found on-site)
- String or twine (one 3-foot piece per team)
- A small stuffed animal or toy figure per team (the "inhabitant" the shelter must protect)
- Clipboards + planning sheets (printed: "Our Shelter Plan" with spaces for goal, materials, sketch, steps)
- Cup of water (for the "rain test" at the end)
- Camera/phone for photos (portfolio documentation)

**Schedule**:

1. **Introduction & Planning (10 min, inside)**:
   - *"Engineers and builders always start with a PLAN. Before we go outside, we need to figure out what we're building and how."*
   - Show the challenge: *"Your team must build a shelter for this little figure. The shelter must: (1) stand up on its own, (2) have a roof, and (3) survive a 'rain test' — I'll pour a small cup of water on it."*
   - Teams of 2–3 (assigned intentionally — mix strengths, consider social dynamics).
   - Each team fills out the **"Our Shelter Plan"** sheet:
     - What is our goal? (A shelter that stands, has a roof, and keeps our figure dry)
     - What materials do we need? (List at least 5)
     - Draw a sketch of your plan
     - What are our steps? (Step 1, Step 2, Step 3)
   - Connect to morning EF lesson: *"See? We're using our Plan It steps."*

2. **Material Collection (10 min, outside)**:
   - Teams collect natural materials based on their plan.
   - Rules: stay within boundaries, only use materials on the ground (no breaking live branches), share if another team needs something.
   - Teacher circulates: *"What's your plan for the walls? How will you make the roof stay up?"*

3. **Build Time (20 min, outside)**:
   - Teams construct their shelters.
   - Teacher roles during build time:
     - Ask probing questions: *"What's working? What's not? What would you change?"*
     - Facilitate collaboration: *"I see you disagree about where the door goes. How can you solve that?"*
     - Encourage persistence: *"It fell down? That's what happens to real builders too. What can you try differently?"*
   - SEL integration: If conflicts arise, use the prompt: *"Tell your partner what you need using an 'I' statement."*

4. **Rain Test & Gallery Walk (10 min)**:
   - Each team places their figure inside the shelter.
   - Teacher (or a student volunteer) slowly pours a small cup of water over each shelter.
   - Class observes: Did the figure stay dry? Did the structure hold?
   - Quick gallery walk: teams walk around and observe others' designs.
   - Debrief questions:
     - *"Which designs worked best? Why?"*
     - *"What would you do differently next time?"*
     - *"How did having a PLAN help you?"*

5. **Closing Circle (10 min, inside)**:
   - Students return to seats. Each team shares one thing they learned about building.
   - **Math connection**: *"How many sticks did your team use? If you had to share your sticks equally among 3 friends, how many would each get?"* (Quick mental division — connects to today's math.)
   - **Writing connection**: *"Your story this week could be set at a construction site, or about building something. Keep that in mind."*
   - Self-monitoring checklist review: *"Check off your planning box — did you make a plan today before you started building?"*
   - Photo of each team's shelter for the portfolio.
   - Clean up outdoor space. Return materials.
   - Dismissal at 1:00.

**Academic Connections**:
- **Math**: Planning requires estimation (how many materials?). Debrief includes informal division. Measurement concepts (how tall, how wide).
- **Reading/Writing**: Planning sheet requires writing. Descriptive vocabulary for materials and structures. Story seeds can come from the building experience.
- **Executive Function**: Full application of the "Plan It" strategy in a real-world context.
