### Morning Warm-Up (9:00–9:20)

**Community Check-In: "Rose, Thorn, Bud" (5 min)**:
- Introduce the routine: *"A rose is something good that happened. A thorn is something that was hard. A bud is something you're looking forward to."*
- Teacher models: *"My rose is that I've loved getting to know all of you. My thorn is that I stayed up late grading your assessments. My bud is that tomorrow is Friday and we get to do art!"*
- Go around the circle. Each student shares one (rose, thorn, or bud — their choice). Quick pace.

**Feelings Check-In (2 min)**:
- Finger scale 1–5 (same as yesterday). Teacher scans.

**Brain Game: "Categories" (5 min)**:
- Teacher names a category. Students go around the circle, each naming one thing in that category. If you repeat someone else's answer or can't think of one, you sit down (gently — no shame, just for fun).
- Categories to use:
  - Animals that live in water
  - Foods you eat for breakfast
  - Things that are smaller than your hand
  - Words that start with the /sh/ sound (connects to yesterday's digraph lesson)
- SEL note: if a student seems stressed about being "out," modify: everyone stays standing, just skip and come back.

**Movement Activity: "Cross-Body Exercises" (5 min)**:
- These cross-lateral movements activate both brain hemispheres — excellent before a reading-heavy day.
  - Touch right hand to left knee (10x)
  - Touch left hand to right knee (10x)
  - Right hand to left shoulder, then left hand to right shoulder (10x)
  - "Lazy 8s" — draw a figure 8 on its side in the air with one hand, then the other, then both
  - "Brain buttons" — gently massage the area just below the collarbone while looking left and right

**Self-Monitoring Review (3 min)**:
- *"Check your checklist from yesterday. How did you do? Was there one item that was harder for you? Today, focus on that one thing."*
- Students identify their focus area and write it on a sticky note: *"Today I will work on ___."*

### Math Block (9:20–10:20)

**Objective**: Students will solve 1-step addition and subtraction word problems by identifying the operation, representing the problem with a model (drawing or manipulatives), and computing the answer with ≥70% accuracy. Students will use estimation to check the reasonableness of their answers. (IEP Goal: Math Problem Solving — word problems with addition/subtraction.)

**Materials**:
- Dry-erase boards + markers
- Base-ten blocks (at tables)
- Chart paper for anchor chart: "Word Problem Attack Plan"
- Word problem cards (12 problems, printed on card stock, 1 set per pair)
- Word problem recording sheet (1 per student)
- Number lines (desktop)
- Pencils

#### Warm-Up/Activation (5 min)
- **Estimation Jar**: Hold up a clear jar filled with small objects (marbles, cubes, beans — approximately 150–250 items).
- *"How many do you think are in this jar? Write your estimate on your dry-erase board."*
- Collect estimates. Write the highest and lowest on the board.
- *"Estimation is a math superpower. Before you solve a problem, you should always estimate — ask yourself, 'About how much should my answer be?' If your answer is way off from your estimate, that's a signal to check your work."*
- Reveal the actual count (or count together at end of class as a reward).

#### Direct Instruction (15 min) — CRA Level: Representational → Abstract

1. **Word Problem Attack Plan Anchor Chart (7 min)**:
   - Create together on chart paper — the "CUBES" strategy:
     - **C** — Circle the numbers
     - **U** — Underline the question
     - **B** — Box the key words (in all, how many more, how many left, altogether, difference, total)
     - **E** — Evaluate: What operation do I use? Draw a picture or model.
     - **S** — Solve and check: Does my answer make sense?
   - Post this permanently.

2. **Model Problem #1 — Addition (4 min)**:
   - Read aloud: *"The school library has 347 fiction books and 289 nonfiction books. How many books does the library have in all?"*
   - Walk through CUBES:
     - Circle: 347, 289
     - Underline: "How many books does the library have in all?"
     - Box: "in all" → this tells us to ADD
     - Evaluate: Draw a bar model (two parts making a whole). 347 + 289.
     - Estimate first: 347 is close to 350, 289 is close to 300. 350 + 300 = 650. Answer should be near 650.
     - Solve: 347 + 289 = 636. Is 636 close to 650? Yes — reasonable.

3. **Model Problem #2 — Subtraction (4 min)**:
   - Read aloud: *"Marcus had 503 stickers. He gave 278 to his sister. How many stickers does Marcus have left?"*
   - Walk through CUBES together — this time students help at each step.
   - Key words: "how many left" → SUBTRACT
   - Bar model: whole is 503, one part is 278, find the other part.
   - Estimate: 500 - 280 = 220. Answer should be near 220.
   - Solve: 503 - 278 = 225. Reasonable? Yes.

#### Guided Practice (20 min)

**"Word Problem Sort and Solve" — Partner Activity**

- Pairs receive a set of 12 word problem cards. Task has two phases:

**Phase 1 — Sort (5 min)**:
- Sort the 12 cards into two piles: addition problems and subtraction problems.
- Do NOT solve yet — just decide the operation.
- Teacher circulates and checks sorts before moving to Phase 2.
- Discussion: *"How did you decide? What words helped you?"*

**Phase 2 — Solve (15 min)**:
- Each pair chooses 4 problems (2 addition, 2 subtraction) and solves them on the recording sheet.
- For each problem, the recording sheet requires:
  1. Copy the problem
  2. Circle numbers, underline question, box key words
  3. Draw a model (bar model, picture, or number line)
  4. Estimate
  5. Solve
  6. Check: Is my answer reasonable?

- Sample word problems in the set:
  - "A farm has 156 chickens and 238 cows. How many animals are on the farm altogether?" (addition)
  - "Maya read 412 pages this month. Last month she read 287 pages. How many more pages did she read this month?" (subtraction — comparison)
  - "The store had 600 apples. They sold 345 on Saturday. How many apples are left?" (subtraction with zero)
  - "School A has 1,247 students. School B has 983 students. How many students are there in both schools?" (addition with regrouping)
  - "A swimmer swam 750 meters on Monday and 680 meters on Tuesday. How many meters did she swim in total?" (addition)
  - "A builder had 1,000 bricks. He used 463 for a wall. How many bricks are left?" (subtraction from 1,000)

- Teacher circulates, asking:
  - *"What operation are you using? How do you know?"*
  - *"What's your estimate? Does your answer seem reasonable?"*
  - *"Can you draw a picture to show what's happening in this problem?"*

#### Independent Practice (5 min)
- Each student independently solves 1 final problem on their dry-erase board (teacher selects based on level):
  - Below level: "Ben has 45 red crayons and 38 blue crayons. How many crayons does Ben have?"
  - On level: "The school library has 823 books. Students borrowed 367 this week. How many books are still in the library?"
  - Above level: "Maria had $500. She spent $127 on shoes and $89 on a backpack. How much money does she have left?" (2-step)

#### Closure (5 min)
- **Share Out**: 2 pairs share one of their solved problems, explaining their model and solution.
- *"What key words helped you decide the operation today?"* Build a quick list on the board.
- Return to the estimation jar: count together (or reveal the count). Who was closest?
- *"Tomorrow we'll review everything we've learned in math this week."*

#### Data Collection
- Collect recording sheets — score: operation identification accuracy, model use, computation accuracy
- Note which students could sort problems by operation but struggled with computation (vs. students who struggled with both)
- Note bar model / drawing use — who drew a model vs. who jumped straight to the algorithm?
- Record which students needed teacher prompting to identify the operation

#### Differentiation
- **Below level**: Provide a "key word" reference card (addition words on one side, subtraction words on the other). Use 2-digit numbers in problems. Allow base-ten blocks for computation. Solve 2 problems instead of 4.
- **On level**: 3-digit problems as described. Must draw a model for each.
- **Above level**: Include comparison subtraction ("how many more") and 2-step problems. Challenge: *"Write your own word problem for a classmate. It must require subtraction with regrouping."*

#### Part 1: OG Lesson — Closed Syllable Review + Red Words (10:30–10:45)

**Objective**: Students will identify closed syllables (a syllable that ends in a consonant, with a short vowel sound) and read closed-syllable words with blends and digraphs with ≥75% accuracy. Students will read and spell 8 red words (the, was, is, are, have, come, some, one). (IEP Goal: Decoding — closed syllable identification; Sight word recognition.)

**Materials**:
- OG phonogram cards (full deck so far: consonants, short vowels, blends, digraphs)
- Red word cards: the, was, is, are, have (review) + come, some, one (new)
- Dry-erase boards + markers
- Syllable type anchor chart (begin creating)
- Word cards: 12 closed-syllable words of increasing complexity

**OG Lesson Structure**:

1. **Phonogram Drill — Speed Round (3 min)**:
   - Flash the full deck. Students respond chorally. Goal: automatic response for every card.
   - Teacher notes any remaining hesitations. By tomorrow, this deck should be near-automatic.

2. **Red Word Review + New Words (5 min)**:
   - Flash the 5 known red words: the, was, is, are, have. Students read chorally.
   - Introduce 3 new red words: **come, some, one**
   - For each new word:
     - Show the card. Read it.
     - *"What's tricky about this word?"*
       - **come** — the "o" says /ŭ/, the "e" is silent
       - **some** — same pattern: "o" says /ŭ/, silent "e"
       - **one** — completely irregular: /wŭn/
     - Students spell aloud, write in sand tray, heart-tap (hand on heart, spell from memory).
   - Quick assessment: flash all 8 red words randomly. Students respond individually (go around the room). Mark accuracy.

3. **New Concept — Closed Syllable Rule (7 min)**:
   - Begin the **Syllable Type Anchor Chart**:
     - *"A closed syllable ends with a consonant. The vowel is 'closed in' by the consonant, so it says its SHORT sound."*
     - Examples: **cat** (c-a-t, ends in t, vowel a says /ă/) → closed syllable
     - **ship** (sh-i-p, ends in p, vowel i says /ĭ/) → closed syllable
     - **blast** (bl-a-st, ends in st, vowel a says /ă/) → closed syllable
   - Draw the anchor chart symbol: a closed syllable box with the consonant "closing the door" on the vowel.
   - Non-example: *"Is 'he' a closed syllable?"* No — it ends in a vowel. That's an OPEN syllable (preview for later).
   - Students read 12 words and identify if each is a closed syllable:
     - **Closed**: mat, shelf, chunk, spent, drift, flask
     - **Not closed**: me, go, ride, take (these end in vowels or have long vowel sounds — just label "not closed" for now)

**Data Collection**:
- Red word accuracy: record per student (8 words — which ones are mastered, which need more practice?)
- Closed syllable identification: how many of the 12 did students correctly identify?

#### Part 2: Sentence Structure — Subject and Predicate (10:45–11:00)

**Objective**: Students will identify the subject and predicate of simple sentences and write 3 complete sentences with correct capitalization and end punctuation. (IEP Goal: Written Expression — sentence structure.)

**Materials**:
- Chart paper: "Parts of a Sentence" anchor chart
- Sentence strips (pre-written, 6 sentences cut into subject and predicate halves)
- Dry-erase boards + markers
- Learning journals

**Lesson**:

1. **Anchor Chart (5 min)**:
   - *"Every sentence has two parts: WHO or WHAT the sentence is about (the subject), and WHAT HAPPENS (the predicate)."*
   - Model:
     - "The dog / ran to the park."
     - Subject: The dog (WHO?) / Predicate: ran to the park (WHAT DID IT DO?)
   - Write 3 more examples. Students help identify the parts:
     - "My sister / eats cereal every morning."
     - "The teacher / read a funny story."
     - "Three birds / flew over the building."

2. **Sentence Puzzle (5 min)**:
   - Give pairs a set of 6 sentence strips, each cut in half (subject on one piece, predicate on another).
   - Subjects: "The big cat" / "My best friend" / "A yellow bus" / "The students" / "Our neighbor" / "Five frogs"
   - Predicates: "jumped into the pond." / "drove down the street." / "climbed the tall tree." / "played soccer after school." / "made cookies for us." / "read their books quietly."
   - Students match subjects with predicates that make sense. (Some can be matched multiple ways — that's fine!)
   - Share some silly combinations for laughs: *"Five frogs made cookies for us!"*

3. **Independent Writing (5 min)**:
   - In their learning journals, students write 3 sentences of their own.
   - Requirements: capital letter at the beginning, end punctuation, must have a clear subject and predicate.
   - Prompt: *"Write about something you've done this week at our program."*
   - Teacher circulates quickly, giving immediate feedback on capitals and punctuation.

#### Part 3: Assessment Pulls — Sight Word Inventory & Comprehension Baseline (11:00–11:30)

**Structure**: Teacher pulls students individually for the Sight Word Inventory while the group completes the Comprehension Baseline (whole-group read-aloud with written response).

**Sight Word Inventory — Individual Pulls (2–3 min per student)**:
- Use a sight word checklist of 50 high-frequency words, tiered:
  - Tier 1 (Pre-primer/Primer): the, and, is, it, was, for, on, are, have, his, they, said, from, this, with
  - Tier 2 (1st–2nd grade): because, could, would, every, people, friend, again, always, before, around
  - Tier 3 (3rd grade): through, thought, enough, especially, different, important, probably, usually, beautiful, beginning
- Student reads from a clean copy (no marks). Teacher marks accuracy on the checklist copy.
- Stop when the student misses 5 consecutive words.
- Record the highest tier mastered and total words read correctly.

**Comprehension Baseline — Whole Group (11:00–11:25)**:

**ASSESSMENT: Comprehension Baseline (read-aloud + written questions)**

- Teacher reads a fiction passage aloud (~500 words, grade 3–4 level). Suggested: a short story about a character learning something new (ties to writing prompt topic and the "Who We Are as Learners" theme).

- Suggested passage topic: A story about a girl named Maya who is nervous about her first day at a new school. She doesn't know anyone, but she meets a kind classmate at lunch who shows her around. By the end of the day, she feels better.

- Read the passage aloud twice:
  - First reading: students just listen (books closed / passage face-down).
  - Second reading: students follow along on their own copy.

- After the second reading, students answer 5 written questions independently:
  1. **Literal**: *"Where does the story take place?"* (school — the setting)
  2. **Literal**: *"What happened when Maya sat down at lunch?"* (she met a kind classmate)
  3. **Inferential**: *"How do you think Maya felt at the beginning of the day? What makes you think that?"*
  4. **Inferential**: *"Why do you think the classmate decided to help Maya?"*
  5. **Vocabulary**: *"In the story, it says Maya felt 'relieved' at the end of the day. What do you think 'relieved' means?"*

- Students write answers in complete sentences on the response sheet.
- Allow 15 minutes. If students finish early, they can draw a picture of their favorite scene.

**Data Collection**:
- Sight Word Inventory: record per student — total words correct, highest tier mastered, specific words missed
- Comprehension questions: score each question (2 points = complete and correct, 1 point = partially correct or incomplete, 0 = incorrect or blank). Record total out of 10.
- Note: Who answered inferential questions well? Who could only do literal? This determines comprehension group placement.

**Differentiation for Comprehension Baseline**:
- **Below level**: Read the passage a third time to this small group before they answer. Allow them to look back at the passage while answering. Accept shorter answers (phrases instead of full sentences).
- **On level**: Two readings as described. Answer in complete sentences.
- **Above level**: Add a 6th question: *"What lesson did Maya learn? Has something like this ever happened to you?"* (text-to-self connection).

### Exploration & Enrichment (12:00–1:00)

**Activity**: Classroom Economy Introduction — "IEP & Thrive Bucks"

**Enrichment Category**: Life Skills (Thursday theme)

**Overview**: Students learn about a classroom economy system that will run throughout the 6-week program. They earn "IEP & Thrive Bucks" for positive behaviors, completed work, and kindness. They can spend them at a weekly "store." Today they set up their wallets, learn the rules, and practice making change.

**Materials**:
- Play money sets (bills: $1, $5, $10, $20; coins optional for advanced students)
- Student "wallets" (envelopes with names, decorated)
- Classroom economy anchor chart (rules and earnings)
- "Store" price tags (for future use — show them what's coming)
- Pencils and paper (for practice problems)
- "IEP & Thrive Bucks" — custom-printed or sticker-labeled play money

**Schedule**:

1. **Introduction: How Economies Work (8 min)**:
   - *"Has anyone ever earned money for doing chores? Or saved up for something you really wanted?"*
   - *"In our classroom, we have our own economy. You can earn IEP & Thrive Bucks for things like: completing your work, being kind to a classmate, showing great effort, helping clean up, or reaching a learning goal."*
   - Post the **Earnings Chart** on the wall:
     - Completing morning work: $2
     - Trying your best on an assessment: $3
     - Helping a classmate: $1
     - Following all classroom agreements for a full block: $5
     - Exceptional effort or improvement: $5 (teacher bonus)
     - Friday clean-up helper: $2
   - *"Every Friday, we'll open the 'IEP & Thrive Store.' You can buy things like: extra free time ($10), choose-your-seat for a day ($15), homework pass ($20), small prize from the treasure box ($10), lunch with the teacher ($25)."*
   - Post the **Store Menu** with prices.

2. **Wallet Setup (5 min)**:
   - Each student receives an envelope. They write their name on it and decorate it (1–2 minutes).
   - Each student receives a "starting bonus" of $10 in play money (2 five-dollar bills).
   - *"This is your starting balance. Take care of your wallet — it goes in your folder at the end of each day."*

3. **Money Skills Practice (20 min)**:

   **Activity A: "Count Your Cash" (7 min)**
   - Give each student an assortment of bills. They count the total value and write it on their dry-erase board.
   - Example: 2 fives + 3 ones = $13.
   - Repeat with 3 different assortments.
   - **Math connection**: This is addition with money — real-world place value.

   **Activity B: "Make Change" (8 min)**
   - *"If something costs $7 and you pay with a $10 bill, how much change do you get?"*
   - Model counting up: $7... $8, $9, $10 — that's $3 change.
   - Practice 4 problems on dry-erase boards:
     - Item costs $4, pay with $10. Change = $6.
     - Item costs $12, pay with $20. Change = $8.
     - Item costs $8, pay with two $5 bills. Change = $2.
     - Item costs $15, pay with $20. Change = $5.

   **Activity C: "Shopping Scenario" (5 min)**
   - In pairs, students role-play: one is the shopkeeper, one is the buyer.
   - Shopkeeper has items with price tags ($3, $5, $8, $12).
   - Buyer selects an item, pays with play money, and shopkeeper makes change.
   - Switch roles and repeat.

4. **Earning Your First Bucks (7 min)**:
   - *"Let's practice. You've all been here for 4 days now. Let me give you some bucks you've already earned."*
   - Walk through the earnings chart. Award each student based on the week so far:
     - Completed morning work each day? That's $2 x 4 = $8.
     - Tried your best on assessments? $3 x 2 (math probe + writing) = $6.
     - Followed agreements? $5 per day = $20 (or prorated based on actual behavior).
   - Students count their total earnings and add to their starting balance.
   - Write the total on the outside of their wallet envelope.

5. **Reflection (5 min)**:
   - In their learning journals: *"I earned $___ this week so far. I want to save up for ___. To earn that, I need to ___."*
   - **Portfolio piece**: The wallet (ongoing) + the journal reflection entry.

**Academic Connection**:
- **Math**: Addition, subtraction, counting money, making change — all connected to Week 1's number sense and computation goals.
- **Reading/Writing**: Reading the earnings chart and store menu (functional literacy), writing reflections.
- **Executive Function**: Goal-setting (saving for something), delayed gratification, self-monitoring (earning bucks requires specific behaviors).

**Closing Circle (5 min)**:
- *"Tomorrow is Friday — our last day of Week 1. We'll do our self-portrait art project, review everything we've learned, and have our first store opening."*
- *"I want you to know: I've been looking at all your assessments this week, and I'm so impressed by how hard you've tried. Every single one of you showed me something you're really good at."*
- Self-monitoring checklist: complete for the day.
- Award any final bucks for the day.
- Dismissal at 1:00.
