### Math Block (9:20–10:20)

**Objective**: Students will solve addition and subtraction problems (with and without regrouping) using place value strategies, demonstrating current fluency levels. (IEP Goal: Math Computation — addition/subtraction fact fluency and multi-digit computation.)

**Materials**:
- Base-ten blocks (available at tables)
- Dry-erase boards + markers
- Place value charts (laminated)
- Chart paper for anchor chart: "Addition & Subtraction Strategies"
- Math Computation Probe (printed, 1 per student) — 20 problems, 2-minute timed
- Pencils
- Visual timer

#### Warm-Up/Activation (5 min)
- **"Number Bonds Flash"**: Teacher holds up a number (e.g., 10). Students write on dry-erase boards two numbers that add up to that number. Share.
- Repeat with: 15, 20, 100.
- *"Addition and subtraction are partners — if you know 7 + 8 = 15, you also know 15 - 8 = 7. Today we'll practice both."*

#### Direct Instruction (15 min) — CRA Level: Concrete → Representational → Abstract

1. **Addition with Regrouping Review (8 min)**:
   - **Concrete**: Use base-ten blocks to model 247 + 385.
     - Build 247 (2 flats, 4 rods, 7 units). Build 385 (3 flats, 8 rods, 5 units).
     - Combine: start with units. 7 + 5 = 12 units. Trade 10 units for 1 rod. Now we have 2 units and 1 extra rod.
     - Rods: 4 + 8 + 1 (traded) = 13 rods. Trade 10 rods for 1 flat. Now 3 rods and 1 extra flat.
     - Flats: 2 + 3 + 1 (traded) = 6 flats.
     - Answer: 632.
   - **Representational**: Draw quick-pics on the board showing the same process.
   - **Abstract**: Write the standard algorithm next to it. Show how each step matches the blocks.
   - Key language: *"When we have 10 or more in any place, we regroup — we trade 10 of those for 1 of the next place."*

2. **Subtraction with Regrouping Review (7 min)**:
   - **Concrete**: Use base-ten blocks to model 503 - 267.
     - Build 503 (5 flats, 0 rods, 3 units).
     - *"I need to subtract 7 units, but I only have 3. What do I do?"*
     - But there are 0 rods to trade from! Must go to the flats: trade 1 flat for 10 rods, then trade 1 rod for 10 units.
     - Now: 4 flats, 9 rods, 13 units. Subtract: 13 - 7 = 6; 9 - 6 = 3; 4 - 2 = 2. Answer: 236.
   - **Representational**: Model crossing out and regrouping in the algorithm.
   - **Key misconception to address**: *"When there's a zero in the middle, we can't borrow from it until we give it something to share. We have to go to the next place over first."*

#### Guided Practice (15 min)
- **"Solve and Show" — Dry-Erase Board Practice**:
  - Teacher writes a problem on the board. Students solve on their dry-erase boards using any strategy (blocks, drawing, algorithm). Hold up boards on the count of 3.
  - Problems (progressive difficulty):
    1. 45 + 38 = ___ (83 — single regrouping)
    2. 126 + 257 = ___ (383 — no regrouping, confidence builder)
    3. 364 + 478 = ___ (842 — double regrouping)
    4. 72 - 45 = ___ (27 — simple regrouping)
    5. 400 - 163 = ___ (237 — regrouping across zeros)
    6. 1,000 - 548 = ___ (452 — regrouping across multiple zeros)
  - After each problem, select one student to explain their strategy to the class.
  - Build anchor chart as you go: "Strategies We Use" — blocks, drawings, number line, algorithm.

- **Strategy Discussion**: *"Did anyone solve it a different way?"* Validate all strategies. Emphasize: *"There is no single 'right way' — the right way is the way that helps you get the correct answer and understand why."*

#### Independent Practice — Math Computation Probe (10 min)

**ASSESSMENT: Math Computation Probe (timed — 2 minutes)**

- *"This is a 2-minute timed sheet. There are 20 problems — a mix of addition and subtraction. Do as many as you can. If you get stuck on one, skip it and come back. This is NOT about finishing all 20. It's about showing me what you know right now."*
- Set visual timer for 2 minutes.
- Students work silently. When the timer goes off, pencils down.
- Collect probes.

**After the probe (remaining 8 min)**: Give students a non-graded practice sheet with 6 word problems (mixed addition and subtraction). They can use manipulatives. This keeps them engaged and provides observational data.

- Sample word problems:
  1. "Marcus had 456 baseball cards. He gave 128 to his friend. How many does he have now?"
  2. "There are 267 students at Lincoln School and 389 students at Washington School. How many students are there in both schools together?"
  3. "A bookstore had 500 books. They sold 213 on Monday and 175 on Tuesday. How many are left?"

#### Closure (5 min)
- *"What strategy worked best for you today?"* 2–3 students share.
- Anchor chart addition: tape the "Strategies We Use" chart to the wall.
- *"Tomorrow we'll keep working with addition and subtraction in a really fun way — stay tuned."*

#### Data Collection
- Score Computation Probe tonight: record number correct, number attempted, and types of errors (fact errors vs. regrouping errors vs. place value errors)
- On the word problem sheet, note: Did students choose a correct operation? Did they show work? Did they use manipulatives?
- Record strategy preferences per student (who relies on blocks? who uses the algorithm? who uses mental math?)

#### Differentiation
- **Below level**: During guided practice, provide base-ten blocks and require concrete step before writing the algorithm. On the computation probe, allow them to use a number line (this is noted on their individual data — "with support"). Word problems: limit to single-digit and 2-digit numbers.
- **On level**: Work with 3-digit numbers as described. Computation probe administered standard.
- **Above level**: Include 4-digit addition/subtraction. Word problems include 2-step problems. After the probe, challenge: *"Write your own word problem for a classmate to solve."*

#### Part 1: OG Lesson — Digraphs (10:30–10:55)

**Objective**: Students will identify and read words containing digraphs sh, ch, th, wh, and ck, distinguishing them from consonant blends, with ≥70% accuracy. (IEP Goal: Decoding — digraph recognition and application.)

**Materials**:
- OG phonogram cards: sh, ch, th, wh, ck (add to the deck)
- Dry-erase boards + markers
- Sand/salt trays
- Word cards: 15 words containing digraphs (pre-made)
- Sorting mat: "Blends vs. Digraphs" (printed, 1 per student)

**OG Lesson Structure**:

1. **Review — Quick Phonogram Drill (3 min)**:
   - Flash all cards from Monday and Tuesday (consonants, short vowels, blends).
   - Speed drill: aim for automatic responses (< 2 seconds per card).

2. **New Concept — Digraphs (8 min)**:
   - *"A blend is two letters where you hear BOTH sounds — /s/ /t/ in 'stop.' A digraph is two letters that make ONE new sound. They work as a team."*
   - Introduce each digraph with a key word and physical motion:
     - **sh** — "ship" (finger to lips, "shhhh")
     - **ch** — "chip" (chopping motion with hand)
     - **th** — "thumb" (hold up thumb). Note: 2 sounds — voiced /th/ as in "the" and unvoiced /th/ as in "thin"
     - **wh** — "whale" (blow air out — some speakers distinguish /w/ and /wh/)
     - **ck** — "duck" (quacking motion). Rule: /ck/ comes after a short vowel at the end of a word.
   - Students repeat each sound + motion + key word 3 times.
   - Write each digraph in the sand tray as you say the sound.

3. **Guided Practice — Digraph Word Reading (8 min)**:
   - Write on the board: **shop, chat, thin, when, duck, shell, check, thick, whip, stuck**
   - Students read each word. For each, ask: *"What's the digraph? What sound does it make?"*
   - Sorting activity: Give each student the "Blends vs. Digraphs" sorting mat. Teacher reads 12 words aloud. Students write each word in the correct column:
     - Blends: step, flag, grin, crisp, sled, brand
     - Digraphs: chip, shed, thick, whack, duck, thump
   - Check together. Discuss any words students found confusing.

4. **Dictation (6 min)**:
   - Sand tray dictation — teacher says the word, students write in sand:
     - **shut, chop, that, when, tack**
   - Then transfer to dry-erase boards:
     - **clash, shrimp, thud**
   - Note: "clash" and "shrimp" combine a blend AND a digraph — preview of how patterns layer.

**Data Collection**:
- Digraph accuracy: tally which digraphs each student reads/spells correctly
- Sorting mat: collect and review — which students confused blends and digraphs?
- Dictation: photograph boards, note errors

#### Part 2: Writing Baseline Assessment (10:55–11:30)

**ASSESSMENT: Baseline Writing Sample**

**Objective**: Students will produce a personal narrative writing sample of at least 5 sentences in response to a prompt, demonstrating current writing ability in organization, sentence structure, spelling, and conventions. (IEP Goal: Written Expression — baseline data.)

**Materials**:
- Writing paper: primary-lined (wider lines, with dotted midline) for students who need it; regular-lined for others
- Pencils + erasers
- Writing prompt poster (displayed at front of room)
- Anchor chart: "What Good Writers Do" (create quickly together before they write)
- Visual timer (20 minutes)

**Setup (5 min)**:
- *"Now we're going to do some writing. This is the FIRST thing you write in this program — and at the end of the summer, we'll compare it to the LAST thing you write. You'll be amazed at how much you grow."*
- Quick anchor chart — "What Good Writers Do" (co-created):
  - Start with a capital letter
  - End sentences with a period, question mark, or exclamation point
  - Try to spell words the best you can (it doesn't have to be perfect)
  - Tell what happened in order
  - Add details: what did you see, hear, feel?

- Read the prompt aloud and post it:

> **Writing Prompt**: *"Tell me about a time when you learned something new. It could be learning to ride a bike, learning a game, learning to cook something, or learning anything at all. What happened? Was it easy or hard? How did you feel?"*

- *"Close your eyes for 30 seconds and think about your story. Picture it in your mind. Who was there? Where were you? What happened first?"*

**Writing Time (20 min)**:
- Set visual timer for 20 minutes.
- *"Write as much as you can. Try to write at least 5 sentences. If you finish early, read it back to yourself and see if you can add more details."*
- Students write independently. Teacher does NOT help with spelling or ideas — this is a diagnostic sample.
- Teacher circulates silently, observing:
  - Who starts writing immediately vs. who stares at the paper?
  - Who writes in complete sentences vs. fragments?
  - Handwriting: legibility, letter formation, spacing.
  - Spelling patterns: phonetic spelling, sight word errors, vowel confusion.
  - Organization: does the student tell events in order?
  - Length: count approximate number of sentences/words per student.

**After Writing (5 min)**:
- *"Pencils down. Read your story to yourself one more time. Circle one part you're proud of."*
- Students circle their favorite part.
- Collect all writing samples.
- *"You will get these back later so you can see how much your writing changes over the summer. For today — great job putting your ideas on paper."*

**Data Collection**:
- Score each writing sample using a 4-point rubric:
  - **Organization** (1–4): Has a beginning, middle, and end? Events in order?
  - **Sentence Structure** (1–4): Complete sentences? Variety?
  - **Spelling** (1–4): Phonetic accuracy? Sight word accuracy?
  - **Conventions** (1–4): Capitals, punctuation, spacing?
  - **Detail/Elaboration** (1–4): Descriptive language? Feelings? Sensory details?
- Record total score and individual trait scores per student
- Note: this is the PRE score; the same prompt (or parallel prompt) will be given in Week 6

**Differentiation**:
- **Below level**: Provide a graphic organizer with 3 boxes: "First," "Then," "Last." Student can draw AND write. Accept 3 sentences as a goal. Provide a word bank of common words (and, the, was, because, then, but).
- **On level**: Write 5+ sentences using the prompt as described.
- **Above level**: Encourage paragraph structure (indent, topic sentence, details, closing). Challenge: *"Can you include dialogue — someone speaking in your story?"*

### Exploration & Enrichment (12:00–1:00)

**Activity**: STEM Building Challenge — "Build a Structure That Represents You"

**Enrichment Category**: STEM & Building (Wednesday theme)

**Overview**: Students design and build a small structure using craft materials that represents something about themselves — their favorite place, an imaginary room, a home for their pet, etc. This introduces the engineering design process and connects to the "Who We Are as Learners" theme.

**Materials**:
- Craft sticks (50 per student)
- Cardboard pieces (pre-cut into rectangles and squares)
- Tape (masking tape and clear tape)
- Glue sticks
- Straws (20 per student)
- Marshmallows (for connectors) or modeling clay
- Scissors
- Rulers (for measuring)
- Paper and pencils (for planning)
- Chart paper with engineering design process

**Schedule**:

1. **Introduction: The Engineering Design Process (8 min)**:
   - Post the 4-step process on chart paper:
     1. **Ask**: What am I building? What is it supposed to do?
     2. **Plan**: Sketch your idea. What materials will you use?
     3. **Build**: Construct your structure. Test it. Does it work?
     4. **Improve**: What could you change to make it better?
   - *"Engineers follow these steps when they build anything — bridges, buildings, even toys. Today, you're the engineer."*
   - Explain the challenge: *"Build a structure that represents something about you. It could be your dream bedroom, a fort you'd love to play in, a house for your favorite animal, or a building from a place you love. It must stand on its own and be at least 6 inches tall."*
   - Show materials available. Explain rules: share materials, clean up your scraps, be safe with scissors.

2. **Planning Phase (7 min)**:
   - Students sketch their plan on paper. Must include:
     - A drawing of what they want to build
     - Labels for what materials they'll use
     - Their name and a title for the structure
   - Teacher circulates: *"Tell me about your plan. Why did you choose this? What will be the hardest part?"*

3. **Building Phase (25 min)**:
   - Students build. Teacher circulates with guiding questions:
     - *"How can you make that joint stronger?"*
     - *"What if you used a triangle shape? Triangles are the strongest shape in engineering."*
     - *"Your structure keeps falling — what could you change at the base?"*
   - **Math connection**: *"How tall is your structure? Measure it with your ruler. How many craft sticks long is the base?"*
   - **Reading/writing connection**: As students build, ask: *"Can you describe what you're making in one sentence? That's going to be your label."*
   - If a student's structure collapses, coach them through the "Improve" step: *"That's exactly what engineers do. They test, it fails, and they figure out why. What happened? What will you try differently?"*

4. **Gallery Walk & Presentations (12 min)**:
   - Structures stay on tables. Students write a label card (index card): name, title, and 1 sentence about what it represents.
   - Gallery walk: students walk around and observe each structure. Must give at least 2 classmates a specific compliment: *"I like how you used triangles to make it strong"* (not just *"It's cool"*).
   - 3–4 volunteers share with the whole group: what they built, what was hard, and what they'd improve.

5. **Reflection & Cleanup (8 min)**:
   - In their learning journals, students complete:
     - *"I built ___. It represents ___."*
     - *"One challenge I had was ___."*
     - *"If I built it again, I would ___."*
   - **Portfolio piece**: Teacher photographs each structure with the student. The sketch plan + journal reflection + photo = enrichment portfolio entry for today.
   - Clean up materials thoroughly. Structures can go on a display shelf if space allows, or students take them home.

**Academic Connection**:
- **Math**: Measurement (how tall? how wide?), geometry concepts (shapes that provide stability — triangles, rectangles), counting materials used.
- **Reading/Writing**: Descriptive writing (label cards, journal reflection), oral presentation skills.

**Closing Circle (5 min)**:
- *"What was the hardest part about building today?"* (Expect answers about things falling, running out of tape, etc.)
- *"That's what engineers deal with every day. The most important thing in engineering — and in learning — is that you don't give up. You improve."*
- Self-monitoring checklist: complete for the day. Compare to the "goal of the day" sticky note.
- Preview tomorrow: *"Tomorrow we start learning about classroom jobs and money — our classroom economy begins!"*
- Dismissal at 1:00.
