### Morning Warm-Up (9:00–9:20)

**Community Check-In: "Good Morning, ___!" (3 min)**
- Students sit in a circle. Go around: each student makes eye contact with the person next to them and says, *"Good morning, [name]!"* The other responds, *"Good morning, [name]! I'm glad you're here."*
- Teacher models first. Practice the handshake/fist bump/wave option (student's choice).

**Feelings Check-In (3 min)**:
- Point to the feelings chart. Students share with a partner: *"Today I feel ___ because ___."*
- Quick whip-around: each student says their feeling word (one word only — keeps it fast).

**Brain Game: "Would You Rather?" (7 min)**:
- Stand on one side of the room or the other based on your choice:
  - "Would you rather be able to fly or be invisible?"
  - "Would you rather have pizza every day or ice cream every day?"
  - "Would you rather read a book or solve a puzzle?"
  - "Would you rather go to the beach or the mountains?"
- After each, ask 1–2 students from each side: *"Why did you choose that?"* (Builds oral language and reasoning.)

**SEL Connection: Classroom Expectations Co-Creation (5 min)**:
- On chart paper titled **"Our Learning Community Agreements"**, ask: *"What do we need from each other to learn well together?"*
- Guide students to suggest 4–5 agreements. Reframe as positive statements:
  - "Listen when someone is talking" (not "don't interrupt")
  - "Try your best, even when it's hard"
  - "Help each other"
  - "Make mistakes — that's how we learn"
  - "Take care of our space and materials"
- All students sign the chart paper. Post it permanently.

**Self-Monitoring Checklist Review (2 min)**:
- *"Remember our checklist from yesterday? Today, try to check yourself at snack time and at the end of the day. Did I do these things?"*
- Quick reminder of each item.

### Math Block (9:20–10:20)

**Objective**: Students will place numbers on a number line and compare 4-digit numbers using >, <, and = symbols with ≥75% accuracy. Students will round numbers to the nearest 10 and 100 using a number line. (IEP Goal: Number Sense — number comparison and rounding.)

**Materials**:
- Desktop number lines (0–10,000) — 1 per student
- Dry-erase boards + markers
- Base-ten blocks (available at tables for students who need them)
- Place value chart (laminated, from yesterday)
- Chart paper for anchor chart: "Comparison & Rounding"
- Printed practice worksheet: Number Line & Comparison (15 problems)
- Open number line template (blank, for rounding)

#### Warm-Up/Activation (5 min)
- **"Human Number Line"**: Give each student a card with a number on it (use values between 100 and 5,000). Students must silently arrange themselves in order from least to greatest along a line on the floor (marked with tape).
- Once they think they're in order, read the line aloud as a group. Discuss: *"How did you know where to stand? What did you look at first?"*
- This gets bodies moving and previews today's content.

#### Direct Instruction (15 min) — CRA Level: Concrete → Representational

1. **Number Line Placement (8 min)**:
   - Draw a number line on chart paper: 0 to 1,000 with tick marks at every 100.
   - *"Where would 350 go on this number line? How do you know?"*
   - Model thinking aloud: *"350 is between 300 and 400. It's exactly halfway."*
   - Place 350 with a dot and label.
   - Now ask: *"Where does 725 go? Talk to your partner."* Have a student come up and place it.
   - Place 2 more numbers together: 180 and 910.
   - Students practice on their desktop number lines: call out 3 numbers, students mark them with dry-erase marker on their laminated number lines. Hold up to show.

2. **Comparison Using Number Lines (7 min)**:
   - *"When two numbers are on a number line, how do we know which is greater?"* (The one farther to the right.)
   - Write on the board: **462 ___ 481**
   - Both numbers are between 400 and 500. Place them on the line. 462 is to the left. So 462 < 481.
   - Introduce/review the symbols: > (greater than), < (less than), = (equal to).
   - Memory trick: *"The alligator always eats the bigger number — the open mouth faces the bigger number."*
   - Practice 3 pairs together on dry-erase boards:
     - 3,207 ___ 3,270
     - 1,589 ___ 1,589
     - 4,008 ___ 4,080
   - Key question: *"When two numbers have the same thousands digit, what do you look at next?"*

#### Guided Practice (20 min)

**Activity: "Number Line Challenge" — Partner Work**

- Students work in pairs. Each pair gets a large blank number line (paper strip) and a set of 10 number cards (pre-cut, numbers between 1,000 and 9,999).
- Task 1: Place all 10 numbers on the number line in the correct approximate positions. Glue them down.
- Task 2: Choose 4 pairs of numbers and write comparison sentences using >, <, = on their dry-erase boards.
- Task 3: Teacher calls out 2 numbers. Students write the comparison on their boards and hold up. Check as a class.

**Rounding Introduction (within guided practice)**:
- After 10 min of comparison work, shift to rounding.
- Draw a number line: 400 to 500, with tick marks at every 10.
- *"Where does 437 land? Is it closer to 400 or 500?"*
- Students see it visually: 437 is closer to 400. So 437 rounded to the nearest hundred is 400.
- *"Now look at the tens: is 437 closer to 430 or 440?"* (440 — because 7 is ≥5.)
- Practice 4 rounding problems together using the number line:
  - Round 682 to the nearest 10 → 680
  - Round 682 to the nearest 100 → 700
  - Round 3,250 to the nearest 100 → 3,300
  - Round 3,250 to the nearest 1,000 → 3,000

#### Independent Practice (10 min)
- **Number Line & Comparison Worksheet** (15 problems):
  - Questions 1–5: Place numbers on a number line (number line provided)
  - Questions 6–10: Compare two numbers using >, <, =
  - Questions 11–15: Round to the nearest 10, 100, or 1,000

- Teacher circulates. Look for:
  - Do students use the number line as a tool, or try to do it in their head?
  - Common error: rounding 450 to the nearest hundred — do they know the "5 rounds up" rule?
  - Do students confuse > and < symbols?

#### Closure (5 min)
- **Exit Ticket** (dry-erase boards):
  - *"Write a number that is greater than 2,500 but less than 3,000."* (Check: is it in range?)
  - *"Round 4,678 to the nearest hundred."* (Answer: 4,700)
- Quick share: 2 students explain their rounding reasoning.
- *"Tomorrow we'll start looking at addition and subtraction — how we put numbers together and take them apart."*

#### Data Collection
- Collect the Number Line & Comparison Worksheet — score sections separately (placement /5, comparison /5, rounding /5)
- Note students who consistently misuse > and < symbols
- Note students who could not round even with a number line (may need concrete support with base-ten blocks for rounding)
- Record exit ticket accuracy

#### Differentiation
- **Below level**: Provide a number line with more tick marks (every 10 instead of every 100). Use 3-digit numbers only. Provide a > / < reference card at their desk.
- **On level**: 4-digit numbers as described. Use number line as a visual tool.
- **Above level**: Include 5-digit numbers. Challenge: *"Can you round 45,678 to the nearest ten-thousand?"* Ask them to explain the rule for rounding when a 5 is in the deciding place.

#### Part 1: OG Review — Whole Group with Teaching Assistant (10:30–11:00)

**Objective**: Students will read and spell CVC and CCVC words with consonant blends (bl, cl, fl, sl, br, cr, dr, fr, gr, tr, st, sp, sn, sm) with ≥70% accuracy. Students will begin learning the first 5 red words (the, was, is, are, have). (IEP Goal: Decoding — consonant blends; Sight word recognition.)

**Materials**:
- OG phonogram cards (consonants, short vowels, blends)
- Red word cards: the, was, is, are, have
- Dry-erase boards + markers
- Sand/salt trays
- Word list: 20 CVC/CCVC words (printed, 1 per student)

**OG Lesson Structure**:

1. **Review — Phonogram Card Drill (5 min)**:
   - Quick drill: flash all consonant and short vowel cards from yesterday.
   - Today add blend cards: bl, cl, fl, sl, br, cr, dr, fr, gr, tr, st, sp, sn, sm
   - Students say the sounds chorally. Teacher notes hesitations.
   - Speed round: go through the deck as fast as students can respond accurately.

2. **New Concept — Red Words (8 min)**:
   - *"Some words don't follow the rules. We call them 'red words' because they're like red lights — you have to stop and remember them."*
   - Introduce 5 red words: **the, was, is, are, have**
   - For each word:
     - Teacher holds up the card and reads it.
     - Students repeat.
     - Spell it aloud together (letter by letter).
     - Students write it in the sand tray 3 times.
     - Underline the "tricky part" (the part that doesn't follow the rules):
       - **the** — the "e" at the end doesn't make it say /thē/
       - **was** — the "a" says /ŭ/ not /ă/
       - **is** — the "s" says /z/
       - **are** — the whole word is irregular
       - **have** — the "a" says /ă/ but there's a silent "e"
   - Heart tap: students place their hand on their heart and spell each word from memory (stores it in "heart words" — words we know by heart).

3. **Guided Practice — Blend Word Reading (10 min)**:
   - Write on the board: **stop, flag, drip, clam, snap, grin, step, blob, slim, frog**
   - Students finger-tap and read each word.
   - For each word, ask: *"What's the blend? What's the vowel? What's the ending sound?"*
   - Then dictation — teacher says 5 words, students write on dry-erase boards:
     - **slip, brand, crisp, stomp, blend**
   - Students hold up boards. Teacher checks and provides immediate feedback.

4. **Connected Text (7 min)**:
   - Read a short decodable passage together (CVC/CCVC level):
     > *"Brad had a big red sled. He ran up the hill and slid down fast. The sled hit a bump and Brad fell in the slush. He got up and did it again!"*
   - Read once together (choral reading).
   - Read again: students whisper-read while teacher listens to 2–3 individuals.
   - Comprehension check: *"What happened first? What happened at the end? Would you sled down that hill? Why or why not?"*

**Data Collection**:
- Tally blend accuracy on class roster (which blends are students missing?)
- Note red word recognition — who learned all 5? Who is still struggling?
- Dictation boards: photograph or note errors (common: blending errors in the middle of the word, vowel substitutions)

#### Part 2: Phonics Screener Part 2 + ORF Baseline — Individual Pulls (11:00–11:30)

**Structure**: Teacher pulls students individually for Phonics Screener Part 2 (blends, digraphs, vowel teams) and ORF Baseline. Remaining students work on a structured independent task.

**Phonics Screener Part 2 — Individual (3–4 min per student)**:
- Continues from Monday. Covers: consonant blends, digraphs (sh, ch, th, wh, ck), vowel teams (initial assessment — not yet taught).
- Students who completed Part 1 yesterday now do Part 2.
- New students (not yet screened) do Parts 1 + 2 in sequence.

**ORF Baseline — Individual (2 min per student)**:
- Use a grade-level passage (grade 3 passage for 3rd graders, grade 4 for 4th graders).
- Student reads aloud for 1 minute. Teacher marks errors on a scoring copy.
- Record: words correct per minute (WCPM), accuracy percentage, types of errors (substitutions, omissions, insertions).
- After timing, ask 2–3 comprehension questions about the passage (brief, to gauge understanding vs. just speed).

**Independent Task for Remaining Students: "Word Hunt" (11:00–11:30)**:
- Students receive a printed passage (age-appropriate, 150–200 words, about a nature topic to connect to yesterday's enrichment).
- Task (posted on chart paper):
  1. Read the passage quietly to yourself.
  2. Circle all the words that start with a blend (bl, cl, fl, sl, br, cr, dr, fr, gr, tr, st, sp, sn, sm). Write them in your journal under the heading "Blend Words I Found."
  3. Underline any red words you find (the, was, is, are, have).
  4. Choose your favorite sentence and copy it neatly into your journal.
  5. Draw a picture of what the passage was about.
- This is self-paced and keeps students engaged while teacher pulls individuals.

**Differentiation**:
- **Below level**: Provide the passage with blends already highlighted in yellow. Student just needs to copy them. Reduce to finding 5 blend words.
- **On level**: Complete the word hunt as described.
- **Above level**: After finding blends, sort them into categories (l-blends, r-blends, s-blends). Write 2 sentences of their own using blend words.

### Exploration & Enrichment (12:00–1:00)

**Activity**: "About Me" Museum — Personal Artifact Sharing

**Enrichment Category**: Museums & Culture (Tuesday theme)

**Overview**: Each student presents a personal artifact they brought from home and explains its significance. The classroom is arranged as a "museum" with artifacts displayed on desks. Students practice oral language, active listening, and descriptive writing.

**Materials**:
- Students' artifacts from home (reminder was sent Monday)
- Index cards (for museum labels, 2 per student)
- Pencils + colored pencils
- "About Me" template handout (pre-printed)
- Tape or sticky tack (to display labels)
- Optional: tablecloths or fabric to make "display tables" look museum-like

**Schedule**:

1. **Introduction: What Is a Museum? (5 min)**:
   - *"Has anyone been to a museum? What did you see there?"*
   - *"A museum tells stories through objects. Today, our classroom is a museum — and each of you is an exhibit. Your artifact tells a story about who you are."*
   - Model with your own artifact: *"I brought this [object]. It's important to me because ___. It tells you that I am someone who ___."*
   - Post the sharing framework on the board:
     1. "This is my ___."
     2. "It's important to me because ___."
     3. "It tells you that I am someone who ___."

2. **Museum Label Writing (10 min)**:
   - Each student writes a "museum label" for their artifact on an index card:
     - Object name
     - Owner's name
     - 1–2 sentences about why it's important
   - Teacher circulates to help with spelling and sentence structure.
   - Students arrange their artifact + label on their desk space.

3. **Sharing Circle — "Museum Presentations" (25 min)**:
   - Each student stands at their "exhibit" and presents (using the framework).
   - Allow about 2 minutes per student (8–10 students).
   - After each presentation, audience members may ask 1 question or give 1 compliment.
   - Teacher models active listening: eye contact, body still, ask a question.
   - Sentence starters for audience posted on board:
     - "I liked hearing about ___."
     - "I was surprised that ___."
     - "I have a question: ___?"

4. **Museum Walk (10 min)**:
   - Students walk around the room like museum visitors, quietly looking at each exhibit.
   - On a half-sheet of paper, students write the name of one classmate's artifact that they found interesting and why (1–2 sentences).
   - This practices writing and builds community — students feel seen.

5. **Wrap-Up & Portfolio (10 min)**:
   - Collect museum labels — these go in the portfolio.
   - Students complete the "About Me" template handout:
     - My name: ___
     - My age/grade: ___
     - 3 things I'm good at: ___
     - 1 thing I want to learn this summer: ___
     - My favorite subject: ___
     - Draw yourself: [box]
   - **Portfolio piece**: Museum label + "About Me" template.
   - Teacher takes a photo of each student with their artifact.

**Academic Connection**:
- **Reading/Writing**: Oral presentation using sentence frameworks; descriptive writing on museum labels; active listening comprehension.
- **Math**: *"How many artifacts do we have total? If we sorted them into categories (sports, family, hobbies, etc.), which category would have the most?"* — quick sorting/data connection.

**Closing Circle (5 min at end)**:
- *"What did you learn about someone today that you didn't know before?"*
- 3–4 students share.
- Self-monitoring checklist: students complete for the day.
- Preview tomorrow: *"Tomorrow in enrichment we're building something — it's STEM day! Think about what kind of structure you'd like to build."*
- Dismissal at 1:00.
