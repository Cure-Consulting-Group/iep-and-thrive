### Morning Warm-Up (9:00–9:20)

**Community Check-In: "Breaking It Down"**

1. **Feelings Check-In (3 min)**:
   - Students point to the feelings chart on entry.
   - Teacher acknowledges the midpoint: *"We are officially halfway through our summer program. That means you've already been working hard for three weeks. This week, our theme is Taking Things Apart — we're going to learn that the best way to understand something big is to break it into smaller pieces."*

2. **Executive Function Mini-Lesson: "Break It Down" Strategy (10 min)**:
   - Hold up a long word on a card: **basketball**
   - *"This is a big word. If I try to read it all at once, my brain might freeze. But what if I break it into parts?"* Cover half: **basket** + **ball**. *"Now I can read each part!"*
   - Hold up a math problem: 48 + 27. *"If I try to add this in my head all at once, it's tricky. But what if I break it down? 48 + 20 = 68. Then 68 + 7 = 75."*
   - Hold up a writing prompt: "Write a story about a time you were brave." *"That feels like a LOT. But what if I break it down? First: Who was there? Then: What happened? Then: How did it end?"*
   - Post the **"Break It Down" anchor chart**:
     - Big words → syllables
     - Big numbers → place value / parts
     - Big tasks → small steps
     - Big feelings → name each part
   - *"This week, any time something feels hard, I want you to say: 'Let me break it down.' That's our power phrase for the week."*

3. **Brain Game: "Compound Word Smash" (5 min)**:
   - Teacher says two words. Students smash them together and say the compound word.
   - sun + flower = sunflower; back + pack = backpack; cup + cake = cupcake; rain + bow = rainbow; pop + corn = popcorn; bed + room = bedroom
   - Then reverse: Teacher says the compound word, students break it apart.
   - *"See? You just took words apart AND put them back together. That's what readers do with big words."*

4. **Self-Monitoring Checklist Review (2 min)**:
   - Students get their laminated checklists. Review goals for the day.
   - New addition this week: students have a simple progress graph in their folders. *"On Friday, you'll add your new scores to your graph. We're tracking our own growth."*

### Math Block (9:20–10:20)

**Objective**: Students will identify and name unit fractions (1/2, 1/3, 1/4, 1/6, 1/8) using fraction strips and fraction circles, explaining that fractions represent equal parts of a whole. (IEP Goal: Math Concepts — fraction identification and representation.)

**Materials**:
- Fraction strips (commercial or laminated — 1 whole, halves, thirds, fourths, sixths, eighths) — 1 set per student
- Fraction circles (same denominator set) — 1 set per pair
- Pattern blocks (hexagons, trapezoids, rhombuses, triangles) — 1 set per table
- Dry-erase boards + markers (1 per student)
- Chart paper + marker (for anchor chart)
- "My Fraction Strips" recording sheet (printed, 1 per student)
- Resealable bags for fraction strips (1 per student)

#### Warm-Up/Activation (5 min)
- Place a piece of chart paper on each table with a circle drawn on it.
- *"Your table has a pizza. I need you to figure out how to share it equally among everyone at your table. Draw lines to show the cuts. Go!"*
- After 2 minutes, gallery walk: *"Did all the tables cut their pizza the same way? Why not?"* (Different number of people at each table.)
- Key question: *"What does it mean to share equally? Why does that matter?"*
- *"When we share something equally — that's fractions. Fractions are what happen when we take one whole thing and break it into equal parts."*

#### Direct Instruction (15 min) — CRA Level: Concrete

1. **Introducing the Whole (3 min)**:
   - Hold up the "1 whole" fraction strip. *"This is one whole. It's not broken into pieces — it's complete."*
   - *"Now watch."* Take the 2-piece (halves) strip. Place it directly below the whole. *"I broke the whole into 2 equal parts. Each part is called one-half. We write it like this."*
   - Write on board: **1/2**
   - *"The bottom number — the denominator — tells me how many equal parts the whole was divided into. The top number — the numerator — tells me how many parts I'm talking about."*
   - Tap the desk rhythmically: *"De-NOM-i-na-tor — DOWN below. NU-mer-a-tor — on top, the NUMBER we know."*

2. **Exploring Unit Fractions with Strips (7 min)**:
   - Students take out their fraction strip sets.
   - *"Line up your whole strip at the top. Now place the halves strip below it. Do the two halves cover the whole exactly?"* (Yes.)
   - Repeat with thirds: *"How many equal pieces? Three. Each piece is one-third: 1/3."*
   - Repeat with fourths (1/4), sixths (1/6), eighths (1/8).
   - After each: students write the fraction on their dry-erase board and hold it up.
   - Key observation: *"What do you notice about the size of the pieces as the denominator gets bigger?"* Guide to discovery: more pieces = smaller pieces.
   - Post vocabulary on anchor chart:
     - **Fraction**: a part of a whole
     - **Numerator**: how many parts we have (top number)
     - **Denominator**: how many equal parts in the whole (bottom number)
     - **Unit fraction**: a fraction with 1 on top (1/2, 1/3, 1/4...)

3. **Pattern Block Connection (5 min)**:
   - Hold up a yellow hexagon. *"This is our whole."*
   - *"Can you find a block that is exactly half of the hexagon?"* (Red trapezoid — 2 fit on the hexagon.)
   - *"What about one-third?"* (Blue rhombus — 3 fit on the hexagon.)
   - *"One-sixth?"* (Green triangle — 6 fit on the hexagon.)
   - Students verify by placing blocks on top of the hexagon.
   - *"So the green triangle is 1/6 of the hexagon. The blue rhombus is 1/3. The red trapezoid is 1/2. Same whole — different fractional parts."*

#### Guided Practice (15 min)

- **"My Fraction Strips" Recording Sheet**: Students line up fraction strips and trace/color each row, writing the fraction name for each piece.
  - Row 1: 1 whole — write "1"
  - Row 2: 2 halves — label each piece "1/2"
  - Row 3: 3 thirds — label each piece "1/3"
  - Row 4: 4 fourths — label each piece "1/4"
  - Row 5: 6 sixths — label each piece "1/6"
  - Row 6: 8 eighths — label each piece "1/8"

- After completing the sheet, answer these questions (written on board):
  1. Which unit fraction has the biggest pieces? (1/2)
  2. Which unit fraction has the smallest pieces? (1/8)
  3. How many eighths does it take to equal one whole? (8)
  4. How many fourths does it take to equal one whole? (4)

- Teacher circulates. For each student, ask: *"Point to one-fourth on your strip. How do you know that's one-fourth?"* Listen for use of vocabulary (denominator = 4, so the whole is in 4 equal parts).

#### Independent Practice (10 min)

- **Fraction Match Game** (pairs): 12 cards with fraction strip pictures, 12 cards with fraction names. Students match the picture to the name.
- While students play, teacher pulls 2–3 students for re-teaching if needed during guided practice.

#### Closure (5 min)
- **Exit Ticket** (dry-erase boards): Teacher holds up a fraction circle with 3 out of 4 pieces shaded.
  - *"What fraction is shaded?"* (3/4)
  - *"Is 3/4 a unit fraction?"* (No — the numerator is 3, not 1.)
  - *"What IS the unit fraction for this circle?"* (1/4)
- Collect recording sheets for portfolio.
- Preview tomorrow: *"Tomorrow we'll put fractions on a number line and start finding fractions that are equal — even though they look different!"*

#### Differentiation
- **Group A (Below level)**: Stay with halves and fourths only. Use fraction circles (more intuitive than strips for some learners). Teacher provides a vocabulary card with numerator/denominator definitions and arrows showing which is top/bottom. Fair sharing language: "If 4 kids share one cookie equally, each kid gets 1/4."
- **Group B (On level)**: Full progression through halves, thirds, fourths, sixths, eighths as described. Transition from strips to recording sheet. Pattern block exploration included.
- **Group C (Above level)**: After completing the recording sheet, add a row for tenths and twelfths (draw their own). Challenge question: "If I told you a fraction was 1/100, would each piece be big or tiny? Why?" Begin thinking about fractions greater than 1: "What would 5/4 look like with your strips?"

#### Part 1: OG Lesson — VCCV Syllable Division (10:30–11:05)

**Objective**: Students will divide VCCV words between the two consonants and decode each syllable, reading multisyllabic words with ≥70% accuracy. (IEP Goal: Decoding — multisyllabic word reading using syllable division.)

**Materials**:
- OG phonogram cards — cumulative deck (all patterns Weeks 1–4)
- Red word cards: answer, listen (today's 2 new red words)
- Multisyllabic VCCV word cards (printed on cardstock, can be cut at syllable break)
- Scissors (1 per student)
- Dry-erase boards + markers
- Syllable type reference chart (updated through Week 4)
- Sand/salt trays

**OG Lesson Structure**:

1. **Review — Phonogram Drill (5 min)**:
   - Rapid-fire phonogram card review: all consonants, short vowels, digraphs, vowel teams (ai/ay, ee/ea, oa/ow, oi/oy, ou/ow, au/aw), r-controlled vowels (ar, or, er/ir/ur).
   - Students respond chorally with the sound.
   - Speed round: teacher flips cards faster. *"Let's see if you can keep up!"*
   - Any cards with >50% hesitation go in the "review" pile for tomorrow.

2. **Red Words — Introduce "answer" and "listen" (5 min)**:
   - Hold up the card **answer**.
   - *"This word doesn't follow the rules we've learned. The 'w' is silent. Watch my mouth: AN-ser. The w is there but we don't hear it."*
   - Multisensory practice: students say "answer," spell it aloud (a-n-s-w-e-r), write it in sand trays, then on dry-erase boards. Underline the tricky part (w).
   - Repeat with **listen**: *"The 't' is silent. LIS-en. We don't hear the t."*
   - Students underline the silent t.
   - Practice in a sentence: *"Write: 'Listen to the answer.' Circle the two red words."*

3. **New Concept — VCCV Syllable Division (10 min)**:
   - Write on chart paper: **rabbit**
   - *"This word has more than one syllable. To figure it out, I need to break it apart — just like our theme this week!"*
   - Step-by-step:
     1. *"First, I find the vowels."* Underline **a** and **i**. *"Two vowels — that usually means two syllables."*
     2. *"Now I look at what's between the vowels."* Circle **bb**. *"Two consonants between the vowels. This is called a VCCV pattern — Vowel, Consonant, Consonant, Vowel."*
     3. *"When I see two consonants between vowels, I divide between them."* Draw a line: **rab | bit**.
     4. *"Now I read each syllable. 'rab' — that's a closed syllable, short a. 'bit' — closed syllable, short i. Rab-bit. Rabbit!"*
   - Post the **VCCV Rule**: When two consonants appear between two vowels, divide between the consonants. Each syllable is usually a closed syllable (short vowel sound).
   - Model 2 more: **napkin** (nap | kin), **basket** (bas | ket)
   - Students identify vowels, consonants between, draw the division line, and read.

4. **Guided Practice — "Cut It Apart" Activity (8 min)**:
   - Distribute VCCV word cards (printed large, one word per card):
     - **picnic, garden, helmet, sunset, plastic, muffin, puppet, pencil, cactus, blanket**
   - Students work in pairs:
     1. Underline the vowels
     2. Circle the two consonants between
     3. Draw a line where they would divide
     4. Cut the card at the division point with scissors
     5. Read each syllable, then blend together
   - Teacher circulates and listens to each pair read at least 2 words.

5. **Dictation (7 min)**:
   - Students use dry-erase boards.
   - *"I'm going to say a word. You repeat it, tap the syllables on your arm, then write it — dividing it with a line between syllables."*
   - Dictation words:
     - **rabbit** (model together)
     - **napkin**
     - **sunset** (also a compound word — note this!)
     - **puppet**
     - **basket**
   - After each word: *"Hold up your board. Where did you divide? What type of syllable is the first part? The second part?"*
   - Sentence dictation: **"The rabbit sat in the basket."** (Includes VCCV words + review CVC words.)

**Data Collection**:
- Track per student: VCCV word reading accuracy (out of 10 words from cut-apart activity)
- Note who could identify the vowels and consonant pattern independently vs. who needed prompting
- Dictation: note spelling accuracy and correct syllable division

#### Part 2: Inference Introduction & Writing (11:05–11:30)

**Objective**: Students will make an inference using the "I read... I know... So I think..." framework when presented with a picture and a short passage. (IEP Goal: Comprehension — making inferences using text evidence.)

**Materials**:
- Inference anchor chart (pre-made or built live): "I read... I know... So I think..."
- 3 inference picture cards (high-quality images showing a scene where students must infer what happened or what will happen)
- Short passage (4–5 sentences, at instructional level) with 2 inference questions
- Reading response journals
- Sticky notes

**Lesson**:

1. **Introduction: What Is an Inference? (5 min)**:
   - Hold up a picture: a child sitting on the ground next to a bicycle, holding their knee, with the bicycle tipped over.
   - *"What happened here?"* Students respond: "They fell off the bike."
   - *"Does the picture SAY 'I fell off my bike'? No. So how did you figure that out?"*
   - *"You used clues in the picture AND what you already know about bikes and falling. That's called an inference."*
   - Post anchor chart:
     - **I read** (or I see)... → clues from the text or picture
     - **I know**... → what I already know from my own life
     - **So I think**... → my inference (what I figure out)
   - Model with the bike picture: *"I see a kid on the ground next to a tipped-over bike, holding their knee. I know that when bikes tip over, people can fall and get hurt. So I think the kid fell off the bike and scraped their knee."*

2. **Guided Practice with Pictures (5 min)**:
   - Show 2 more pictures. For each, students turn-and-talk using the framework, then share.
   - Picture 2: A woman holding an umbrella, looking up at a gray sky, puddles on the ground.
     - *"I see puddles and a gray sky. I know that puddles come from rain. So I think it just rained — or is about to rain again."*
   - Picture 3: A boy standing at a door with a backpack, and a woman waving from inside.
     - *"I see a boy with a backpack at the door and someone waving. I know kids carry backpacks to school. So I think the boy is leaving for school and his mom is saying goodbye."*

3. **Writing Connection — Narrative Middle (15 min)**:
   - *"This week, you're going to finish the story you started in Week 3. Today, let's work on the middle — the part where the problem happens and things get interesting."*
   - Quick review: *"What are the parts of a story?"* (Character, setting, problem, events, solution, ending.)
   - *"The middle is where the problem unfolds. The character tries to solve it — maybe it works, maybe it doesn't. This is where you add details that help the reader SEE and FEEL what's happening."*
   - Model "show, don't tell" with one sentence:
     - Tell: "She was scared."
     - Show: "Her hands shook and she pressed her back against the wall, barely breathing."
   - *"Which one paints a picture in your mind?"*
   - Students open their narrative drafts from Week 3. Today's task: write the middle (at least 5 sentences). Try to include at least one "show, don't tell" detail.
   - Post the **Sensory Details Word Bank** for reference (sight, sound, touch, smell, taste).
   - Teacher circulates with sticky notes, writing one positive comment on each student's draft: *"I can picture this!" or "Great action detail!"*

**Data Collection**:
- Note which students can articulate the inference framework independently
- Observe narrative drafts: who is writing with detail vs. who is producing minimal sentences
- Collect narrative drafts at end of day (keep in portfolio folder)

**Differentiation**:
- **Below level**: Provide the inference sentence frame on a card. For writing, give a "middle of the story" graphic organizer with boxes: Event 1 → Event 2 → Event 3. Students can draw and label before writing sentences.
- **On level**: Use the inference framework with pictures and short text. Write the narrative middle using the sensory details word bank.
- **Above level**: Make inferences from a short text passage (not just pictures). For writing, include dialogue in the middle of their story using correct punctuation.

### Exploration & Enrichment (12:00–1:00)

**Activity**: Dissecting Plants — Parts and Functions

**Enrichment Category**: Science + Informational Text Connection (Monday theme)

**Overview**: Students carefully take apart flowering plants and bean seedlings to identify and label parts (roots, stem, leaves, petals, seeds). This connects to the week's theme ("Taking Things Apart"), builds informational text vocabulary, and introduces fractions in a real-world context (parts of a whole plant).

**Materials**:
- Flowering plants with visible parts (3–4 stems per table — daisies, lilies, or similar with roots intact if possible; alternatively, bean seedlings grown in advance)
- Magnifying glasses (1 per student)
- Paper plates (1 per student — for dissection surface)
- Tweezers (1 per student, plastic or blunt-tip)
- Plant Parts diagram (printed, 1 per student — labeled: roots, stem, leaf, flower/petal, seed)
- Blank recording sheet: "My Plant Dissection" (sketch area + lines for labeling + sentence starters)
- Chart paper for group observations
- Tape (for taping plant parts to recording sheet)
- Wet paper towels for cleanup
- Safety note: confirm no student allergies to plants being used

**Schedule**:

1. **Introduction (8 min, inside)**:
   - *"Scientists don't just look at things — they take them apart to understand how they work. Today, you're going to be plant scientists."*
   - Show the Plant Parts diagram. Review each part and its function:
     - **Roots**: absorb water and nutrients from soil; anchor the plant
     - **Stem**: carries water and nutrients up to the leaves and flowers; holds the plant up
     - **Leaves**: make food for the plant using sunlight (photosynthesis)
     - **Flower/Petals**: attract pollinators; protect the seeds inside
     - **Seeds**: grow into new plants
   - *"Your job today: carefully take your plant apart, find each part, and label it."*
   - Model: *"I start at the top. I gently pull one petal off. I look at it with my magnifying glass. I see tiny lines — those are called veins. They carry water."*
   - Rules: gentle hands, use tweezers for small parts, everything stays on your plate, wash hands after.

2. **Plant Dissection (20 min)**:
   - Each student receives a plant and a paper plate.
   - Students carefully separate: petals, leaves, stem, seeds (if visible), roots (if present).
   - As they work, they arrange parts on their plate and sketch each part on the recording sheet, labeling it.
   - Teacher circulates with prompting questions:
     - *"How many petals does your flower have? Could you write that as a fraction of the whole flower?"*
     - *"What do you notice about the inside of the stem? Is it hollow or solid?"*
     - *"Look at the leaf with your magnifying glass. What do you see?"*
     - *"If I took away the roots, what would happen to the plant? How do you know?"*
   - **Math connection**: *"Your flower had [X] petals. You've pulled off [Y]. What fraction of the petals have you removed?"* Students write the fraction on their recording sheet.
   - **Reading connection**: Students must write at least 2 complete sentences describing what they observed. Sentence starters on board:
     - "I noticed that the ___ of the plant..."
     - "The function of the ___ is to..."
     - "I was surprised that..."

3. **Group Observations (10 min)**:
   - Each table shares one interesting discovery.
   - Teacher records observations on chart paper: "What We Learned by Taking a Plant Apart."
   - Discussion question: *"Could the plant work if one part was missing? Why does each part matter?"*
   - Connect to theme: *"Just like we broke big words into syllables this morning, we broke the plant into parts. When we take things apart, we understand them better."*

4. **Tape and Record (10 min)**:
   - Students tape small, flat plant parts (a petal, a leaf) onto their recording sheet.
   - Complete the sentence starters.
   - Teacher takes photos of students' dissection plates for portfolio documentation.

5. **Closing Circle (12 min)**:
   - Clean up: plant parts in compost/trash, plates disposed of, hands washed.
   - Return to circle. Each student shares one thing they learned about plants today.
   - **"Break It Down" reflection**: *"We broke down big words into syllables, we broke a whole into fractions, and we broke a plant into parts. What does breaking things down help us do?"* (Understand them better.)
   - Self-monitoring checklist review: students fill in their checklist for the day.
   - Preview tomorrow: *"Tomorrow, we'll learn how museums take care of really old, fragile things — by carefully taking them apart and putting them back together. And in reading, we'll learn a new syllable pattern called VCV."*
   - Dismissal at 1:00.

**Academic Connection**:
- **Reading/Writing**: Informational text vocabulary (roots, stem, function, photosynthesis), descriptive writing, observation as pre-writing
- **Math**: Fractions in a real-world context (petals as parts of a whole flower)
- **Science**: Plant biology, scientific observation and recording
