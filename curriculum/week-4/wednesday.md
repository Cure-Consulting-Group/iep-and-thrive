### Math Block (9:20–10:20)

**Objective**: Students will compare fractions with the same denominator and fractions with the same numerator using area models and fraction strips, recording comparisons with >, <, and = symbols. (IEP Goal: Math Concepts — comparing fractions using models.)

**Materials**:
- Fraction strips (from Monday/Tuesday) — 1 per student
- Area model fraction worksheets (pre-printed rectangles divided into equal parts for shading) — 1 per student
- Fraction comparison recording sheet (with >, <, = spaces) — 1 per student
- Dry-erase boards + markers
- Chart paper + marker
- Colored pencils (2 colors per student)

#### Warm-Up/Activation (5 min)
- **"Which Would You Rather Have?"** game:
  - *"Would you rather have 1/2 of a pizza or 1/4 of a pizza?"* Students vote (show of hands).
  - *"Would you rather have 3/8 of a candy bar or 5/8 of a candy bar?"*
  - *"Would you rather have 1/3 of a cake or 1/6 of a cake?"*
  - After each: *"Why? How did you decide?"* Listen for reasoning.
  - *"Today we're going to prove our answers using models — and learn the rules for comparing fractions."*

#### Direct Instruction (15 min) — CRA Level: Concrete → Representational

1. **Comparing Fractions with the Same Denominator (7 min)**:
   - Draw two rectangles on chart paper, both divided into 8 equal parts.
   - Shade 3/8 in one, 5/8 in the other.
   - *"Both rectangles are divided into eighths — the same size pieces. Which has more shaded?"* (5/8.)
   - *"When the denominator is the same, the pieces are the same size. So we just compare the numerators — who has MORE pieces?"*
   - Write the rule: **Same denominator → compare the numerators. More pieces = larger fraction.**
   - Verify with fraction strips: lay 3/8 next to 5/8. The 5/8 bar is longer.
   - Practice together: 2/6 vs. 5/6, 1/4 vs. 3/4, 7/8 vs. 4/8. Students give thumbs-up for the larger fraction.

2. **Comparing Fractions with the Same Numerator (8 min)**:
   - *"Now here's a trickier one."* Draw two rectangles: one divided into 3 equal parts, one into 6 equal parts. Shade 1 part in each.
   - *"1/3 and 1/6. Both have 1 piece shaded. But which fraction is BIGGER?"*
   - Students use fraction strips to compare: 1/3 strip vs. 1/6 strip side by side. *"1/3 is bigger!"*
   - *"Wait — 6 is a bigger number than 3. Why is 1/6 SMALLER?"*
   - Key insight: *"When you divide something into MORE pieces, each piece gets SMALLER. So a bigger denominator means smaller pieces."*
   - Write the rule: **Same numerator → compare the denominators. BIGGER denominator = SMALLER pieces = SMALLER fraction.**
   - Emphasize: *"This is the tricky part of fractions. More pieces doesn't mean more pizza — it means smaller slices!"*
   - Practice together: 1/2 vs. 1/8, 1/4 vs. 1/6, 2/3 vs. 2/8. Students show thumbs for the larger fraction.

3. **Transition to Area Models (Representational)**:
   - *"We've been using fraction strips. Now let's draw what we see."*
   - Model: draw two identical rectangles. Divide one into 4 parts, shade 3. Divide the other into 4 parts, shade 1.
   - *"3/4 > 1/4. I can see it in my drawing. I don't need the strips anymore — the drawing proves it."*
   - *"Today you'll practice with area models — drawing fractions and comparing them."*

#### Guided Practice (15 min)

- **Fraction Comparison Recording Sheet**:
  - Section A — Same Denominator (6 problems): Compare and write >, <, or =.
    - 2/5 ___ 4/5
    - 7/8 ___ 3/8
    - 1/6 ___ 5/6
    - 4/4 ___ 3/4
    - 2/3 ___ 2/3
    - 1/8 ___ 6/8
  - Section B — Same Numerator (6 problems):
    - 1/2 ___ 1/4
    - 1/8 ___ 1/3
    - 2/6 ___ 2/4
    - 3/8 ___ 3/4
    - 1/6 ___ 1/2
    - 4/6 ___ 4/8
  - Section C — Area Model Proof (4 problems): Draw area models to prove your answer.
    - Draw and shade to show that 3/4 > 1/4
    - Draw and shade to show that 1/3 > 1/6
    - Draw and shade to show that 2/8 = 1/4 (equivalent!)
    - Draw and shade to show that 5/6 > 5/8

- Students work independently. Fraction strips available for checking.
- Teacher circulates, checking area models for accuracy (are the wholes the same size? are the parts equal?).

#### Independent Practice (10 min)

- **"Fraction War" Card Game** (pairs):
  - Deck of fraction cards (each card shows a fraction).
  - Each player flips a card. Compare fractions. Player with the larger fraction takes both cards.
  - If fractions are equivalent, it's "war" — flip again.
  - Students must explain WHY their fraction is larger before taking the cards.
  - Teacher listens for use of comparison rules during explanations.

#### Closure (5 min)
- **Exit Ticket** (dry-erase boards):
  - *"Write the correct symbol: 3/6 ___ 5/6"* (3/6 < 5/6)
  - *"Write the correct symbol: 1/4 ___ 1/8"* (1/4 > 1/8)
  - *"Explain in ONE sentence why 1/4 is larger than 1/8."* (When you cut something into 4 pieces, each piece is bigger than when you cut it into 8 pieces.)
- Collect comparison recording sheets.
- Preview tomorrow: *"Tomorrow we put all our fraction skills together — naming, placing on number lines, equivalence, AND comparing. And we'll deconstruct a recipe in enrichment!"*

#### Differentiation
- **Group A (Below level)**: Focus on same-denominator comparisons only. Use fraction strips for EVERY comparison — do not require area models yet. Teacher works with this group during guided practice. Use "pizza" language: "Which is more pizza — 2 slices out of 4 or 3 slices out of 4?" Limit denominators to 2, 3, 4.
- **Group B (On level)**: Full progression — same denominator and same numerator comparisons. Transition to area models during Section C. Fraction strips available for checking.
- **Group C (Above level)**: After completing the recording sheet, tackle "tricky comparisons" where neither the numerator nor denominator is the same: 3/4 vs. 2/3. Strategy: use benchmark of 1/2 (is each fraction more or less than 1/2?). Or find equivalent fractions with a common denominator. Preview Week 5 comparison skills.

#### Part 1: OG Lesson — Compound Words & Consonant-le Syllable Type (10:30–11:05)

**Objective**: Students will decode compound words by identifying two known words within them, and will decode consonant-le (C+le) words by identifying the final stable syllable. (IEP Goal: Decoding — multisyllabic word reading using compound word and consonant-le strategies.)

**Materials**:
- OG phonogram cards — cumulative deck
- Red word cards: science, special (today's 2 new red words)
- Compound word puzzle cards (two halves that combine — 15 pairs)
- Consonant-le word cards (printed, one word per card)
- Dry-erase boards + markers
- Syllable type reference chart (updated to include C+le)
- Scissors

**OG Lesson Structure**:

1. **Review — Phonogram Drill + Syllable Division Review (5 min)**:
   - Phonogram drill: rapid-fire through the deck.
   - Flash 3 VCCV words and 3 VCV words from Monday/Tuesday — students read and identify the syllable pattern.
   - Red word review: flash **answer, listen, often, island**. Students read chorally.

2. **Red Words — Introduce "science" and "special" (5 min)**:
   - **science**: *"This word has a tricky spelling. The 'sc' makes the /s/ sound, and the 'ence' sounds like 'ents.' SY-ents."* Students say, spell (s-c-i-e-n-c-e), write in sand tray, write on board. Underline tricky part (sc- and -ence).
   - **special**: *"The 'ci' in this word makes the /sh/ sound. SPEH-shul."* Students say, spell (s-p-e-c-i-a-l), write. Underline tricky part (cial).
   - Practice sentence: *"Write: 'The science class was special.' Circle the two red words."*

3. **New Concept A — Compound Words (8 min)**:
   - Write on chart paper: **sunflower**
   - *"This is a LONG word. But watch what happens when I look for two words I already know hiding inside."*
   - Draw a line: **sun | flower**. *"Sun — I know that word. Flower — I know that word. Sun-flower!"*
   - *"A compound word is two whole words stuck together to make a new word. When you see a long word, ask: 'Are there two words I know hiding in there?'"*
   - **Compound Word Puzzle Activity**: Distribute puzzle card halves. Each student has a word. They must find their "match" — the person whose word combines with theirs to make a compound word.
     - Pairs: sun/flower, back/pack, cup/cake, rain/bow, bed/room, foot/ball, air/plane, fire/truck, snow/flake, pop/corn, play/ground, pan/cake, door/bell, base/ball, book/shelf
   - Once matched, students write the compound word on their dry-erase boards, draw the dividing line, and read it aloud.

4. **New Concept B — Consonant-le Syllable Type (8 min)**:
   - Write on chart paper: **table**
   - *"This word ends in -le. When you see a consonant followed by -le at the end of a word, that's a syllable type called consonant-le, or C+le."*
   - **C+le Rule**: Count back 3 letters from the end. That's where you divide.
   - Model: **t-a-b-l-e** → count back 3 from end: b-l-e → divide before the b: **ta | ble**
   - *"The last syllable is '-ble.' What about the first syllable? 'ta' — that's an open syllable. Long a. TAY-ble. Table!"*
   - Model more:
     - **simple**: count back 3 → p-l-e → divide: **sim | ple** (sim = closed, short i; ple = C+le)
     - **puzzle**: → **puz | zle** (puz = closed, short u; zle = C+le)
     - **candle**: → **can | dle** (can = closed, short a; dle = C+le)
     - **gentle**: → **gen | tle** (gen = closed, short e; tle = C+le)
   - Post on syllable type chart: **Consonant-le (C+le)**: the consonant + le at the end of a word; count back 3 to divide.

5. **Guided Practice — C+le Cut and Read (5 min)**:
   - Distribute C+le word cards: **bubble, maple, title, little, handle, single, tumble, circle, purple, wiggle**
   - Students: underline the -le ending, count back 3, draw the line, cut the card at the divide, read each syllable, blend.
   - Pairs check each other's divisions.

6. **Dictation (4 min)**:
   - Dry-erase boards. Words: **backpack** (compound), **table** (C+le), **sunset** (compound + VCCV), **simple** (C+le), **pancake** (compound).
   - Sentence: **"The simple puzzle was on the table."**

**Data Collection**:
- Track compound word identification accuracy (out of 15 matched pairs)
- Track C+le word reading accuracy (out of 10 cut-and-read words)
- Red word accuracy: science, special
- Cumulative: all 6 red words of the week

#### Part 2: Informational Writing Introduction (11:05–11:30)

**Objective**: Students will identify the features of an informational paragraph and begin planning a "How ___ Works" piece connected to the enrichment theme. (IEP Goal: Written Expression — informational writing with topic sentence, supporting details, and closing.)

**Materials**:
- Model informational paragraph: "How a Plant Makes Food" (pre-written on chart paper)
- "How ___ Works" planning graphic organizer (1 per student)
- Reading response journals
- Colored pencils for labeling text features

**Lesson**:

1. **Model Text Analysis (8 min)**:
   - Display the model paragraph on chart paper:

   > **How a Plant Makes Food**
   >
   > Plants make their own food through a process called photosynthesis. First, the roots absorb water from the soil. Next, the leaves take in sunlight and carbon dioxide from the air. Then, inside the leaf, the plant uses the sunlight to turn the water and carbon dioxide into sugar — that's the plant's food! Finally, the plant releases oxygen into the air, which is what we breathe. Every part of the plant has a job in this process.

   - Read aloud. Then analyze together:
     - *"What's the topic sentence?"* (Plants make their own food through a process called photosynthesis.)
     - *"What transition words do you see?"* (First, Next, Then, Finally.) Circle them.
     - *"What's the closing sentence?"* (Every part of the plant has a job in this process.)
     - *"Notice: this paragraph explains HOW something works, step by step."*
   - Post **Informational Paragraph Structure**:
     - Topic sentence (tells the reader what you'll explain)
     - Steps or details (in order, using transition words)
     - Closing sentence (wraps it up)

2. **Planning "How ___ Works" (10 min)**:
   - *"You're going to write your own 'How ___ Works' paragraph. It can be about ANYTHING — how a plant grows, how a recipe is made, how a video game works, how a bike works, how a friendship starts."*
   - Distribute planning graphic organizer:
     - Topic: How ___ works
     - Step 1 (First...):
     - Step 2 (Next...):
     - Step 3 (Then...):
     - Step 4 (Finally...):
     - Closing sentence:
   - Students brainstorm and fill in the planner. They do NOT need to write the paragraph yet — that's Thursday.
   - Teacher circulates, helping students choose topics and organize their steps.
   - *"Tomorrow in enrichment, we'll deconstruct a recipe. That might give you ideas too!"*

3. **Inference Quick Practice (7 min)**:
   - Read a short paragraph aloud (3–4 sentences about electronics):
     > "The old clock had been sitting in the attic for thirty years. When Maya opened the back, she found a thick layer of dust on the gears. Some of the tiny screws had turned orange. She could see where a wire had snapped in two."
   - *"What can you infer about the condition of the clock?"* Students use the "I read... I know... So I think..." framework on sticky notes.
   - 2–3 students share. Validate any inference supported by text evidence.
   - *"Great inference practice — this connects perfectly to what we'll do in enrichment today. We're taking apart old electronics!"*

**Data Collection**:
- Collect "How ___ Works" planning graphic organizers (formative — check for clear topic, logical steps, use of transition words)
- Inference sticky notes (check for correct use of framework)
- Note who chose topics independently vs. who needed significant support

**Differentiation**:
- **Below level**: Provide a partially completed graphic organizer with a topic already chosen (e.g., "How to Make a Sandwich"). Student fills in the steps. Sentence starters provided for each step.
- **On level**: Choose own topic, complete the graphic organizer with 4 steps and transition words.
- **Above level**: Complete the graphic organizer AND begin drafting the full paragraph. Include at least one "expert vocabulary word" related to their topic.

### Exploration & Enrichment (12:00–1:00)

**Activity**: Safe Electronics Disassembly — STEM

**Enrichment Category**: STEM + Technology (Wednesday theme)

**Overview**: Students safely take apart old, non-functioning electronics (keyboards, calculators, alarm clocks) to discover the components inside. They identify parts, hypothesize about function, and practice observation and inference skills. Connects to fractions (parts of a whole device), informational writing (describing how something works), and the "Break It Down" theme.

**Materials**:
- Old electronics for disassembly (collected in advance — 2–3 items per table):
  - Computer keyboards (unplugged, no cord)
  - Calculators (battery removed)
  - Alarm clocks (battery removed)
  - Old remote controls (battery removed)
  - Broken or donated small appliances (teacher pre-screened for safety)
- Screwdrivers (Phillips and flathead, 2 per table)
- Safety goggles (1 per student) — **REQUIRED**
- Magnifying glasses
- Plastic trays or paper plates (to hold small parts)
- "Electronics Disassembly Report" recording sheet (sketch of device, labeled parts, observations, "How I Think It Works" paragraph space)
- Chart paper for group vocabulary list
- Ziplock bags (for storing parts at end)

**Safety Rules (POST ON BOARD)**:
1. Safety goggles ON at all times during disassembly
2. Never force anything — if it won't come apart easily, ask the teacher
3. Keep small parts on your tray — nothing goes in mouths
4. No cutting wires — just observe
5. Wash hands when finished

**Schedule**:

1. **Introduction & Safety Review (8 min)**:
   - *"Today we're engineers. Your job is to take apart an old electronic device, discover what's inside, and figure out how it works."*
   - Show an example: teacher opens a calculator, removes the circuit board, shows the buttons, the screen, the battery compartment.
   - Name the parts as you go: **circuit board** (the brain), **wires** (carry electricity), **buttons/keys** (input), **screen/display** (output), **battery compartment** (power source).
   - Post vocabulary on chart paper.
   - Review safety rules. Students put on safety goggles.

2. **Disassembly Exploration (25 min)**:
   - Each table receives 2–3 devices. Students work in pairs.
   - Step 1: Observe the outside. Sketch the device on the recording sheet. Label any visible parts.
   - Step 2: Begin disassembly. Remove screws, open cases, separate layers. Place each component on the tray.
   - Step 3: Examine components with magnifying glass. Try to identify: circuit board, wires, buttons, speakers, screen.
   - Step 4: On the recording sheet, sketch the inside and label as many parts as possible.
   - Teacher circulates with prompting questions:
     - *"What do you think this part does? What makes you think so?"* (Inference!)
     - *"How many separate parts have you found? If the whole device is 1, and you've found 8 parts, what fraction of the device is the circuit board?"* (Fraction connection!)
     - *"If you were going to explain how this device works to a kindergartner, what would you say?"* (Informational writing connection!)
     - *"What's the smallest part you can find? The largest?"*
   - Math extension: *"Count all the keys on the keyboard. If 10 keys are letters, what fraction of the keys are letters?"*

3. **Recording & Reflection (12 min)**:
   - Students complete the "How I Think It Works" section of their recording sheet:
     - *"Write 3–4 sentences explaining how you think your device works. Use transition words (first, next, then, finally)."*
   - This directly practices the informational writing structure taught during the writing block.
   - Share: each table presents their device and one interesting discovery to the class.
   - Teacher records observations on chart paper: "Amazing Things We Found Inside Electronics."

4. **Clean-Up & Closing Circle (15 min)**:
   - Students place all parts in ziplock bags (labeled with device name) for possible future use.
   - Goggles off, hands washed.
   - Closing circle:
     - *"What surprised you most about what's inside electronics?"*
     - *"How was taking apart an electronic device similar to dividing a word into syllables?"* (You find the parts, you look at each one, and then you understand the whole thing better.)
     - *"How was it similar to fractions?"* (The device is a whole, and each part is a fraction of it.)
   - Self-monitoring checklist.
   - Preview tomorrow: *"Tomorrow we take apart a recipe — not just the ingredients, but what each ingredient DOES. And in math, we put ALL our fraction skills together."*
   - Dismissal at 1:00.

**Academic Connection**:
- **Reading/Comprehension**: Inference (what does this part do?), technical vocabulary
- **Math**: Fractions as parts of a whole device, counting and comparing
- **Writing**: Informational paragraph structure (how the device works)
- **Science/STEM**: Engineering, technology, systems thinking
