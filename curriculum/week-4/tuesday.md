### Math Block (9:20–10:20)

**Objective**: Students will place unit fractions on a number line from 0 to 1, and discover equivalent fractions by comparing fraction strips laid side by side. (IEP Goal: Math Concepts — fraction identification on a number line; equivalent fractions with models.)

**Materials**:
- Fraction strips (same sets from Monday) — 1 per student
- Fraction number line mats (laminated, 0 to 1, with tick marks for halves, thirds, fourths, sixths, eighths) — 1 per student
- Blank number lines (0 to 1, no tick marks) — for students to create their own
- Dry-erase boards + markers
- Equivalent Fraction Exploration recording sheet (1 per student)
- Chart paper + marker
- Tape or magnets for displaying fraction strips on the board

#### Warm-Up/Activation (5 min)
- **Fraction Flashback**: Hold up fraction strip pieces one at a time. Students write the fraction name on dry-erase boards.
  - 1 piece from the halves strip → 1/2
  - 1 piece from the fourths strip → 1/4
  - 1 piece from the eighths strip → 1/8
  - 1 piece from the thirds strip → 1/3
- *"Yesterday you learned to name fractions. Today we're going to put them in order on a number line — and discover something amazing about fractions that look different but are actually the same size."*

#### Direct Instruction (15 min) — CRA Level: Concrete → Representational

1. **Fractions on a Number Line (8 min)**:
   - Draw a number line on chart paper: 0 on the left, 1 on the right.
   - *"This number line goes from 0 to 1. Zero means none of the whole. One means all of the whole. Fractions live BETWEEN 0 and 1."*
   - *"Let's put halves on first."* Divide the line into 2 equal sections. Mark the midpoint. *"That's 1/2 — exactly in the middle. It's halfway between 0 and 1."*
   - Now draw a second number line. Divide into 4 equal sections. Label: 0, 1/4, 2/4, 3/4, 4/4.
   - *"Where is 1/4?"* (One jump from 0.) *"Where is 3/4?"* (Three jumps from 0.)
   - *"What do you notice about 4/4?"* (It's the same as 1 — the whole thing!)
   - *"What do you notice about 2/4?"* Point to where 1/2 is on the first number line. *"2/4 is in the SAME spot as 1/2. They're at the same point on the number line. Hmm... we'll come back to that."*

2. **Equivalent Fractions Discovery (7 min)**:
   - *"Take out your fraction strips. Line up the 1 whole strip at the top of your desk."*
   - *"Now place 1 half strip below it. How much of the whole does it cover?"* (Half.)
   - *"Now take 2 fourth strips and place them below the half. Do they cover the same amount?"* (Yes!)
   - *"So 1/2 and 2/4 cover the SAME amount. We call these equivalent fractions — they're equal."*
   - Write on board: **1/2 = 2/4**
   - *"Can you find another fraction that equals 1/2? Try with your sixths... Try with your eighths..."*
   - Students discover: 1/2 = 2/4 = 3/6 = 4/8
   - *"WOW. Four different fractions that all mean the same amount. They're equivalent."*
   - Post on anchor chart: **Equivalent Fractions = fractions that name the same amount**

#### Guided Practice (15 min)

- **Equivalent Fraction Exploration Recording Sheet**:
  - Section A: Place fractions on a number line (pre-printed number line, 0 to 1, with tick marks).
    - Place 1/2, 1/4, 3/4, 1/3, 2/3 on the number line. Use fraction strips to verify placement.
  - Section B: Find equivalent fractions using strips.
    - 1/2 = ___/4 = ___/6 = ___/8
    - 1/3 = ___/6
    - 2/4 = ___/2
    - 2/6 = ___/3
  - Section C: True or False (use strips to check):
    - 1/2 = 3/6 (True)
    - 1/4 = 2/6 (False)
    - 2/3 = 4/6 (True)
    - 1/3 = 2/8 (False)

- Students work with a partner. Both must verify with strips before writing the answer.
- Teacher circulates with key questions:
  - *"How do you know 2/4 equals 1/2? Show me with your strips."*
  - *"If 1/3 equals 2/6, what do you notice about the numbers?"* (2 is double 1; 6 is double 3.)

#### Independent Practice (10 min)

- **"Fraction Number Line Challenge"**: Students create their own number line from 0 to 1 on a blank strip of paper.
  - Divide it into eighths (fold the paper in half 3 times, then unfold and mark).
  - Label all eighths: 0/8, 1/8, 2/8, 3/8, 4/8, 5/8, 6/8, 7/8, 8/8.
  - Then mark where 1/2 and 1/4 and 3/4 would go. Circle the equivalent fractions.
- While students work, teacher pulls Group A students for small-group re-teaching with fraction circles.

#### Closure (5 min)
- **Exit Ticket** (dry-erase boards):
  - *"Place 3/4 on this number line."* (Teacher draws 0 to 1 on the board; students draw on their boards.)
  - *"Name a fraction equivalent to 1/2."* (Accept: 2/4, 3/6, 4/8.)
  - *"Which is larger: 1/3 or 1/6? How do you know?"* (1/3 — fewer pieces means each piece is bigger.)
- Collect recording sheets and number lines for portfolio.
- Preview tomorrow: *"Tomorrow we'll compare fractions and figure out which is bigger — even when the denominators are different. And in our enrichment time, we'll take apart old electronics!"*

#### Differentiation
- **Group A (Below level)**: Focus on halves and fourths on the number line. Equivalence limited to 1/2 = 2/4. Fraction strips remain on desk at all times. Teacher works with this group during independent practice using fraction circles and the "fair sharing" context (2 kids sharing = halves, 4 kids sharing = fourths).
- **Group B (On level)**: Full progression as described — number line placement, equivalent fraction discovery with strips through eighths. Partner work on recording sheet.
- **Group C (Above level)**: After completing the recording sheet, explore: *"Can you find ALL the fractions equivalent to 1/3 using your strips?"* Create a "Fraction Families" chart grouping equivalent fractions. Begin noticing the multiplication pattern (1/2 = 2/4 because 1×2=2, 2×2=4).

#### Part 1: OG Lesson — VCV Syllable Division (10:30–11:05)

**Objective**: Students will apply the VCV syllable division strategy (try open syllable first; if the word doesn't sound right, try closed) to decode multisyllabic words. (IEP Goal: Decoding — multisyllabic word reading using syllable division.)

**Materials**:
- OG phonogram cards — cumulative deck
- Red word cards: often, island (today's 2 new red words)
- VCV word cards (printed on cardstock, one word per card)
- Dry-erase boards + markers
- Syllable type reference chart
- "Open or Closed?" sorting mat (two columns)

**OG Lesson Structure**:

1. **Review — Phonogram Drill + VCCV Quick Check (5 min)**:
   - Phonogram drill: rapid-fire through the deck (30 seconds).
   - VCCV review: flash 5 words from yesterday — **picnic, garden, blanket, muffin, cactus**. Students read chorally.
   - Red word review: **answer, listen**. Students spell on dry-erase boards.

2. **Red Words — Introduce "often" and "island" (5 min)**:
   - **often**: *"The 't' is silent in this word, just like in 'listen.' OF-en. Some people say OFF-ten, but the standard pronunciation is OF-en."* Students say, spell, write in sand tray, write on board. Underline silent t.
   - **island**: *"This one is really tricky. The 's' is silent. EYE-land. It looks like it should say 'is-land,' but it's 'island.'"* Students say, spell, write. Underline silent s.
   - Practice sentence: *"Write: 'We often visit the island.' Circle the two red words."*

3. **New Concept — VCV Syllable Division (12 min)**:
   - Write on chart paper: **open**
   - *"Yesterday we learned VCCV — two consonants between two vowels. Today: what if there's only ONE consonant between two vowels? That's called VCV."*
   - Underline vowels: **o** and **e**. Circle the single consonant between them: **p**.
   - *"With one consonant, I have a choice. Do I put the consonant with the first syllable or the second?"*
   - **Strategy — "Try it two ways"**:
     - **First try**: Divide BEFORE the consonant (open syllable). o | pen. The first syllable is open, so the vowel says its long sound: /ō/ - pen. "Oh-pen." *"Does that sound like a real word? YES! Open!"*
     - **Second try** (if first doesn't work): Divide AFTER the consonant (closed syllable). op | en. The first syllable is closed, so the vowel is short: /ŏp/ - en.
   - *"We always try the open syllable first — long vowel sound. If it doesn't sound right, we try closing it — short vowel sound."*
   - Model with **river**: ri | ver (try open: /rī/-ver... "ryver"? That's not a word.) → riv | er (closed: /rĭv/-er. "River!" That's it!)
   - Model with **moment**: mo | ment (try open: /mō/-ment. "Moment!" Yes!)

   - Post the **VCV Rule**:
     - Find the single consonant between two vowels
     - Try 1: Divide BEFORE the consonant → open syllable (long vowel)
     - Try 2: If it doesn't sound right, divide AFTER → closed syllable (short vowel)
     - *"Be a flexible reader — try it both ways!"*

4. **Guided Practice — "Open or Closed?" Sort (8 min)**:
   - Distribute VCV word cards:
     - **Open first syllable (V|CV)**: open, paper, tiger, music, moment, frozen, hotel, motor
     - **Closed first syllable (VC|V)**: river, visit, cabin, lemon, comic, magic, seven, melon
   - Students work in pairs with sorting mat:
     1. Underline vowels, circle the single consonant
     2. Try open first (divide before consonant)
     3. If it sounds like a real word → place in "Open" column
     4. If not → try closed (divide after consonant) → place in "Closed" column
   - Teacher circulates. Key correction: *"You tried /tī/-ger and got 'tiger.' Does that sound right? Yes! So where does it go?"*

5. **Dictation (5 min)**:
   - Dry-erase boards.
   - Dictation words: **open, cabin, paper, visit, moment**
   - Students write each word with a dividing line showing the syllable break.
   - Sentence dictation: **"We often open the cabin."** (Includes red word + VCV words.)

**Data Collection**:
- Track per student: VCV word sort accuracy (how many correctly sorted into open vs. closed)
- Note who grasped the "try it two ways" strategy vs. who only tried one way
- Track red word accuracy: answer, listen, often, island (cumulative)

#### Part 2: Inference with Text & Writing (11:05–11:30)

**Objective**: Students will make inferences from a short informational passage using the "I read... I know... So I think..." framework and identify text features. (IEP Goal: Comprehension — inference with text evidence; informational text features.)

**Materials**:
- Short informational passage: "How Museums Keep Old Things Safe" (4–5 paragraphs, at instructional level, with a heading, a caption under an image description, and 2 bold vocabulary words)
- Inference graphic organizer (3 columns: "I read..." / "I know..." / "So I think...")
- Sticky notes (2 per student)
- Reading response journals
- "Show, Don't Tell" revision checklist

**Lesson**:

1. **Text Features Preview (5 min)**:
   - Display the passage. Before reading, explore the text features:
     - *"What do you notice about this text before we even read it?"*
     - Point out: **heading** (tells what the section is about), **bold words** (important vocabulary), **caption** (explains the picture/diagram).
     - *"Informational texts are organized with features that help you find information quickly. These are like signposts."*
   - Post on anchor chart: **Informational Text Features** — heading, caption, bold words, diagram, glossary.

2. **Shared Reading with Inference Stops (10 min)**:
   - Read the passage aloud together (teacher reads, students follow along).
   - Paragraph 1: General statement about museums preserving old things.
   - Paragraph 2: Describes how conservators wear gloves and use special tools. Stop here.
     - *"The text says conservators wear gloves when handling artifacts. It doesn't say WHY. Can you make an inference?"*
     - Model: *"I read that they wear gloves. I know that oils from our hands can damage delicate surfaces. So I think they wear gloves to protect the artifacts from damage."*
   - Paragraph 3: Describes how some paintings are cleaned very slowly, one tiny section at a time. Stop.
     - Students turn-and-talk: use the framework. Then share.
     - *"I read they clean one tiny section at a time. I know paintings are delicate and could be ruined. So I think they go slowly to avoid making a mistake."*
   - Students write one inference on their graphic organizer using the framework.

3. **Writing Connection — Narrative Middle Continued (10 min)**:
   - Students continue writing the middle of their narrative (from yesterday).
   - Today's focus: add at least one "show, don't tell" sentence. Use the revision checklist.
   - Post 3 examples:
     - Tell: "He was happy." → Show: "A grin spread across his face and he pumped his fist in the air."
     - Tell: "The room was messy." → Show: "Clothes covered every inch of the floor and towers of books leaned against the walls."
     - Tell: "It was cold." → Show: "Her breath came out in white puffs and she shoved her hands deep into her pockets."
   - Students underline their "show, don't tell" sentence with a colored pencil so teacher can find it easily.
   - Teacher circulates, conferring with 3–4 students about their narratives.

**Data Collection**:
- Collect inference graphic organizers — score: did the student correctly identify a text clue, connect it to background knowledge, and state a reasonable inference?
- Check narrative drafts: progress on the middle section, presence of descriptive detail
- Note who could identify text features (heading, caption, bold words) independently

**Differentiation**:
- **Below level**: Provide the inference graphic organizer pre-filled with the "I read..." column. Student only needs to complete "I know..." and "So I think..." Teacher reads the passage aloud to this group while others read with a partner.
- **On level**: Complete inference graphic organizer independently after shared reading. Continue narrative writing with "show, don't tell" revision checklist.
- **Above level**: After the shared reading, read a second short passage independently and complete 2 inference graphic organizers. In writing, add both a "show, don't tell" detail AND a line of dialogue.

### Exploration & Enrichment (12:00–1:00)

**Activity**: How Museums Preserve Artifacts — Conservation Science

**Enrichment Category**: Cultural + Science Connection (Tuesday theme)

**Overview**: Students explore how museum conservators carefully take apart, clean, and restore fragile objects. They practice conservation techniques on "artifacts" (teacher-prepared items: old coins, weathered papers, objects coated in dirt). Connects to inference (figuring out what an object is and how it was used) and fractions (measuring and dividing materials carefully).

**Materials**:
- Images/short video of museum conservation (Metropolitan Museum of Art conservation lab videos on YouTube — 3–5 minutes, appropriate for age)
- "Artifacts" for conservation practice: old coins (or play coins rubbed with dirt), weathered papers (tea-stained paper with writing), small objects partially wrapped in masking tape or tissue paper
- Soft brushes (paintbrushes or toothbrushes, 1 per student)
- Magnifying glasses
- Cotton swabs
- Small dishes of water
- Paper towels
- "Conservation Report" recording sheet: sketch of artifact before, sketch after, observations, inference about the object
- Museum label cards (blank, for students to create exhibit labels)

**Schedule**:

1. **Introduction & Video (10 min)**:
   - *"Yesterday we took apart a plant to understand it. Today, we're going to learn how museums take care of very old, very fragile things — without breaking them."*
   - Show 3–5 minute video of conservators at work (cleaning a painting, restoring a ceramic, preserving a document).
   - Discussion: *"What did you notice about how carefully they worked? Why can't they just scrub it clean?"*
   - *"Conservators are like detectives AND doctors. They figure out what the object is, what's wrong with it, and how to fix it — without making it worse."*

2. **Conservation Station Activity (25 min)**:
   - Set up 3 stations around the room (students rotate every 8 minutes):
   - **Station 1 — Coin Cleaning**: Students receive dirty coins. Using soft brushes and damp cotton swabs, carefully clean the surface. Use magnifying glass to observe details revealed. Sketch the coin before and after. Write: *"I think this coin is from ___ because I see ___."* (Inference practice.)
   - **Station 2 — Document Preservation**: Students receive "aged" papers (tea-stained, crumpled). Carefully flatten, examine text or drawings. Use magnifying glass to read any writing. Record observations. Write: *"I think this document was used for ___ because ___."*
   - **Station 3 — Object Unwrapping**: Students carefully unwrap a small object from tissue paper layers. Examine. Create a museum label card: Name of Object, What I Think It Is, Where It Might Be From, What It Was Used For.
   - Teacher circulates. At each station, prompt:
     - *"What clues are you using to figure out what this is?"*
     - *"How is this like making an inference in reading?"*
     - *"If the conservator only cleaned 1/4 of the painting in one day, how long would it take to clean the whole thing?"* (Fraction connection.)

3. **Museum Labels & Mini-Exhibit (10 min)**:
   - Each student completes a museum label card for one of their artifacts.
   - Label format:
     - Title: [Name of Object]
     - Description: 2 sentences about what it is and how they know
     - "Conserved by: [Student Name], Junior Conservator"
   - Arrange artifacts and labels on a table as a "mini-museum exhibit."
   - Gallery walk: students view each other's exhibits. Leave a sticky-note compliment or question on one exhibit.

4. **Closing Circle (15 min)**:
   - *"How is conservation like what we do in reading?"* (We look for clues, use what we know, figure things out — just like inference!)
   - *"How does breaking things down carefully help us learn?"*
   - Each student shares one favorite discovery from conservation stations.
   - Self-monitoring checklist: students fill in for the day.
   - Preview tomorrow: *"Tomorrow we learn about compound words — two words stuck together — and we'll take apart old electronics in our enrichment time. Bring your curiosity!"*
   - Dismissal at 1:00.

**Academic Connection**:
- **Reading/Comprehension**: Inference practice (what is this object? how was it used?), informational text features (museum labels)
- **Math**: Fractions in context (conservator cleaned 1/4 of the painting)
- **Writing**: Descriptive writing for museum labels, observation skills
