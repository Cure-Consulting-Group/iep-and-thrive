# Week 4 — "Breaking It Down"
## Theme: Taking Things Apart to Understand Them

**Dates**: July 28 – August 1, 2026
**Focus**: Syllable division strategies, multisyllabic word decoding, inference, fractions (Part 1), descriptive writing

---

## Weekly Learning Objectives

### Reading & Phonics (OG)
- Teach syllable division strategy for VCCV words (divide between two consonants): rab-bit, nap-kin, bas-ket, pic-nic, gar-den
- Teach syllable division strategy for VCV words (try dividing before the consonant first — open syllable; if the word doesn't sound right, divide after — closed syllable): o-pen, mo-ment, ri-ver, vis-it, cab-in
- Introduce compound words as a decoding strategy: breaking apart into two known words (sun-flower, back-pack, cup-cake)
- Introduce consonant-le (C+le) syllable type: ta-ble, sim-ple, puz-zle, can-dle, gen-tle
- Teach the "chunk it" approach: identify syllable types within multisyllabic words, decode each chunk, blend together
- Multisensory practice: arm tapping for syllables, scooping syllables on dry-erase boards, cutting apart word cards at division points
- OG dictation includes multisyllabic words and review patterns from Weeks 1–3
- Red words for the week: answer, listen, often, island, science, special
- Decodable text practice with multisyllabic words embedded

### Comprehension
- Teach inference using the "I read... I know... So I think..." framework
- Model making inferences with picture books first (what can we figure out that isn't directly stated?)
- Practice finding text evidence to support answers — teach "Go back to the text" as a habit
- Introduce asking and answering questions about text (who, what, where, when, why, how — and which answers require inference?)
- Begin informational text features: headings, captions, diagrams, bold words, glossaries
- Reading response journal: "What I figured out that the author didn't directly say"

### Writing
- Continue narrative writing: drafting the middle and end of the story begun in Week 3
- Teach "show, don't tell" with descriptive detail: instead of "She was scared," write "Her hands shook and her heart pounded"
- Practice adding sensory details (what did the character see, hear, feel, smell?)
- Introduce informational writing: "How ___ Works" paragraph (connects to enrichment theme)
- Thursday: model an informational paragraph about how something is taken apart or how it works
- Friday: students choose — revise narrative story OR draft a "How ___ Works" piece (portfolio piece)

### Math
- Introduce fractions using concrete manipulatives: fraction strips and fraction circles
- Teach unit fractions (1/2, 1/3, 1/4, 1/6, 1/8) — fractions where the numerator is 1
- Name and represent fractions: numerator (number of parts we have) / denominator (total equal parts)
- Place fractions on a number line (0 to 1)
- Introduce equivalent fractions using manipulatives: show that 1/2 = 2/4 = 3/6 using fraction strips laid side by side
- Compare fractions with same denominator (3/8 vs. 5/8 — more pieces of the same size) and same numerator (1/3 vs. 1/6 — same number of pieces but different sizes)
- CRA progression: Monday–Tuesday concrete (fraction strips, fraction circles, pattern blocks), Wednesday representational (area models, number line diagrams), Thursday–Friday abstract (fraction notation, comparison symbols)
- Connect to theme: fractions are about taking a whole and breaking it into equal parts

### Executive Function & SEL
- Teach "breaking it down" as a universal strategy: big words → syllables; big problems → steps; big feelings → parts
- Practice the strategy explicitly: "This feels overwhelming. Let me break it down."
- Self-monitoring: students begin tracking their own progress on a simple graph (ORF score, math facts correct)
- SEL focus: persistence and frustration tolerance — multisyllabic words and fractions are hard; normalize productive struggle
- Introduce "ask for help" protocol: try it yourself, try a strategy, ask a peer, ask the teacher

## Differentiation Notes

### Flexible Grouping (Adjusted Based on Mid-Cycle Check #2)

**Phonics Groups**:
- Group A: Focus on VCCV syllable division and compound words — these are the most transparent patterns; limit VCV words to high-frequency examples (open, over, even); C+le introduced with heavy support; continue daily review of vowel teams and r-controlled vowels from Weeks 2–3
- Group B: Full progression through VCCV, VCV, compound words, and C+le; practice the "chunk it" approach with 2-syllable words; daily OG dictation includes multisyllabic words
- Group C: Rapid acquisition of syllable division — add 3-syllable words (wonderful, remember, important); practice syllable division with less common patterns; begin previewing prefix/suffix concepts for Week 5 (e.g., "un-hap-py" — what does "un-" do?)

**Math Groups**:
- Group A: Focus on naming and representing fractions with heavy concrete support; fraction strips stay on the desk all week; unit fractions primarily; introduce comparison with same denominator only; keep connection to fair sharing language ("If 4 people share equally...")
- Group B: Full fraction progression — naming, number line, equivalent fractions with strips, comparing (same numerator and same denominator); transition from concrete to representational by Thursday
- Group C: Move more quickly to abstract fraction notation; equivalent fractions using multiplication relationship (1/2 = 2/4 because we multiply both by 2); compare fractions using benchmark strategy (Is it more or less than 1/2?); preview addition of fractions with like denominators

**Comprehension**: All students practice inference with the same framework. Differentiate by:
- Text complexity: inference questions paired with texts at instructional reading level
- Scaffolding: some students receive the "I read... I know..." sentence frame in writing; others use it orally and write independently
- Text evidence: all students practice going back to the text; some receive highlighted passages, others locate evidence independently

**Writing**: Students are at different stages of narrative writing and informational writing introduction.
- Students who need more time: focus on completing the narrative draft; add 1–2 descriptive details using the "show, don't tell" checklist
- Students on pace: complete narrative draft and begin revising; start a "How ___ Works" informational paragraph
- Advanced: revise narrative with multiple sensory details; write a full "How ___ Works" piece with text features (heading, diagram with labels)
