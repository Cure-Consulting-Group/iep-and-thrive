### Math Block (9:20–10:20)

**Objective**: Students will demonstrate understanding of fractions by completing mixed practice that includes naming fractions, placing them on number lines, identifying equivalent fractions, and comparing fractions — transitioning to abstract notation without concrete manipulatives. (IEP Goal: Math Concepts — fraction identification, equivalence, and comparison.)

**Materials**:
- Fraction strips (available on request, but not automatically distributed — pushing toward abstract)
- Fraction practice worksheet: mixed problems (naming, number line, equivalent, comparing) — 1 per student
- Dry-erase boards + markers
- Chart paper with cumulative fraction anchor chart (vocabulary, rules, strategies)
- Colored pencils
- "Fraction Challenge" extension cards (for Group C)

#### Warm-Up/Activation (5 min)
- **"Fraction Riddles"**:
  - *"I'm a fraction. My denominator is 4. I'm less than 1/2. What could I be?"* (1/4)
  - *"I'm equivalent to 1/2. My denominator is 8. What am I?"* (4/8)
  - *"I have the same numerator as 1/3, but my pieces are bigger. Who am I?"* (1/2 — same numerator, smaller denominator means bigger pieces)
  - *"I'm bigger than 3/8 but smaller than 7/8. My denominator is 8. What are my options?"* (4/8, 5/8, 6/8)
  - Students answer on dry-erase boards.

#### Direct Instruction (10 min) — CRA Level: Representational → Abstract

1. **Review All Fraction Skills on One Anchor Chart (5 min)**:
   - Point to cumulative anchor chart. Review each skill:
     - **Naming**: numerator (top — parts you have) / denominator (bottom — total equal parts)
     - **Number line**: fractions live between 0 and 1; divide the line into equal parts based on the denominator
     - **Equivalent fractions**: fractions that name the same amount (1/2 = 2/4 = 3/6 = 4/8)
     - **Comparing — same denominator**: compare numerators (more pieces = bigger)
     - **Comparing — same numerator**: compare denominators (bigger denominator = smaller pieces = smaller fraction)
   - *"You've learned all of this in 3 days. Today, we practice EVERYTHING — and we push ourselves to work without the strips when we can."*

2. **Modeling Abstract Notation (5 min)**:
   - *"On Monday, you used strips. Tuesday, you drew area models. Today, I want you to TRY working with just the numbers."*
   - Model: *"Compare 3/6 and 5/6. Same denominator — 6. Compare numerators: 3 < 5. So 3/6 < 5/6. I didn't need a picture."*
   - Model: *"Is 2/4 equivalent to 1/2? I know 1/2 = 2/4 because if I multiply 1 by 2, I get 2, and 2 by 2, I get 4. Same fraction, different name."*
   - *"BUT — if you're unsure, you can ALWAYS go back to your strips or draw a picture. That's not going backward. That's being smart."*

#### Guided Practice (15 min)

- **Fraction Practice Worksheet — Mixed Problems**:
  - Section A — Name the Fraction (4 problems): Given an area model, write the fraction. Given a fraction, shade the correct area model.
  - Section B — Number Line (4 problems): Place given fractions on a number line from 0 to 1. Identify a fraction at a marked point on a number line.
  - Section C — Equivalent Fractions (4 problems): Fill in the blank: 1/3 = ___/6, 2/4 = ___/2, ___/8 = 1/2, 2/3 = ___/6. May use strips to verify.
  - Section D — Comparing Fractions (6 problems): Write >, <, or =.
    - 3/4 ___ 1/4 (same denom)
    - 1/6 ___ 1/3 (same numer)
    - 5/8 ___ 7/8 (same denom)
    - 2/4 ___ 1/2 (equivalent)
    - 1/8 ___ 1/2 (same numer)
    - 4/6 ___ 2/6 (same denom)
  - Section E — Word Problems (2 problems):
    - "Maria ate 3/8 of a pizza. James ate 5/8 of the same pizza. Who ate more? How do you know?"
    - "Liam walked 1/4 of a mile. Sophia walked 1/3 of a mile. Who walked farther? Explain using what you know about fractions."

- Students work independently. Teacher circulates with targeted questions:
  - *"Can you solve that without your strips? Try!"*
  - *"How did you know 1/4 was less than 1/3?"*
  - *"That word problem asks you to compare and explain. What's your evidence?"*

#### Independent Practice (15 min)

- **"Fraction Station Rotation"** (3 stations, 5 min each):
  - **Station 1 — Fraction War** (card game from yesterday, continued practice)
  - **Station 2 — Fraction Number Line** (blank number lines — students create and label their own, placing at least 6 fractions)
  - **Station 3 — Equivalent Fraction Match** (memory game: flip two cards, keep them if they're equivalent — 1/2 and 3/6, 2/4 and 1/2, 1/3 and 2/6, etc.)
- Teacher uses this time to pull Group A students for small-group support on any skill area that's shaky before tomorrow's probe.

#### Closure (5 min)
- **Exit Ticket** (written, collected):
  1. Name the fraction shaded: [rectangle divided into 6 parts, 4 shaded] (4/6)
  2. Place 3/4 on a number line from 0 to 1.
  3. Name one fraction equivalent to 1/2. (____)
  4. Compare: 2/3 ___ 2/8 (2/3 > 2/8)
  5. BONUS: "If I eat 2/6 of a pie and my sister eats 1/3, did we eat the same amount? Prove it." (Yes — 2/6 = 1/3)
- Collect exit tickets. Score tonight to identify who needs last-day support before the fraction probe.
- Preview tomorrow: *"Tomorrow is assessment day — Weekly Probe #3 and Mid-Cycle Check #2. You've worked so hard this week. The assessments will show the growth. And in enrichment, we'll create collage art!"*

#### Differentiation
- **Group A (Below level)**: Fraction strips remain on desk for the entire practice worksheet. For Section E word problems, teacher reads aloud and students draw a picture to solve. Limit equivalent fraction problems to 1/2 = 2/4. During station rotation, teacher pulls this group for re-teaching on naming and comparing with same denominator. Provide a fraction reference card (visual of all unit fractions with strip pictures).
- **Group B (On level)**: Complete the full practice worksheet. Fraction strips available on request but encouraged to try without them first. Full station rotation. May use area models (draw) to verify answers.
- **Group C (Above level)**: Complete the practice worksheet without strips. "Fraction Challenge" extension cards:
  - "Is 3/4 greater than or less than 2/3? Prove it." (Use common denominator or benchmark strategy.)
  - "Find a fraction between 1/4 and 3/4." (2/4, or any equivalent.)
  - "If I have 3/4 of a candy bar and you have 5/8, who has more? How do you know?" (Convert to common denominator: 3/4 = 6/8 > 5/8.)
  - Preview: add fractions with like denominators (1/8 + 3/8 = 4/8 = 1/2).

#### Part 1: OG Lesson — "Chunk It" Multisyllabic Word Strategy (10:30–11:05)

**Objective**: Students will apply the "Chunk It" strategy to decode 2- and 3-syllable words by identifying syllable types, dividing, decoding each chunk, and blending. (IEP Goal: Decoding — multisyllabic word reading using combined syllable division strategies with ≥70% accuracy.)

**Materials**:
- OG phonogram cards — cumulative deck
- "Chunk It" strategy poster (pre-made)
- Multisyllabic word list — mixed patterns (printed, 1 per student)
- Dry-erase boards + markers
- Syllable type reference chart (all 6 types)
- Decodable text passage with multisyllabic words embedded
- Highlighters (3 colors)

**OG Lesson Structure**:

1. **Review — Phonogram Drill + Red Words (5 min)**:
   - Rapid phonogram drill (30 seconds).
   - Flash all 6 red words: **answer, listen, often, island, science, special**. Students read, then spell on boards.
   - *"How many can you get right? Let's aim for 6 out of 6."* Track individual accuracy.

2. **New Concept — "Chunk It" Strategy (10 min)**:
   - Post the **"Chunk It" Strategy Poster**:
     1. **Look** at the whole word. Don't panic.
     2. **Find** the vowels. (Underline them.)
     3. **Check** for patterns you know:
        - Compound word? (two words you recognize)
        - VCCV? (two consonants between vowels → divide between)
        - VCV? (one consonant between vowels → try open first, then closed)
        - C+le at the end? (count back 3)
     4. **Divide** the word into syllables.
     5. **Read** each chunk.
     6. **Blend** the chunks together.
     7. **Check**: Does it sound like a real word? Does it make sense?

   - Model with **fantastic**:
     1. Look: "This is a long word. Let me break it down."
     2. Find vowels: underline a, a, i → 3 vowels = probably 3 syllables.
     3. Check: VCCV between first and second vowel (n-t), VCCV between second and third (s-t). Last chunk ends in -tic (closed syllable).
     4. Divide: fan | tas | tic
     5. Read each chunk: /făn/ - /tăs/ - /tĭk/
     6. Blend: fan-tas-tic. Fantastic!
     7. Check: "Yes, that's a real word!"

   - Model with **pineapple**:
     1. Find vowels: i, e, a, (silent e at end with C+le)
     2. Check: compound word? pine + apple? "pine" is a word. "apple" — the -le at the end... is this C+le? a-p-p-l-e → ap | ple. So: pine | ap | ple.
     3. Read: /pīn/ (open/VCe) - /ăp/ - /pəl/. Pineapple!

   - Model with **remember**:
     1. Find vowels: e, e, e → 3 vowels.
     2. Check: VCV between first and second vowel (m) — try open: re | ... then VCCV between second and third (m-b): mem | ber. So: re | mem | ber.
     3. Read: /rē/ - /mĕm/ - /bər/. Remember!

3. **Guided Practice — "Chunk It" Together (10 min)**:
   - Distribute multisyllabic word list. Students work in pairs with dry-erase boards.
   - For each word, they:
     1. Write the word on the board
     2. Underline vowels (one color highlighter)
     3. Identify the pattern and mark the division point (second color)
     4. Read each chunk, then blend
   - Word list (progressive difficulty):
     - 2-syllable: **napkin** (VCCV), **frozen** (VCV-open), **lemon** (VCV-closed), **cupcake** (compound), **candle** (C+le)
     - 2-syllable mixed: **picture** (VCCV), **spider** (VCV-open), **cabin** (VCV-closed), **fireman** (compound), **gentle** (C+le)
     - 3-syllable: **umbrella**, **strawberry**, **basketball**, **remember**, **important**
   - After each word, pairs hold up their boards. Teacher checks divisions and confirms correct reading.
   - Celebrate: *"You just read the word 'important.' That's a 3-syllable word. Two weeks ago, some of you were working on blends. Look at your growth!"*

4. **Connected Text — Decodable Passage (8 min)**:
   - Students read a short passage (teacher-prepared, 6–8 sentences) that includes multisyllabic words from this week:
     > "The sunset turned the garden golden. A gentle rabbit hopped along the garden path, nibbling on a simple dandelion. It was a fantastic moment — the kind you want to remember. I sat on the blanket and listened to the insects humming in the pumpkin patch. Often, the best moments are the quiet ones on this little island of peace."
   - Students read silently first, then aloud with a partner (alternating sentences).
   - Circle any words that required the "Chunk It" strategy. How many multisyllabic words did they find?
   - Comprehension check: *"What is the mood of this passage? How do you know?"* (Peaceful, calm — inference!)

5. **Dictation (2 min)**:
   - Quick dictation on dry-erase boards: **pumpkin** (VCCV), **maple** (C+le), **playground** (compound)
   - Sentence: **"The special science class was fantastic."** (Red words + multisyllabic)

**Data Collection**:
- Track per student: multisyllabic word reading accuracy on the 15-word list (record which patterns were correct/incorrect)
- Note who used the "Chunk It" strategy independently vs. who needed step-by-step prompting
- Connected text fluency: note any students who read the passage fluently vs. those who needed multiple attempts at multisyllabic words
- This data previews tomorrow's formal multisyllabic word reading assessment

#### Part 2: Informational Paragraph Drafting + Narrative Revision (11:05–11:30)

**Objective**: Students will draft an informational "How ___ Works" paragraph using their planning graphic organizer, OR revise their narrative story's middle and end with descriptive detail. (IEP Goal: Written Expression — informational or narrative writing with structure and detail.)

**Materials**:
- "How ___ Works" planning graphic organizers (from Wednesday)
- Lined paper or composition notebooks
- "Show, Don't Tell" revision checklist
- Sensory details word bank
- Informational paragraph model (on chart paper from Wednesday)
- Colored pencils (for revision marks)

**Lesson**:

1. **Mini-Lesson: Modeling an Informational Paragraph (8 min)**:
   - *"Yesterday you planned your 'How ___ Works' piece. Today, let's turn that plan into a paragraph."*
   - Teacher models using a think-aloud. Topic: "How a Cookie Recipe Works."
   - Step 1: Write the topic sentence: *"Making chocolate chip cookies involves several important steps."*
   - Step 2: Use the planning graphic organizer. Turn each step into a sentence with a transition word:
     - *"First, you mix the butter and sugar together until they are smooth and fluffy."*
     - *"Next, you add the eggs and vanilla and stir them in."*
     - *"Then, you fold in the flour, baking soda, and salt to form the dough."*
     - *"Finally, you scoop the dough onto a baking sheet and bake at 350 degrees for 10 minutes."*
   - Step 3: Closing sentence: *"Following these steps carefully is the key to delicious cookies every time."*
   - *"Notice: topic sentence, steps in order with transition words, closing sentence. That's informational writing."*

2. **Student Choice Writing Time (17 min)**:
   - Students choose ONE portfolio piece to work on:
     - **Option A**: Draft the "How ___ Works" informational paragraph using their graphic organizer.
     - **Option B**: Revise their narrative story — complete the middle and end, add descriptive details using the "show, don't tell" checklist and sensory word bank.
   - Teacher circulates for writing conferences (aim for 4–5 students):
     - For informational writers: *"Does your topic sentence tell the reader what you're explaining? Do your steps go in order? Did you use transition words?"*
     - For narrative writers: *"Read me your best 'show, don't tell' sentence. Where could you add what the character heard or felt?"*
   - Students who finish early: peer conference — swap papers with a partner, read, and write one positive comment and one suggestion on a sticky note.

3. **Share Chair (5 min)**:
   - 2 volunteers sit in the "share chair" and read one paragraph of their work aloud.
   - Class gives "warm feedback" only (positive): *"I liked when you said ___" or "I could really picture ___."*
   - *"Tomorrow, you'll choose your best piece for your portfolio. Today was about getting words on the page."*

**Data Collection**:
- Collect all drafts (both informational and narrative) — these are formative writing samples
- Score informational paragraphs for: topic sentence (yes/no), transition words (count), closing sentence (yes/no), logical order (yes/no)
- Score narrative revisions for: "show, don't tell" detail (present/absent), sensory details (count), completion of middle and end (yes/partial/no)

**Differentiation**:
- **Below level**: For informational writing, provide sentence frames: "How ___ works is interesting. First, ___. Next, ___. Then, ___. Finally, ___. That is how ___ works." Student fills in the blanks using their graphic organizer. For narrative, provide a checklist: Did I write what happened next? Did I write how it ended? Did I add one detail about what the character saw or heard?
- **On level**: Draft the full paragraph or revision as described. Use transition words and at least 2 descriptive details.
- **Above level**: For informational writing, add a diagram with labels (visual text feature). For narrative, include a paragraph of dialogue with correct punctuation AND a "show, don't tell" detail for at least 2 emotions.

### Exploration & Enrichment (12:00–1:00)

**Activity**: Deconstructing a Recipe — What Does Each Ingredient Do?

**Enrichment Category**: Life Skills + Science (Thursday theme)

**Overview**: Students take apart a simple recipe (chocolate chip cookies or simple bread) by examining each ingredient individually, hypothesizing about its function, and testing some ingredients' properties. Connects to informational writing (explaining a process), fractions (measuring ingredients), and the "Break It Down" theme.

**Materials**:
- Recipe printed on chart paper (large, visible): Simple Chocolate Chip Cookies
- Individual ingredients in separate labeled containers:
  - Flour, sugar, brown sugar, butter (softened), eggs, vanilla extract, baking soda, salt, chocolate chips
- Measuring cups and spoons (1 set per table)
- Small paper plates (for examining ingredients, 1 per student)
- Magnifying glasses
- "Recipe Deconstruction Report" recording sheet (ingredient name, what it looks like/feels like, what I think it does, actual function)
- Chart paper for group "What Each Ingredient Does" poster
- Wet wipes and paper towels
- Ziplock bags (for any take-home treats if time permits to assemble dough — optional)

**Allergy check**: Confirm no student has allergies to any ingredients before beginning. Have alternatives ready (gluten-free flour, egg substitute, etc.) or make this an observation-only activity for students with severe allergies.

**Schedule**:

1. **Introduction (8 min)**:
   - *"All week we've been taking things apart — plants, museum artifacts, electronics. Today, we're taking apart something you probably eat: cookies!"*
   - Display the recipe on chart paper. Read through it together.
   - *"A recipe is an informational text. Look — it has a title, a list of ingredients, and numbered steps. Sound familiar?"* (Text features!)
   - *"But here's the question: WHY does each ingredient matter? What would happen if we left one out? Today, you're food scientists. Your job is to figure out what each ingredient does."*

2. **Ingredient Examination Stations (20 min)**:
   - Set up 5 stations around the room (students rotate every 4 minutes):
   - **Station 1 — Flour**: Touch it, describe it (soft, powdery, white). *"What do you think flour does in the cookie?"* (Gives structure — it's the main building block.)
   - **Station 2 — Sugar & Brown Sugar**: Compare the two. Touch, taste (small amount on fingertip). *"What does sugar do besides make things sweet?"* (Helps cookies spread, makes them chewy or crispy depending on type.)
   - **Station 3 — Butter & Eggs**: Observe softened butter (what state is it?). Crack an egg into a bowl (observe the parts). *"What do you think butter does? What about the egg?"* (Butter = flavor and texture; egg = holds everything together, like glue.)
   - **Station 4 — Baking Soda + Vinegar Demo**: Teacher-led station. Add a spoonful of baking soda to vinegar and watch the fizz. *"Baking soda is a leavening agent — it makes the cookies rise and puff up. What happened when we mixed it with vinegar? That's a chemical reaction — and something similar happens inside the cookie when it bakes."*
   - **Station 5 — Chocolate Chips & Vanilla**: Smell the vanilla. Observe the chips. *"These are for flavor. But what fraction of the recipe do you think is chocolate chips?"* (Fraction connection!)

   - At each station, students fill in their recording sheet: ingredient name, sensory description, hypothesis about function.
   - **Fraction connection at every station**: Recipes use fractions! Point out: 1/2 cup butter, 3/4 cup sugar, 1/4 teaspoon salt, etc.
     - *"The recipe calls for 3/4 cup of sugar. Show me on this measuring cup where 3/4 is."*
     - *"If I only wanted to make HALF the recipe, how much flour would I need?"* (If the recipe says 2 1/4 cups... that's hard! But for simpler amounts: half of 1 cup = 1/2 cup.)

3. **Group Discussion: What Each Ingredient Does (10 min)**:
   - Return to seats. Teacher reveals the actual function of each ingredient on chart paper:
     - **Flour**: structure (the body of the cookie)
     - **Sugar**: sweetness + texture (crispy edges)
     - **Brown sugar**: moisture + chewiness
     - **Butter**: flavor + tenderness
     - **Eggs**: binding (holds ingredients together)
     - **Vanilla**: flavor enhancer
     - **Baking soda**: leavening (makes it rise)
     - **Salt**: enhances flavor (a tiny amount makes everything taste better)
     - **Chocolate chips**: flavor + texture
   - Compare to student hypotheses. Celebrate correct guesses!
   - Discussion: *"What would happen if you left out the eggs?"* (Cookie falls apart — no binder.) *"What if you doubled the baking soda?"* (Cookie puffs up too much, tastes metallic.)
   - *"Every ingredient has a purpose. Nothing is in the recipe by accident. That's like how every part of a word has a purpose — the syllables, the vowels, the consonants all work together."*

4. **Writing Connection (7 min)**:
   - *"This recipe is actually a perfect 'How ___ Works' informational text. Think about your own 'How ___ Works' paragraph. Does every step have a purpose? Is anything missing?"*
   - Students review their informational drafts from the writing block. Add or revise one detail based on what they learned about clear, purposeful steps.

5. **Closing Circle (15 min)**:
   - Optional: If time and setup allow, each table assembles one small batch of cookie dough to take home in a ziplock (do not bake on-site unless kitchen is available). Follow the recipe together, measuring each ingredient as a group.
   - If no baking: students create a "Recipe for a Great Reader" or "Recipe for a Fraction Expert" — a fun twist using the recipe format:
     - "1 cup of practice, 1/2 cup of patience, 3/4 cup of 'chunk it' strategy..."
   - Self-monitoring checklist for the day.
   - Preview tomorrow: *"Tomorrow is assessment day. We'll do our Weekly Probe #3 and Mid-Cycle Check #2. That means ORF, multisyllabic word reading, a phonics check, a fraction probe, and you'll choose your portfolio writing piece. Tonight, practice your red words and look at your fraction strips at home. You're ready."*
   - **Home practice reminder**: Red words (all 6), read aloud with a family member for 10 minutes (practice chunking big words), ask a family member about fractions during dinner ("What fraction of the pizza did you eat?").
   - Dismissal at 1:00.

**Academic Connection**:
- **Reading/Comprehension**: Informational text structure (recipe as text with features), inference (what does each ingredient do?)
- **Math**: Fractions in measurement (3/4 cup, 1/2 teaspoon), halving recipe amounts
- **Writing**: Informational paragraph revision (clear, purposeful steps)
- **Science**: Chemical reactions (baking soda), states of matter (solid butter vs. melted), observation skills
