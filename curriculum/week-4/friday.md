#### Individual Pulls: ORF + Multisyllabic Word Reading (4–5 min per student)

**Assessment 1: Oral Reading Fluency (ORF) Probe**

**Materials**: 2 ORF passages per student (grade 3 level and grade 4 level), stopwatch, scoring sheet

**Procedure**:
1. Give the student the passage (grade level determined by their reading level — use grade 3 for students reading below grade level, grade 4 for on-level or above).
2. *"I'd like you to read this passage out loud. Try to read it the best you can. If you come to a word you don't know, try your best and then keep going. Ready? Begin."*
3. Start timer when the student reads the first word. Stop at 1 minute.
4. Mark errors: omissions, substitutions, reversals (self-corrections within 3 seconds are NOT errors).
5. Calculate: Words Correct Per Minute (WCPM) = total words read − errors.
6. Record accuracy percentage: (WCPM / total words read) × 100.
7. Note prosody on a 1–4 scale (NAEP rubric):
   - 1 = word-by-word reading
   - 2 = mostly 2-word phrases, little expression
   - 3 = 3-4 word phrases, some expression
   - 4 = smooth, natural phrasing with expression

**Data Recording**:
- WCPM: ___
- Accuracy: ___%
- Prosody: ___/4
- Compare to Week 1 baseline and Week 3 score — plot on student growth chart

#### Fraction Identification Probe (10:30–11:00)

**Objective**: Assess student mastery of fraction concepts taught this week: naming fractions, placing on number lines, identifying equivalent fractions, comparing fractions.

**Materials**: Fraction probe worksheet (1 per student), pencils, fraction strips (available on request for students who need concrete support — note which students request them)

**Probe Format** (10 problems, ~20 minutes + 5 min review/collection):

**Section A — Name the Fraction (3 problems)**:
1. [Area model: rectangle divided into 6 equal parts, 5 shaded] Write the fraction: ___
2. [Area model: circle divided into 4 equal parts, 1 shaded] Write the fraction: ___
3. [Number line from 0 to 1, divided into 8 equal parts, arrow pointing to the 3rd mark] What fraction is the arrow pointing to? ___

**Section B — Equivalent Fractions (2 problems)**:
4. Are these fractions equivalent? 2/4 and 1/2. Circle: YES or NO. Show or explain how you know.
5. Fill in the blank: 1/3 = ___/6

**Section C — Comparing Fractions (3 problems)**:
6. Compare using >, <, or =: 5/8 ___ 3/8
7. Compare using >, <, or =: 1/4 ___ 1/6
8. Compare using >, <, or =: 2/3 ___ 4/6

**Section D — Word Problems (2 problems)**:
9. Jasmine ate 2/8 of a pizza. Marco ate 5/8 of the same pizza. Who ate more pizza? Explain how you know.
10. A gardener planted flowers in 1/3 of her garden and vegetables in 1/4 of her garden. Which section is bigger — the flowers or the vegetables? Use words or a drawing to prove your answer.

**Administration**:
- Read each problem aloud while students follow along on their papers. Give 2 minutes per problem.
- For problems with area models or number lines, ensure students can see the images clearly.
- Students who request fraction strips may use them — note this on the scoring sheet (indicates the student is still at the concrete level).
- Students who finish early: flip paper over and draw their own fraction on the back, then write a comparison question about it.

**Scoring**:
- Each problem worth 1 point (total: 10)
- Record per student: Section A ___/3, Section B ___/2, Section C ___/3, Section D ___/2
- Note: Used concrete manipulatives? Yes / No
- Note: Which skill areas are strong? Which need continued support in Weeks 5–6?

**Materials**:

**Procedure**:

     - What I did well in this piece (check all that apply):

### Exploration & Enrichment (12:00–1:00)

**Activity**: Collage Art — Deconstructing and Reconstructing Images

**Enrichment Category**: Art + Synthesis (Friday theme)

**Overview**: Students create collage art by cutting apart images from magazines and reconstructing them into something new. This is the perfect culmination of the "Taking Things Apart" week — deconstruction as a creative act. Connects to the week's themes: breaking wholes into parts (fractions), breaking words into syllables, and now breaking images into pieces to create new meaning.

**Materials**:
- Old magazines (variety — nature, food, sports, animals, architecture; pre-screened for age-appropriateness)
- Scissors (1 per student)
- Glue sticks (2–3 per table)
- Large construction paper or cardstock (1 piece per student — 12x18 or larger)
- Colored pencils and markers (for adding details to collage)
- Collage example images (posted for inspiration — Romare Bearden, Eric Carle, or other collage artists)
- "Artist Statement" template (half-sheet, 1 per student)

**Schedule**:

1. **Introduction & Inspiration (8 min)**:
   - *"All week we've been taking things apart — plants, electronics, recipes, words, numbers. Today we put things BACK TOGETHER in a new way."*
   - Show examples of collage art:
     - **Romare Bearden**: used cut paper and magazine images to tell stories about community and music
     - **Eric Carle**: (students may know his books!) tore and layered painted paper to create animals and scenes
   - *"Collage artists take apart images and rearrange them to create something completely new. The pieces come from different places, but together they make something meaningful."*
   - *"Your collage will tell a story or show an idea. Maybe it's about your week. Maybe it's about your favorite place. Maybe it's abstract — just colors and shapes that feel right together."*
   - Rules: use scissors carefully, share magazines, keep your workspace tidy.

2. **Collage Creation (25 min)**:
   - Students browse magazines, cutting out images, shapes, colors, textures, and words they like.
   - Phase 1 (10 min): **Deconstruction** — Just cut. Don't glue yet. Collect pieces in a pile.
   - Phase 2 (5 min): **Arrangement** — Lay pieces on the construction paper. Move them around. Try different arrangements. *"Don't glue until you like the way it looks."*
   - Phase 3 (10 min): **Reconstruction** — Glue pieces down. Add details with colored pencils or markers. Fill in gaps with drawn elements.
   - Teacher circulates with prompting connections:
     - *"You're breaking images apart and putting them together in a new way — just like we broke words into syllables and blended them back together."*
     - *"How many pieces are in your collage? If your whole collage has 12 pieces and 3 are from nature magazines, what fraction of your collage is nature?"* (Fraction connection!)
     - *"What story does your collage tell?"*
     - *"What can someone INFER about you from looking at your collage?"* (Inference connection!)

3. **Artist Statements (7 min)**:
   - Each student completes an "Artist Statement" template:
     - Title of my collage: ___
     - My collage is about: ___ (1–2 sentences)
     - I chose these images because: ___ (1–2 sentences)
     - One thing I want people to notice: ___
   - This practices informational/explanatory writing in a low-pressure, creative context.

4. **Gallery Walk & Closing (20 min)**:
   - Tape or prop collages around the room with artist statements below them.
   - **Gallery Walk (8 min)**: Students walk silently, viewing each other's work. Leave a sticky-note compliment on at least 2 collages.
   - **Whole-Group Reflection (7 min)**:
     - *"This week was called 'Taking Things Apart.' Let's look at everything we took apart:"*
       - **Monday**: plants — parts and functions
       - **Tuesday**: museum artifacts — careful observation and conservation
       - **Wednesday**: electronics — components and systems
       - **Thursday**: a recipe — ingredients and their purposes
       - **Friday**: images — and we put them back together into something new
     - *"And in our learning:"*
       - We took apart big words into syllables
       - We took apart whole numbers into fractions
       - We took apart texts to find hidden meanings (inferences)
       - We took apart stories and paragraphs to see how they're built
     - *"What's the big lesson?"* Guide students: *"When we break things down, we understand them better. And then we can put them back together in even better ways."*
   - **Progress Tracking (3 min)**: Students update their personal progress graphs with today's scores (teacher shares ORF score and fraction probe score with each student individually — written on a slip of paper during lunch).
   - *"Look at your graph. See the line going up? That's YOUR growth. That's evidence that your brain is getting stronger."*

5. **Self-Monitoring & Week Wrap-Up (5 min)**:
   - Students complete their self-monitoring checklists for the day.
   - Teacher shares one specific positive observation for each student (pre-written on index cards — hand to each student as they leave):
     - Example: *"[Name], you went from reading 2-syllable words to 3-syllable words this week. That took real persistence. I'm proud of you."*
     - Example: *"[Name], your informational paragraph had clear steps and great transition words. You're becoming a strong writer."*
   - *"Have a wonderful weekend. Practice your red words and read for 10 minutes every night. On Monday, we start Week 5 — 'Real-World Problem Solvers.' We'll learn about prefixes and suffixes, write opinion pieces, and do some real-world math with fractions and measurement. Two more weeks — let's make them count."*
   - Dismissal at 1:00.

**Academic Connection**:
- **Reading/Comprehension**: Inference (what does the collage communicate?), visual literacy
- **Math**: Fractions of a collage (parts of the whole)
- **Writing**: Artist statement (explanatory writing), creative expression
- **Art**: Collage techniques, composition, Romare Bearden and Eric Carle as mentor artists
