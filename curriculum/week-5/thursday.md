### Morning Warm-Up (9:00–9:20)

1. **Feelings Check-In (2 min)**: Quick round — one word for how you feel today.

2. **Morpheme Speed Round (5 min)**:
   - Teacher holds up morpheme cards in rapid succession. Students read the word and state its meaning:
   - un- + kind = unkind (not kind), re- + play = replay (play again), pre- + heat = preheat (heat before), dis- + agree = disagree (not agree), help + -ful = helpful (full of help), care + -less = careless (without care).

   - Arm-tap: sud-den-ly. Write on boards 3 times. Use in a sentence.
   - *"Look — 'suddenly' is a word we already know how to break apart! Sudden + ly. The suffix -ly turns it into an adverb."*

#### Warm-Up/Activation (5 min)
- *"If I want to buy bread ($3.50) and milk ($4.25), how much will I spend?"*
- Students solve on boards: $3.50 + $4.25 = $7.75
- *"If I only have $10, how much change will I get?"* $10.00 - $7.75 = $2.25
- *"If I want 2 boxes of pasta ($1.25 each), how much is that?"* $1.25 x 2 = $2.50

#### Direct Instruction (10 min)

1. **Multi-Step Money Problems (5 min)**:
   - Model a 2-step problem: *"You're making a pasta dinner. You need pasta ($1.25), tomato sauce ($2.00), and cheese ($3.25). How much does your dinner cost?"*
   - Step 1: List the prices. Step 2: Add them up. $1.25 + $2.00 + $3.25 = $6.50.
   - *"You have $10. How much change do you get?"* $10.00 - $6.50 = $3.50.
   - Key strategy: *"Write down each item and its price first. Then add. Then subtract from your budget."*

2. **Budgeting (5 min)**:
   - *"A budget means you decide HOW MUCH to spend BEFORE you shop. If your budget is $15, you need to choose items that add up to $15 or less."*
   - Model: *"My budget is $15. I need bread ($3.50), eggs ($3.75), bananas ($1.50), and chicken ($5.50). Total: $14.25. I'm within budget with $0.75 left."*
   - *"What if I also want cereal ($4.50)? $14.25 + $4.50 = $18.75. That's over budget! What could I swap out?"*

#### Guided Practice (15 min)

- **Budget Challenge**: Students work in pairs.
  - **Task**: Plan a meal for a family of 4 using the price list. Your budget is $20.
  - Choose at least 5 items from the list.
  - Fill in the shopping list template: Item, Price, Quantity, Total Cost.
  - Add up the total. Is it within budget?
  - Calculate change from $20.
  - If over budget, adjust your list.

- Teacher circulates:
  - *"How did you decide which items to include?"*
  - *"What's your running total? Are you on track?"*
  - *"Can you estimate before calculating exactly?"*

- After 10 minutes, each pair shares their meal plan and total cost. Record totals on chart paper.

#### Independent Practice (10 min)

- **"Grocery Math" Practice Page**:
  - Problem 1: *"You buy apples ($4.00), yogurt ($1.00), and bread ($3.50). What's your total?"* ($8.50)
  - Problem 2: *"You have $20. You buy chicken ($5.50), rice ($2.75), and broccoli ($2.50). How much change?"* ($9.25)
  - Problem 3: *"Eggs cost $3.75. You buy 2 dozen. How much?"* ($7.50)
  - Problem 4: *"Your budget is $12. You bought pasta ($1.25), sauce ($2.00), cheese ($3.25), and butter ($2.00). Can you also buy milk ($4.25)? Explain."* ($8.50 + $4.25 = $12.75 — No, over budget by $0.75)
  - Problem 5: *"You spent $16.50 at the store and paid with a $20 bill. What coins and bills could you get as change?"* (Multiple correct answers: $3.50 = one $1, two $1, two quarters, OR...)

#### Closure (5 min)
- **Bar Graph Introduction**: Using the class data from the Budget Challenge (each pair's total meal cost), create a quick bar graph on chart paper.
  - X-axis: Team names. Y-axis: Total cost ($0–$20).
  - *"Which team spent the most? The least? What was the range?"*
  - *"Tomorrow during enrichment, you'll collect more data and make your own bar graphs."*
- Preview: *"Tomorrow is Friday — assessment day AND persuasive poster day!"*

#### Differentiation
- **Below level (Group A)**: Budget of $10 (simpler numbers). Choose only 3 items. Use play money to physically count out each item's cost. Practice page has items A and B only, with picture support and smaller numbers. Bar graph: use pre-drawn axes, just fill in bars.

- **Above level (Group C)**: Budget of $25 with a constraint: *"You must include at least one protein, one vegetable, and one fruit."* Add unit pricing: *"Apples are $4.00 for a bag of 6. How much is one apple?"* Calculate cost per serving for their meal. Create an individual bar graph comparing item costs.

#### Part 1: OG Lesson — Suffixes -ness and -ly + Spelling Rules (10:30–10:50)

**Objective**: Students will identify and define the suffixes -ness (state of being) and -ly (in a ___ way), apply spelling rules (doubling, drop-e, change-y) when adding suffixes, and build word webs showing derived forms of a base word. (IEP Goal: Decoding — apply morphological knowledge and spelling rules to encode words with >=80% accuracy.)

**Materials**:
- Morpheme cards: add suffix cards -ness and -ly (red) to existing set
- Spelling rules anchor chart (from Wednesday — add change-y rule today)
- Word web graphic organizer (1 per student)
- Dry-erase boards + markers
- Colored pencils: blue (prefix), black (base word), red (suffix)

**OG Lesson Structure**:

1. **Cumulative Review (3 min)**:
   - Flash all 4 prefixes and 2 suffixes learned: un-, re-, pre-, dis-, -ful, -less.
   - Quick word build: Teacher says "unhelpful." Students identify: prefix un- (not) + base help + suffix -ful (full of). *"Wait — that seems contradictory! It means 'not full of help' — not helpful."*
   - Flash red words: beautiful, different, important, probably, suddenly (all 5).

2. **New Concept — Suffixes -ness and -ly (8 min)**:
   - Hold up **-ness** (red card). *"-ness means 'the state of being.' It turns an adjective into a noun."*
   - Examples: kind → kindness (the state of being kind), sad → sadness, dark → darkness, happy → happiness, sick → sickness, weak → weakness.
   - Students build 4 words with cards.
   - Hold up **-ly** (red card). *"-ly means 'in a ___ way.' It usually turns an adjective into an adverb — a word that describes HOW something is done."*
   - Examples: kind → kindly (in a kind way), slow → slowly, quick → quickly, sudden → suddenly, careful → carefully, hopeful → hopefully.
   - Students build 4 words with cards.
   - *"Notice: 'carefully' has BOTH a suffix AND a suffix! care + -ful + -ly. Some words have multiple morphemes."*

3. **Change-y Spelling Rule (5 min)**:
   - Add to the spelling rules anchor chart:
   - **Change-y Rule**: *"If a word ends in a consonant + y, change the y to i before adding a suffix (unless the suffix starts with i)."*
     - happy + -ness = happiness (change y to i, add -ness)
     - happy + -ly = happily (change y to i, add -ly)
     - easy + -ly = easily
     - beauty + -ful = beautiful (change y to i, add -ful)
   - *"That's why 'beautiful' is spelled with an I — beauty changes to beauti + ful!"*
   - Practice on boards:
     - lazy + -ness = laziness
     - noisy + -ly = noisily
     - lonely + -ness = loneliness
     - busy + -ly = busily

4. **Word Web Activity (4 min)**:
   - Students complete a word web for the base word **help**:
     - Center: **help**
     - Branches: helpful, helpless, helpfully, helplessly, unhelpful, helper, helping, helpfulness
   - *"From one simple base word, we can build 8 or more words! Morphology is like a word-building superpower."*
   - Students choose one more base word (kind, care, hope, or thank) and create their own word web. Share with a partner.

#### Differentiation
- **Below level (Group A)**: Focus on -ly only today (introduce -ness Friday or through review). Use only base words that don't require spelling changes (slow + ly = slowly, quick + ly = quickly, kind + ly = kindly). Skip change-y rule — teacher handles those words. Word web has 4 branches pre-labeled.
- **On level (Group B)**: Full lesson — both suffixes. Practice change-y rule with 3–4 examples. Complete word web with 6+ branches.
- **Above level (Group C)**: Full lesson plus: identify the part of speech change when adding each suffix. *"-ness turns adjectives into nouns. -ly turns adjectives into adverbs. Can you find examples in your opinion essay?"* Build words with prefix + base + suffix (unkindness, disagreeably).

#### Part 2: Summarizing Informational Text (10:50–11:00)

**Objective**: Students will summarize an informational text using the framework: Topic + Key Details + So What? (IEP Goal: Comprehension — summarize informational text identifying main idea and 2+ supporting details with >=80% accuracy.)

**Materials**:
- Short informational text: "How Community Gardens Help Neighborhoods" (1 page, 3 paragraphs)
- Summarizing graphic organizer (Topic / 3 Key Details / So What?)
- Dry-erase boards

**Lesson**:

1. **Teach the Framework (3 min)**:
   - *"Summarizing means retelling the MOST IMPORTANT parts in a short way. We use: Topic + Key Details + So What?"*
   - **Topic**: What is this text mainly about?
   - **Key Details**: What are the 2–3 most important facts or ideas?
   - **So What?**: Why does this matter? What's the takeaway?

2. **Guided Practice (7 min)**:
   - Read "How Community Gardens Help Neighborhoods" aloud together.
   - Paragraph 1: Gardens provide fresh food for families who can't afford grocery store produce.
   - Paragraph 2: Gardens bring neighbors together — people of all ages work side by side and build friendships.
   - Paragraph 3: Gardens make neighborhoods more beautiful and can reduce crime because people are outside and watching.
   - Complete the graphic organizer together:
     - **Topic**: How community gardens help neighborhoods
     - **Detail 1**: They provide fresh, affordable food.
     - **Detail 2**: They bring neighbors together and build community.
     - **Detail 3**: They make neighborhoods safer and more beautiful.
     - **So What?**: Community gardens improve neighborhoods in many ways — food, friendships, and safety.
   - *"Notice I didn't retell every sentence. I pulled out the MAIN IDEA of each paragraph."*
   - Connection: *"This text is about community gardens. What's the author's purpose?"* (Inform — and maybe persuade!)

#### Differentiation
- **Below level**: Teacher reads aloud, students follow along. Graphic organizer has sentence frames: "This text is about ___. One important detail is ___. Another detail is ___."
- **On level**: Complete organizer together, then write a 2–3 sentence summary independently on boards.
- **Above level**: After the group summary, read a second short text independently and summarize using the framework without teacher support.

#### Part 3: Opinion Essay Revision (11:00–11:30)

**Objective**: Students will revise their opinion essay draft using peer feedback and an editing checklist, improving their claim, reasons, transitions, and conventions. (IEP Goal: Written Expression — revise and edit written work for clarity and conventions.)

**Materials**:
- Students' opinion essay drafts (from Wednesday)
- Peer feedback ("Stars and Wishes" from Wednesday)
- Editing checklist (CUPS: Capitalization, Understanding/sense, Punctuation, Spelling)
- Transition words reference card
- Colored pencils (for revision marks: green = additions, red = deletions)
- Clean lined paper (for final copy)

**Lesson**:

1. **Revision Mini-Lesson (5 min)**:
   - *"Revision means 're-seeing' your writing. First, we revise for IDEAS — are your reasons strong? Then we edit for CONVENTIONS — spelling, punctuation, capitalization."*
   - Model one revision using a sample paragraph (on chart paper):
     - Original: "I think dogs are good. They are nice. They are fun."
     - Revised: "I believe dogs make the best pets. First, dogs are loyal companions who are always happy to see you. Also, playing with a dog is great exercise for both the dog and the owner."
   - *"What changed? The opinion is clearer. The reasons are specific. The transitions connect the ideas."*

2. **Revision Cycle (20 min)**:
   - **Step 1 (5 min)**: Reread your draft silently. Circle your opinion statement. Number your reasons. Check: Do you have at least 2 reasons? Do you have transition words?
   - **Step 2 (5 min)**: Read your peer feedback (Stars and Wishes). Make at least one change based on the feedback. Use green pencil for additions.
   - **Step 3 (5 min)**: Use the CUPS editing checklist:
     - **C** — Capitalization: First word of each sentence? Proper nouns?
     - **U** — Understanding: Does each sentence make sense? Read it aloud quietly.
     - **P** — Punctuation: Period, question mark, or exclamation point at the end of each sentence?
     - **S** — Spelling: Check red words (beautiful, different, important, probably, suddenly). Check pattern words (prefixes, suffixes). Circle any word you're unsure about.
   - **Step 4 (5 min)**: Begin writing the final copy on clean paper. (Students who need more time will finish Friday.)

3. **Teacher Conferences (during revision cycle)**:
   - Teacher meets with 3–4 students individually for 3 minutes each.
   - Focus questions: *"What is your strongest reason? How can you make it even stronger? Is your closing connected to your opening?"*
   - Note each student's revision level for data collection.

4. **Closure (5 min)**:
   - *"Tomorrow you'll finalize your 'Why ___ Matters' essay for your portfolio. This is one of the pieces your family will see at the showcase!"*
   - Collect drafts and revised copies in progress.

#### Differentiation
- **Below level**: Teacher conference prioritizes these students. Co-revise: read the draft aloud together, identify one place to add more detail, and one spelling correction. Final copy may be shorter (4–5 sentences total). Accept teacher scribed additions if needed.
- **On level**: Full revision cycle as described. Final copy should have clear opinion, 2+ reasons with transitions, and a closing. CUPS check independently.
- **Above level**: Full revision plus: strengthen word choice (replace "good" with specific adjectives), add a counterargument sentence, and ensure the closing does more than just restate — it should leave the reader thinking. Self-edit using CUPS, then peer-edit a classmate's paper.

### Exploration & Enrichment (12:00–1:00)

**Activity**: Grocery Store Math — Budgeting a Family Meal (Extended Simulation)

**Overview**: Students apply morning math skills in a hands-on grocery shopping simulation. They work with a real-world budget, make purchasing decisions, calculate totals and change, and create a bar graph of their spending.

**Materials**:
- Grocery store flyer or printed price list (same as morning math)
- Shopping list template (1 per student)
- Play money ($20 per student in bills and coins)
- "Store" setup: items labeled with prices at stations around the room (pictures of items on cards, or real packaging if available)
- Calculators (for checking)
- Graph paper for bar graphs
- Bar graph template (pre-drawn axes for some students)
- Receipt template (for recording purchases)

**Schedule**:

1. **Setup & Rules (5 min)**:
   - The room is set up as a "store" with 4 stations: Produce, Protein, Grains, and Dairy.
   - Each station has item cards with prices.
   - Rules: *"You have $20. Your job is to buy ingredients for a healthy family dinner — at least one item from 3 different stations. You must pay with exact change or receive correct change."*
   - Assign store "cashiers" (rotate) — these students practice making change.

2. **Shopping Simulation (20 min)**:
   - Students shop with their $20 in play money and shopping list template.
   - At each station, they select items, record the price, and "pay" the cashier.
   - Cashiers give change (with teacher oversight).
   - Students track running total on their shopping list.
   - Teacher circulates: *"What's your running total? How much do you have left? Are you on budget?"*
   - After shopping, students return to their seats and calculate their exact total and change from $20.

3. **Bar Graph Activity (15 min)**:
   - Students create a bar graph showing how they spent their $20:
     - X-axis: Categories (Produce, Protein, Grains, Dairy)
     - Y-axis: Amount spent ($0–$10, increments of $1)
   - Color each bar differently.
   - Answer analysis questions:
     - *"Which category did you spend the most on? Why?"*
     - *"If you had $5 more, what would you add to your cart?"*
     - *"What fraction of your budget did you spend on protein?"*

4. **Class Data Compilation (10 min)**:
   - Create a class line plot: *"How much did each person spend in total?"*
   - Mark each student's total on a number line ($5–$20).
   - *"What was the most common amount spent? What was the range?"*
   - Connect to real life: *"Budgeting is a skill adults use every day. You just practiced it!"*

5. **Closing Circle (10 min)**:
   - *"What surprised you about budgeting? Was it hard to stay within $20?"*
   - *"How did you use math today that felt like real life?"*
   - Self-monitoring checklist.
   - Preview Friday: *"Tomorrow is our last weekday of Week 5. You'll finish your opinion essays, take our weekly assessments, and create your persuasive posters!"*
   - Dismissal at 1:00.

**Academic Connections**:
- **Math**: Addition, subtraction, multiplication of money; budgeting; bar graphs and line plots; fractions of a budget.
- **Writing**: Decision-making rationale connects to opinion writing — *"Why did you choose those items?"*
- **Reading**: Reading labels and prices (informational text features).

**Differentiation**:
- **Below level**: Budget of $10 (simpler). Buy only 3 items. Play money pre-sorted into exact amounts for each station. Bar graph template pre-drawn; students just fill in bars. Teacher-assisted checkout.
- **On level**: Full activity. $20 budget, 5+ items. Create bar graph independently. Answer all analysis questions.
- **Above level**: $25 budget with the constraint that they must feed a family of 4 a balanced meal (protein, vegetable, grain, dairy). Calculate cost per person. Compare unit prices: *"Is it cheaper to buy a bag of apples or individual apples?"* Create a budget pie chart instead of (or in addition to) a bar graph.
