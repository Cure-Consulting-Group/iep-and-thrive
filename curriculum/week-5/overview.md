# Week 5 — "Real-World Applications"
## Theme: Using What We Know in the Real World

**Dates**: August 4–8, 2026
**Focus**: Morphology (prefixes and suffixes), opinion/persuasive writing, fractions (Part 2) and measurement, real-world problem solving

---

## Weekly Learning Objectives

### Reading & Phonics (OG)
- Teach inflectional endings: -s, -es, -ed, -ing, -er, -est
- Teach spelling rules that apply when adding endings: doubling rule (hop → hopping), drop-e rule (make → making), change-y rule (happy → happiest)
- Introduce common prefixes and their meanings: un- (not), re- (again), pre- (before), dis- (opposite of)
- Introduce common suffixes and their meanings: -ful (full of), -less (without), -ness (state of being), -ly (in a ___ way)
- Practice building word webs: base word in the center, derived forms radiating out (e.g., help → helpful, helpless, helpfully, unhelpful, helper, helping)
- Multisensory: color-coding morphemes (base word in black, prefix in blue, suffix in red); building words with morpheme cards
- OG dictation includes words with prefixes/suffixes and cumulative review of all phonics patterns
- Red words for the week: beautiful, different, important, probably, suddenly
- Connected text practice: reading passages with morphologically complex words; discuss how prefixes/suffixes change meaning

### Comprehension
- Teach author's purpose: persuade, inform, entertain (PIE strategy)
- Practice identifying author's purpose in a variety of short texts (advertisements, news articles, stories, poems)
- Using text features to locate information quickly (table of contents, headings, index, bold words, captions)
- Summarizing informational text: topic + key details + so what?
- Connecting reading to real-world contexts: "Why did the author write this? Who is this for? What do they want me to think or do?"
- Reading response journal: "The author's purpose was ___ because ___"

### Writing
- Opinion/persuasive writing unit
- Monday: What is an opinion? vs. a fact — sorting activity; introduce the opinion writing frame (I believe ___ because ___)
- Tuesday: Stating a claim with reasons — brainstorm topics; model a persuasive paragraph
- Wednesday: Teach transition words for opinion writing: first, also, another reason, in addition, most importantly, because, finally
- Thursday: Draft a persuasive letter — convince someone to visit a place, try something, change something, or support a cause
- Friday: Write portfolio piece — "Why ___ Matters" essay (connects to enrichment real-world problem solving)
- Peer feedback using "Stars and Wishes" (one strength, one suggestion)

### Math
- Compare fractions using benchmarks: Is the fraction closer to 0, 1/2, or 1?
- For 4th graders: add and subtract fractions with like denominators (2/5 + 1/5 = 3/5) using fraction strips and number lines first, then abstractly
- For 3rd graders: deepen fraction comparison and equivalence work from Week 4
- Transition to measurement in real-world contexts:
  - Time: reading analog clocks (to the minute for 4th, to 5 minutes for 3rd); elapsed time problems
  - Money: counting coins and bills; making change; budgeting activities
  - Linear measurement: measuring with rulers (inches and centimeters); estimating length
- Data collection and representation: bar graphs and line plots using real data from enrichment activities
- CRA: all measurement activities begin with concrete tools (clocks, coins, rulers) before moving to paper-based problems

### Executive Function & SEL
- Teach "real-world problem solving" as a transferable skill: identify the problem, brainstorm solutions, evaluate, choose, act, reflect
- Practice applying this framework to academic tasks (stuck on a word → what strategies can I use?) and social situations
- Self-advocacy: "I need help with ___" / "I learn best when ___" / "Can you explain that a different way?"
- SEL focus: empathy and perspective-taking — connect to persuasive writing (understanding your audience) and community helper interviews
- Begin preparing for end-of-program showcase: "What am I most proud of learning this summer?"

## Differentiation Notes

### Flexible Grouping (Finalized After Week 4 Mid-Cycle Check)

**Phonics Groups**:
- Group A: Focus on inflectional endings (-s, -ed, -ing) with high-frequency base words; introduce only un- and re- as prefixes; introduce -ful and -less as suffixes; keep base words to single-syllable known words (unkind, redo, helpful, careless); continue daily review of vowel teams and syllable division from previous weeks
- Group B: Full morphology progression — all inflectional endings with spelling rules, all four prefixes, all four suffixes; word web activity with 2-syllable base words; practice reading morphologically complex words in connected text
- Group C: All Week 5 content plus extension — multiple affixes on one word (unhelpful, unkindness, disagreeably); Greek/Latin root preview (common roots: port, rupt, struct); word detective challenge: find words with prefixes/suffixes in independent reading and explain how the affix changes meaning

**Math Groups**:
- Group A: Continue fraction comparison with concrete manipulatives; measurement focus on time (to 5 minutes) and money (counting coins); bar graph construction with scaffolded template; keep numbers manageable
- Group B: Fraction comparison with benchmarks; 4th graders begin adding/subtracting fractions with like denominators; measurement includes time (to the minute), money (making change), and linear measurement; data collection and line plot construction
- Group C: Fraction addition/subtraction fluent with like denominators; elapsed time problems; multi-step money word problems (budgeting); create and interpret line plots with fractional measurements (e.g., measuring objects to the nearest 1/4 inch)

**Comprehension**: All students work on author's purpose with the PIE strategy. Differentiate by:
- Text complexity: provide texts at instructional reading level for independent practice
- Scaffolding: some students receive a PIE graphic organizer with sentence frames; others identify purpose and explain reasoning independently
- Summarizing: some students use a template (topic + 3 key details + so what); others summarize in their own format

**Writing**: All students write an opinion piece. Scaffold by:
- Providing the opinion frame template and transition word card for all students
- Students who need more support: choose from a provided topic list; use the frame as a direct guide; focus on stating opinion + 2 reasons + closing
- Students on pace: choose their own topic; 3 reasons with evidence or examples; revise with peer feedback
- Advanced: write for a specific audience (letter to principal, review for other kids, letter to the editor); include counterargument ("Some people think ___, but I disagree because ___")
