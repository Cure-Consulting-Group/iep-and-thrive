### Math Block (9:20–10:20)

**Objective**: Students will tell time on an analog clock (to 5 minutes for 3rd / to the minute for 4th), solve elapsed time problems, and count coins and bills to solve money word problems. (IEP Goal: Math Concepts — solve problems involving time and money with >=75% accuracy.)

**Materials**:
- Student analog clocks (geared — 1 per student)
- Large demonstration clock
- Play money sets (1 per pair: pennies, nickels, dimes, quarters, $1, $5, $10, $20 bills)
- Dry-erase boards + markers
- "Time & Money in the Real World" practice page (prepared)
- Number lines (for elapsed time — 0 to 120 minutes, marked in 5-min intervals)
- Word problem cards (8 cards — 4 time, 4 money)
- Chart paper for anchor chart

#### Warm-Up/Activation (5 min)
- **Clock Quick-Drill**: Teacher shows a time on the demonstration clock. Students set their clocks to match and hold them up.
  - 4:00, 7:30, 2:15, 9:45, 11:10 (3rd graders stop at 15-min intervals if needed)
  - *"Which hand tells the hour? Which tells the minutes? How do you know it's 2:15 and not 3:15?"*

#### Direct Instruction (15 min) — CRA: Concrete to Representational

1. **Time — Elapsed Time (8 min)**:
   - *"Elapsed time is the amount of time that passes between a start time and an end time. If a movie starts at 2:00 and ends at 4:30, how long was the movie?"*
   - **Concrete**: Use the student clock. Set it to 2:00. Count forward by hours: 2:00 → 3:00 (1 hour), 3:00 → 4:00 (2 hours). Then count the minutes: 4:00 → 4:30 (30 minutes). Total: 2 hours and 30 minutes.
   - **Representational**: Draw a "time number line" on chart paper:
     ```
     2:00 ---[1 hr]--- 3:00 ---[1 hr]--- 4:00 ---[30 min]--- 4:30
     ```
   - Practice together: *"School starts at 9:00 and ends at 1:00. How long is the school day?"* Students use clocks and then draw a time number line on boards.
   - Another: *"The garden club meets from 3:15 to 4:45. How long is the meeting?"* (1 hour 30 minutes)

2. **Money — Counting and Making Change (7 min)**:
   - *"Community helpers get paid for their work. Let's practice counting money and making change — skills you use in the real world every day."*
   - **Concrete**: Display a set of coins: 2 quarters, 3 dimes, 1 nickel, 4 pennies. *"How much is this?"*
   - Count together: 25, 50, 60, 70, 80, 85, 86, 87, 88, 89 cents. Strategy: start with the biggest coins.
   - **Making change**: *"You buy a snack for 65 cents and pay with a dollar. How much change do you get?"*
   - Model counting up from 65: 65 → 70 (nickel), 70 → 75 (nickel), 75 → 100 (quarter). Change = 35 cents.
   - Students practice with their play money. Teacher states price, students count out the exact amount, then figure the change from $1.00.

#### Guided Practice (15 min)

- **Station Rotation** (2 stations, 7 min each):

  **Station A — Time Problems** (with teacher):
  - Word problem cards:
    1. "The library opens at 10:00 AM and closes at 5:00 PM. How many hours is it open?" (7 hours)
    2. "Firefighters start their shift at 7:00 AM. Their shift is 8 hours long. What time do they finish?" (3:00 PM)
    3. "A doctor's appointment starts at 2:30 and lasts 45 minutes. What time does it end?" (3:15)
    4. "A nurse works from 6:00 AM to 2:30 PM. How long is her shift?" (8 hours 30 minutes)
  - Students use clocks and number lines. Teacher scaffolds.

  **Station B — Money Problems** (independent/partner):
  - Word problem cards:
    1. "A firefighter buys lunch for $8.50. She pays with a $10 bill. How much change?" ($1.50)
    2. "A librarian buys 3 books at $4.00 each. How much does she spend?" ($12.00)
    3. "You have $5.00. You buy a notebook for $2.75. How much is left?" ($2.25)
    4. "A crossing guard earns $15 per hour. She works 3 hours. How much does she earn?" ($45)
  - Students use play money to solve. Record answers on dry-erase boards.

#### Independent Practice (10 min)

- **"Time & Money in the Real World" Practice Page**:
  - Section A (4 problems): Read the clock and write the time.
  - Section B (3 problems): Elapsed time — start time given, end time given, find how long.
  - Section C (3 problems): Count the coins/bills shown and write the total.
  - Section D (2 problems): Word problems combining time or money with a real-world context.

#### Closure (5 min)
- **Exit Ticket**: On dry-erase boards:
  - *"A community helper starts work at 8:30 AM and works for 6 hours. What time does she finish?"* (2:30 PM)
  - *"You have 3 quarters, 2 dimes, and 1 nickel. How much money is that?"* (100 cents = $1.00)
- Preview: *"Tomorrow we measure real objects and use our measurement skills in a design challenge."*

#### Differentiation
- **Below level (Group A)**: Time to the nearest half hour only. Elapsed time in whole hours (no minutes). Money: count coins only (no bills), amounts under $1.00. Provide coin value reference card. Use clocks with color-coded hour/minute hands.
- **On level (Group B)**: Time to 5 minutes (3rd) or to the minute (4th). Elapsed time with hours and 15/30/45-minute intervals. Money to $10 with coins and bills.
- **Above level (Group C)**: Time to the minute. Elapsed time across noon (AM to PM). Multi-step money problems: *"You have $20. You buy items costing $5.75, $3.25, and $6.50. How much change do you get?"* Calculate hourly wages with multiplication.

#### Data Collection
- Collect practice pages. Score each section separately.
- Note during station rotation: Who can tell time fluently? Who struggles with elapsed time? Who can count mixed coins?

#### Part 1: OG Lesson — Prefixes pre- and dis- (10:30–10:50)

**Objective**: Students will identify and define the prefixes pre- (before) and dis- (opposite of/not), build words with all four prefixes taught, and read morphologically complex words in context. (IEP Goal: Decoding — apply knowledge of common prefixes to decode unfamiliar words.)

**Materials**:
- Morpheme cards: base word cards (heat, view, test, pay, school, order, agree, like, appear, connect, obey, cover, trust, approve) + prefix cards (un-, re-, pre-, dis-) in blue
- Prefix meaning reference chart (updated with all 4)
- Dry-erase boards + markers
- Colored pencils: blue (prefix), black (base word)
- Sand/salt trays

**OG Lesson Structure**:

1. **Cumulative Review (3 min)**:
   - Flash 6 phonogram cards (mixed vowel teams and r-controlled).
   - Flash un- and re- prefix cards: students say the prefix and its meaning.
   - Flash 3 red words: beautiful, different, important.

2. **New Concept — pre- and dis- (10 min)**:
   - Hold up **pre-** card. *"Pre- means 'before.' If you preheat the oven, you heat it before you cook. If you preview a movie, you view it before the full movie."*
   - Build together: **preheat, preview, pretest, prepay, preschool, preorder**.
   - Students build with morpheme cards, stating: *"Pre means before. So pre-[word] means [word] before."*
   - Hold up **dis-** card. *"Dis- means 'opposite of' or 'not.' If you disagree, you do NOT agree. If something disappears, it does the opposite of appearing."*
   - Build: **disagree, dislike, disappear, disconnect, disobey, discover, distrust, disapprove**.
   - Students build each word with cards.
   - **Key distinction**: *"Un- and dis- are similar — both can mean 'not.' But dis- often goes with certain words. We don't say 'unagree' — we say 'disagree.' Your ear will help you — does it sound right?"*

3. **Multisensory Practice (4 min)**:
   - **Prefix Sort**: Teacher says a word. Students hold up the correct prefix card (un-, re-, pre-, or dis-).
   - Words: unhappy (un-), replay (re-), preheat (pre-), dislike (dis-), unkind (un-), preschool (pre-), redo (re-), disagree (dis-), unlock (un-), preview (pre-).
   - **Sand tray**: Write 3 words — one with pre-, one with dis-, one review (un- or re-). Color-code the prefix.

4. **Connected Text (3 min)**:
   - Quick partner read: 6 sentences on the board containing all four prefixes. Students read each sentence aloud and underline the prefix word, then state the meaning.
     - "Please preheat the oven before we bake."
     - "I disagree with your answer, but let's talk about it."
     - "She had to redo her homework because it was incomplete."
     - "It is unsafe to cross without looking."
     - "We need to disconnect the old computer before we set up the new one."
     - "The teacher gave us a pretest to see what we already know."

#### Differentiation
- **Below level (Group A)**: Focus on pre- only today (introduce dis- Wednesday). Use only single-syllable base words where possible. Prefix card held up as a physical cue while reading.
- **On level (Group B)**: Full lesson as described.
- **Above level (Group C)**: Add the word "preview" as a metacognitive hook: *"When you preview a text before reading, what prefix are you using? How does knowing the prefix help you understand the word?"* Begin noticing prefixes in their independent reading.

#### Part 2: Author's Purpose — PIE Strategy (10:50–11:05)

**Objective**: Students will identify the author's purpose (persuade, inform, or entertain) in short texts and explain their reasoning with evidence. (IEP Goal: Comprehension — identify author's purpose with >=80% accuracy.)

**Materials**:
- PIE anchor chart (large, posted — P = Persuade, I = Inform, E = Entertain, with descriptions and example text types)
- 4 short texts (printed, 1 set per pair):
  - Text A: An advertisement for a new playground (*persuade*)
  - Text B: An informational paragraph about how recycling works (*inform*)
  - Text C: A funny short story about a dog who thinks he's a cat (*entertain*)
  - Text D: A letter to the editor about why schools need more recess (*persuade*)
- PIE graphic organizer (1 per student — columns: Text, Purpose, Evidence from the text)

**Lesson**:

1. **Introduce PIE (5 min)**:
   - *"Every time someone writes something, they have a purpose — a reason for writing. We use the word PIE to remember the three main purposes."*
   - **P = Persuade**: The author wants to convince you to think, feel, or do something. Look for: opinions, reasons, words like "should," "must," "best."
   - **I = Inform**: The author wants to teach you something or give you facts. Look for: facts, headings, diagrams, definitions.
   - **E = Entertain**: The author wants you to enjoy the story or laugh. Look for: characters, plot, humor, interesting language.
   - Post the PIE anchor chart.

2. **Guided Practice (7 min)**:
   - Read Text A (playground ad) aloud. *"What is the author's purpose? How do you know?"*
   - Model on the graphic organizer: Text A | Persuade | "The ad says 'you should visit' and 'the best playground ever' — those are opinion words trying to convince us."
   - Read Text B (recycling paragraph) together. Students identify: Inform. Evidence: facts, no opinions, teaches how recycling works.
   - Students complete the organizer for Texts C and D with a partner.
   - Debrief: *"Text D was tricky — it's a letter, but is it to entertain or persuade?"* (Persuade — the writer gives reasons why schools need more recess.)

3. **Connection to Writing (3 min)**:
   - *"This week YOU are writing to persuade. Knowing what persuasive writing looks like as a reader will make you a better persuasive writer."*
   - *"When you see words like 'should,' 'must,' 'best,' and 'I believe' — that's the author trying to persuade."*

#### Differentiation
- **Below level**: Read texts aloud to students. Provide PIE reference card with key signal words. Graphic organizer has sentence frames: "The author's purpose is ___ because the text says ___."
- **On level**: Full lesson. Complete organizer with partner, then independently for Text D.
- **Above level**: After identifying purpose, answer: *"Could this text have more than one purpose? What's the primary purpose and is there a secondary one?"* Write their own 3-sentence text with a clear purpose for a partner to identify.

#### Part 3: Persuasive Paragraph Modeling (11:05–11:30)

**Objective**: Students will identify the components of a persuasive paragraph (claim, reasons, transitions, closing) and begin brainstorming topics for their own opinion piece.

**Materials**:
- Model persuasive paragraph on chart paper (pre-written)
- Opinion writing frame template (from Monday — in student folders)
- Transition words reference card (in student folders)
- Brainstorm list: 10 possible opinion topics (posted)
- Composition notebooks

**Lesson**:

1. **Model Paragraph Analysis (10 min)**:
   - Read the model paragraph aloud (on chart paper):

   > *I believe that every school should have a garden. First, a school garden helps students learn about science by watching plants grow from seeds. Also, growing food teaches kids where their meals come from, which is an important life lesson. Another reason is that being outside in a garden is calming and helps students who feel stressed. In conclusion, school gardens make learning more fun and help students in many ways.*

   - *"Let's break this apart. What is the author's opinion?"* (Every school should have a garden.) Underline it.
   - *"What are the reasons?"* Number them: 1, 2, 3. Circle the transition words (First, Also, Another reason).
   - *"How does it end?"* (Restates the opinion — "In conclusion...")
   - Label on the chart: CLAIM → REASON 1 → REASON 2 → REASON 3 → CLOSING.
   - *"Notice the author didn't just say 'because gardens are cool.' Each reason explains WHY."*

2. **Topic Brainstorm (8 min)**:
   - Post 10 possible topics:
     1. Every kid should have a pet.
     2. Schools should have longer recess.
     3. Summer is the best season.
     4. Everyone should learn to cook.
     5. Recycling should be required.
     6. Kids should be allowed to pick their own books.
     7. Screen time should be limited for kids.
     8. Schools should start later in the morning.
     9. The best field trip would be ___.
     10. Community gardens make neighborhoods better.
   - Students can choose from the list OR pick their own topic.
   - In their notebooks, students write their topic at the top and brainstorm 3–4 possible reasons underneath.
   - Teacher circulates: *"Can you explain that reason more? Why do you think that? Who would you be trying to convince?"*

3. **Partner Share (5 min)**:
   - Turn and talk: Share your topic and your strongest reason.
   - Partner gives feedback: *"Your strongest reason was ___ because ___."*

4. **Closure (2 min)**:
   - *"Tomorrow you'll start drafting your persuasive paragraph using the writing frame. Come in tomorrow knowing your topic and at least 2 reasons."*
   - Collect notebooks to review brainstorms.

#### Differentiation
- **Below level**: Choose from the topic list (don't generate own topic). Brainstorm reasons verbally with teacher support, then dictate or write 2 reasons. Use the writing frame as a direct fill-in.
- **On level**: Choose from list or generate own topic. Brainstorm 3 reasons in writing. Ready to draft tomorrow.
- **Above level**: Generate own topic. Brainstorm 3+ reasons with supporting examples. Begin thinking about counterarguments: *"What might someone who disagrees with you say?"*

### Exploration & Enrichment (12:00–1:00)

**Activity**: Community Helpers — Interview & Research Project

**Overview**: Students learn about community helpers through research (and guest interview if available). They connect to the week's persuasive writing theme by forming opinions about which community helper has the most important job.

**Materials**:
- Community helper interview question sheet (prepared — 8 questions)
- Books or printed articles about community helpers (2–3 per table)
- Research recording sheet (Who? What do they do? Why are they important? What surprised me?)
- Drawing paper + colored pencils
- Chart paper for class compilation

**Schedule**:

1. **Introduction (5 min)**:
   - *"Today we research community helpers. You'll become an expert on one helper and then share what you learned."*
   - If a guest speaker is available (firefighter, nurse, librarian, etc.), introduce them now.
   - If no guest: explain that students will research using books and articles.
   - Assign or let students choose a community helper to research.

2. **Research Phase (20 min)**:
   - **If guest speaker is present (20 min)**:
     - Students use the interview question sheet:
       1. What is your job title?
       2. What do you do every day?
       3. Why did you choose this job?
       4. What is the hardest part of your job?
       5. What is the best part?
       6. What skills do you need?
       7. How does your job help the community?
       8. What do you wish kids knew about your job?
     - Students take notes during the interview.
     - 5 minutes for follow-up questions.

   - **If no guest speaker (20 min)**:
     - Students work in pairs, reading about their chosen community helper using provided materials.
     - Complete the research recording sheet: Who is this helper? What do they do? Why are they important? What surprised you?
     - Teacher circulates with guiding questions: *"How does this helper solve problems? What would happen if this job didn't exist?"*

3. **Creative Response (15 min)**:
   - Students create a "Community Helper Profile" — a one-page illustrated profile:
     - Drawing of the community helper at work
     - 3 facts about the job
     - 1 opinion sentence: "I think ___ is the most important community helper because ___."
   - This connects to persuasive writing: they must support their opinion with a reason.

4. **Gallery Share (10 min)**:
   - Profiles displayed on tables. Students do a quick gallery walk.
   - Closing discussion: *"Did anyone change their mind about which helper is most important after seeing others' work?"*
   - Connect to perspective-taking: *"Even if we disagree about which job is MOST important, we can see that ALL of these jobs matter."*

5. **Closing Circle (10 min)**:
   - *"Name one community helper you appreciate more now than you did this morning."*
   - Self-monitoring checklist.
   - Preview tomorrow: *"Tomorrow we design a solution to a real problem — you'll be engineers and inventors!"*
   - Dismissal at 1:00.

**Academic Connections**:
- **Reading**: Informational text comprehension (research materials), author's purpose (identifying purpose of informational texts about helpers).
- **Writing**: Opinion sentence (supporting an opinion with a reason — practice for opinion essay).
- **Math**: Some profiles may include data (hours worked, people helped) — connect to time and multiplication.

**Differentiation**:
- **Below level**: Provide a research recording sheet with sentence frames. Interview questions pre-printed with space to write 1–2 word answers. Profile: draw + 1 fact + 1 opinion sentence.
- **On level**: Full activity as described.
- **Above level**: Research two community helpers. Write a comparison: *"Both ___ and ___ help the community, but in different ways..."* Include data (average salary, hours worked) and connect to math.
