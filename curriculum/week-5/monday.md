### Math Block (9:20–10:20)

**Objective**: Students will compare fractions to the benchmarks 0, 1/2, and 1, placing fractions on a number line and explaining their reasoning. (IEP Goal: Math Concepts — fraction comparison using models and number lines with >=80% accuracy.)

**Materials**:
- Fraction strips (1 set per student — continued from Week 4)
- Fraction number lines (0–1, laminated, 1 per student)
- Fraction benchmark cards: 0, 1/2, 1 (large, for teacher demonstration)
- Dry-erase boards + markers
- Fraction benchmark sorting mat (three columns: "Close to 0," "Close to 1/2," "Close to 1")
- Prepared fraction cards for sorting (1/8, 2/8, 3/8, 4/8, 5/8, 7/8, 1/4, 3/4, 1/3, 2/3, 1/6, 5/6, 2/5, 4/5)
- Student analog clocks (for second half of block)
- Chart paper for anchor chart

#### Warm-Up/Activation (5 min)
- **Fraction Flash**: Hold up fraction strip pieces one at a time. Students give a thumbs up if the fraction is bigger than 1/2, thumbs down if smaller, flat hand if exactly 1/2.
- Flash: 1/4, 3/4, 1/2, 1/8, 7/8, 2/3, 1/3
- *"How do you know 3/4 is more than 1/2? Turn and tell your partner."*

#### Direct Instruction (15 min) — CRA: Concrete to Representational

1. **Concrete — Fraction Strip Benchmarks (8 min)**:
   - Place the 1/2 fraction strip on the table. *"This is our benchmark — our measuring stick. Everything we compare today goes against this."*
   - Hold up 1/4 strip. Place it next to 1/2. *"Is 1/4 closer to 0 or closer to 1/2?"* (Closer to 0 — it's less than half.)
   - Hold up 3/4 strip. *"Is 3/4 closer to 1/2 or closer to 1?"* (Closer to 1 — it's more than half.)
   - Key teaching point: *"If the numerator is less than half the denominator, the fraction is close to 0. If the numerator is about half the denominator, it's close to 1/2. If the numerator is almost as big as the denominator, it's close to 1."*
   - Practice with strips: 1/8, 5/8, 2/6, 5/6. Students place each next to 1/2 strip and sort.

2. **Representational — Number Line (7 min)**:
   - Draw a 0-to-1 number line on chart paper. Mark 0, 1/2, and 1.
   - *"Let's place 3/8 on this number line. First — is 3/8 closer to 0, 1/2, or 1?"*
   - Model: 8 divided by 2 is 4, so 4/8 = 1/2. Since 3/8 < 4/8, it's a little less than 1/2.
   - Place 3/8 on the number line just left of the 1/2 mark.
   - Repeat with 1/6 (close to 0) and 5/6 (close to 1).
   - Create anchor chart: **"Fraction Benchmarks: Is It Close to 0, 1/2, or 1?"**

#### Guided Practice (15 min)

- **Benchmark Sorting Activity**: Each pair receives 14 fraction cards (listed above).
  - Students sort each fraction card into one of three columns on their sorting mat: Close to 0, Close to 1/2, Close to 1.
  - They must use their fraction strips to verify at least 5 of their placements.
  - After sorting, students place all 14 fractions on their laminated number line (0–1) in order.

- **Teacher circulates with guiding questions**:
  - *"How do you decide if 2/5 is close to 0 or 1/2?"*
  - *"Can you use your strips to check?"*
  - *"Which two fractions were hardest to place? Why?"*

#### Independent Practice (10 min)

- **Fraction Benchmark Practice Page**: 12 problems
  - Section A (6 problems): Circle the benchmark each fraction is closest to (0, 1/2, or 1): 1/5, 4/6, 7/8, 2/8, 3/5, 1/10
  - Section B (3 problems): Place fractions on a number line: 2/4, 1/3, 5/6
  - Section C (3 problems): Compare using >, <, =: 3/8 ___ 1/2; 5/6 ___ 2/3; 1/4 ___ 3/4

- **4th Grade Extension (on back)**: Add fractions with like denominators:
  - 1/5 + 2/5 = ___; 3/8 + 2/8 = ___; 2/6 + 3/6 = ___

#### Closure (5 min)
- **Quick Check**: Teacher holds up a fraction card. Students write on dry-erase boards: 0, 1/2, or 1 (nearest benchmark). Flash 5 fractions rapidly.
- Preview: *"Tomorrow we'll use fraction skills and real money to compare prices at the store."*
- **Time Mini-Lesson Transition (5 min built into closure)**: Distribute analog clocks. Quick review: *"Show me 3:00... Show me 3:30... Show me 9:15."* (This sets up measurement work later in the week.)

#### Differentiation
- **Below level (Group A)**: Use only unit fractions and fractions with denominators of 2, 4, and 8. Keep fraction strips in hand for all problems. Sorting mat has visual benchmarks (a fraction strip showing 1/2 is printed on the mat). Practice page has only Section A with picture support.
- **On level (Group B)**: Full lesson as described. Use fraction strips to verify, then transition to reasoning without strips. Complete Sections A–C.
- **Above level (Group C)**: Complete all sections plus 4th grade extension. Challenge: *"Can you find a fraction that is exactly halfway between 1/2 and 1? Prove it with your strips."* Begin adding/subtracting fractions with like denominators.

#### Data Collection
- Collect practice pages. Score Section A (benchmark identification) and Section C (comparison).
- Note during guided practice: Who still relies entirely on strips? Who can reason about benchmarks mentally?
- Record analog clock accuracy during closure mini-lesson.

#### Part 1: OG Lesson — Morphology: Prefixes un- and re- (10:30–11:00)

**Objective**: Students will identify and define the prefixes un- (not) and re- (again), read and build words with these prefixes, and explain how the prefix changes the base word's meaning. (IEP Goal: Decoding — apply knowledge of common prefixes to decode unfamiliar words with >=80% accuracy.)

**Materials**:
- Morpheme cards (cardstock): base word cards (kind, do, happy, lock, fair, tie, safe, read, build, play, write, fill, use, load) + prefix cards (un-, re-) in blue
- Prefix meaning reference chart (posted)
- OG phonogram cards — cumulative deck
- Dry-erase boards + markers
- Colored pencils: blue (prefix), black (base word)
- Word web graphic organizer (1 per student)
- Sand/salt trays
- Red word cards: **beautiful, different** (first 2 of Week 5 set)
- Decodable passage with un-/re- words (prepared, 1 per student)

**OG Lesson Structure**:

1. **Cumulative Review — Phonogram Drill (5 min)**:
   - Rapid-fire phonogram card review: Include all vowel teams (ai, ay, ee, ea, oa, ow, oi, oy, ou, ow, au, aw), r-controlled vowels (ar, or, er, ir, ur), and digraphs.
   - Students respond chorally with the sound.
   - Quick syllable type review: *"What type of syllable is 'hope'?"* (VCe) *"What type is 'hop'?"* (closed) *"What type is 'car'?"* (r-controlled)
   - Mix in 3 red word flash cards from previous weeks: thought, special, island.

2. **New Concept — Prefixes (10 min)**:
   - *"For the last 4 weeks, we've been building our reading muscles by learning sound patterns. This week, we level up. We're going to learn about parts of words called morphemes — the building blocks of meaning."*
   - Hold up the prefix card **un-** (in blue). *"This is a prefix. A prefix goes at the beginning of a word and changes its meaning. Un- means 'not.' Watch what happens."*
   - Place the base word card **kind** on the table. *"Kind means friendly and caring."*
   - Snap the **un-** card in front of it: **unkind**. *"Unkind means 'not kind.' The prefix un- reversed the meaning."*
   - Repeat with **happy → unhappy**, **fair → unfair**, **safe → unsafe**.
   - Students build each word with their own morpheme cards, saying: *"Un means not. [base word] means ___. So un-[base word] means not ___."*
   - Introduce **re-** (in blue). *"Re- means 'again.' Watch."*
   - **read → reread** (*"Read again"*), **build → rebuild** (*"Build again"*), **do → redo** (*"Do again"*), **fill → refill** (*"Fill again"*).
   - Students build with cards and state the meaning.
   - Create anchor chart: **Prefixes: un- = not, re- = again** with 3 examples each.

3. **Multisensory Practice (5 min)**:
   - **Sand tray writing**: Teacher says a word. Students repeat it, identify the prefix, trace the prefix in the sand in blue (using blue marker-tipped finger or just their finger), then the base word, then write the whole word.
   - Words: unlock, redo, untie, replay, unload.
   - **Color-coding on dry-erase boards**: Write the word "rebuild." Underline "re" in blue, "build" in black. Write the meaning: "build again."
   - Practice 3 more: unsafe, rewrite, unhappy.

4. **Red Word Introduction (5 min)**:
   - Introduce **beautiful** and **different**.
   - **beautiful**: *"This word doesn't follow regular patterns. Let's look at it. B-E-A-U-T-I-F-U-L. The 'eau' is unusual — it says /yoo/. The ending '-ful' is a suffix we'll learn later this week — it means 'full of.' So 'beautiful' means 'full of beauty.'"*
   - Finger-spell it. Air-write it. Write it on dry-erase boards 3 times.
   - **different**: *"D-I-F-F-E-R-E-N-T. The tricky part is the middle — some people want to skip the second 'e' and say 'diffrent.' Let's tap it out: dif-fer-ent. Three syllables."*
   - Arm-tap the syllables. Write it on boards. Use in a sentence orally.
   - Add both to the red word wall.

5. **Connected Text (5 min)**:
   - Distribute the decodable passage: *"The Unfair Game"* (a short passage about kids who replay a board game after an unfair outcome — contains 8+ words with un- and re-).
   - Students read the passage with a partner (whisper reading).
   - Task: Circle every word with un- or re-. Write the base word next to it.
   - Quick share: *"How many un- words did you find? How many re- words?"*

#### Differentiation
- **Below level (Group A)**: Use only un- with single-syllable base words (unkind, untie, unzip, unpack, unfair). Provide a reference card with un- = not. Skip re- today — introduce it Tuesday. Red words: focus on "different" only; "beautiful" introduced Tuesday.
- **On level (Group B)**: Full lesson as described — both un- and re- with provided base words. Both red words introduced.
- **Above level (Group C)**: After the lesson, challenge: *"Can a word have BOTH a prefix and a suffix? What about 'unkindly' or 'unhappily'? Build 3 words with a prefix AND a suffix using your cards."* Preview dis- by trying to figure out its meaning from context: "The magician made the rabbit disappear."

#### Part 2: Opinion Writing Launch (11:00–11:30)

**Objective**: Students will distinguish between facts and opinions and state an opinion with at least one supporting reason. (IEP Goal: Written Expression — write an opinion paragraph with a topic sentence, 2+ supporting reasons, and a closing sentence.)

**Materials**:
- Opinion/fact sorting cards (20 statements on cards — 10 facts, 10 opinions)
- Chart paper for anchor chart
- Opinion writing frame template (1 per student)
- Transition words reference card (1 per student, placed in folders)
- Dry-erase boards + markers

**Lesson**:

1. **Activate (5 min)**:
   - Write on the board: "Pizza is the best food." and "Pizza is made with dough, sauce, and cheese."
   - *"Which of these is a fact? Which is an opinion? How do you know?"*
   - Key teaching: *"A fact can be proven true. An opinion is what someone believes or feels. Opinions can be supported with reasons, but people can disagree about them."*
   - *"This week, we're going to write opinions AND give strong reasons. That's called persuasive writing — writing that tries to convince someone."*

2. **Sorting Activity (10 min)**:
   - Pairs receive 20 statement cards. Sort into two piles: FACT and OPINION.
   - Sample fact cards: "Dogs have four legs." / "The sun rises in the east." / "There are 50 states in the United States." / "Water freezes at 32 degrees." / "Fish live in water."
   - Sample opinion cards: "Dogs are the best pets." / "Summer is the best season." / "Math is harder than reading." / "Everyone should recycle." / "Chocolate ice cream is the most delicious flavor."
   - Tricky ones to discuss: "Everyone should recycle" — is that fact or opinion? (Opinion — it's a belief, even if many people agree.) "Recycling reduces waste" — fact or opinion? (Fact — it can be proven.)
   - Debrief as a class: Discuss 3–4 tricky cards.

3. **Opinion Writing Frame Introduction (10 min)**:
   - Post the **Opinion Writing Frame** anchor chart:
     - **I believe that** ___ (state your opinion clearly)
     - **First,** ___ (reason 1)
     - **Also,** ___ (reason 2)
     - **Finally,** ___ (reason 3 — optional for some students)
     - **In conclusion,** ___ (restate your opinion)
   - Model with a think-aloud: *"I believe that recess should be longer. First, kids need time to move their bodies so they can focus better in class. Also, playing with friends helps kids learn to get along. In conclusion, longer recess would help students learn better."*
   - Distribute the writing frame template and transition words reference card.
   - Students practice orally: *"Turn to your partner. Tell them one thing you believe and give one reason why."*
   - Share 3–4 examples aloud. Coach in real time: *"Can you say 'I believe that...' to start?"*

4. **Quick Write (5 min)**:
   - On dry-erase boards, students write: "I believe that _____ because _____."
   - This is low-stakes practice — not the portfolio piece yet.
   - Volunteers share. Teacher highlights strong reasons: *"Notice how [student] didn't just say 'because it's cool' — they explained WHY."*

#### Differentiation
- **Below level**: Provide a topic choice list (recess, pizza, summer, pets). Use the frame as a fill-in-the-blank. Accept 1 reason + closing.
- **On level**: Choose own topic or from list. Use the frame with 2 reasons. Write in complete sentences.
- **Above level**: Choose own topic. Write 3 reasons with specific examples or evidence. Begin thinking about audience: *"Who are you trying to convince?"*

### Exploration & Enrichment (12:00–1:00)

**Activity**: Environmental Stewardship — Measuring a Garden Plot

**Enrichment Category**: Nature & Real-World (Monday theme)

**Overview**: Students design and measure a garden plot on the grounds, applying measurement skills (linear measurement, perimeter) and connecting to environmental stewardship. They practice using rulers and measuring tapes, record data, and create a scale drawing on graph paper.

**Materials**:
- Measuring tapes (at least 4, one per group)
- Rulers (1 per student — inches and centimeters)
- Stakes and string (for marking the garden plot outline)
- Graph paper (1 sheet per student)
- Clipboards + pencils
- "Garden Plot Planning Sheet" (prepared — sections for measurements, perimeter calculation, and sketch)
- Seed catalogs or printed pictures of vegetables/flowers (for planning what to plant)
- Chart paper for class data recording

**Schedule**:

1. **Introduction (8 min, inside)**:
   - *"Real-world problem solvers think about their environment. Today we're going to measure and design a garden plot — just like a real landscaper or environmental scientist would."*
   - Discuss: *"Why do gardens matter? What do they give us?"* (Food, beauty, habitat for pollinators, cleaner air.)
   - Review measurement vocabulary: **length, width, perimeter, inches, feet, centimeters, meters**.
   - Model how to use a measuring tape: pull it taut, read the number at the end of the object, record the measurement.
   - Assign groups of 2–3 students.

2. **Outdoor Measurement (20 min, outside)**:
   - Each group is assigned a section of ground (approximately 6 ft x 4 ft — pre-marked with corner stakes).
   - Tasks:
     - Measure the length and width of your plot in feet AND inches.
     - Calculate the perimeter (add all four sides).
     - Mark the edges with string tied to the stakes.
     - Measure and record 3 objects near the garden area (a bench, a sidewalk section, a tree's circumference) for extra practice.
   - Teacher circulates: *"What unit are you using? Is your tape straight? How do you calculate perimeter?"*

3. **Scale Drawing (15 min, inside)**:
   - Back inside, students create a scale drawing of their garden plot on graph paper.
   - Simple scale: 1 square = 1 foot (or 6 inches for larger plots).
   - Students draw the outline, then plan what to plant: look at seed catalog pictures, choose 3–4 plants, and draw them in sections of the plot.
   - Label each section with the plant name and the measurement of that section.

4. **Math Connection — Perimeter Problems (7 min)**:
   - Each group shares their perimeter with the class. Record all on chart paper.
   - *"If we wanted to put a small fence around our garden, how much fencing would we need?"* (= perimeter)
   - Word problem: *"Fencing costs $3 per foot. If your perimeter is 20 feet, how much would fencing cost?"* Students solve on dry-erase boards.
   - 4th grade extension: *"What if we used fencing on only 3 sides and a wall on the 4th? How would that change the cost?"*

5. **Closing Circle (10 min)**:
   - *"What did you learn about measurement today that you could use in real life?"*
   - Each student shares one observation.
   - Preview tomorrow: *"Tomorrow we explore community helpers — people who solve real-world problems every day."*
   - Self-monitoring check: students rate themselves on the checklist.
   - Dismissal at 1:00.

**Differentiation**:
- **Below level**: Partner with a stronger measurer. Provide a pre-drawn garden plot outline on graph paper — students fill in measurements and plants. Perimeter calculated with a number line.
- **On level**: Full activity as described. Calculate perimeter and solve the fencing word problem.
- **Above level**: Calculate area as well as perimeter. Solve multi-step fencing problems. Plan sections of the garden using fractions: *"If 1/4 of the garden is tomatoes, how many square feet is that?"*
