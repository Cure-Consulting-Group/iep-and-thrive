   - Words: unkind (un- = not), replay (re- = again), preheat (pre- = before), disagree (dis- = not/opposite).

   - Introduce **probably**: *"P-R-O-B-A-B-L-Y. This is a tricky one — prob-ab-ly. Three syllables. People often say 'probly' but we spell all three syllables."*

   - *"Use 'probably' in a sentence about today."* Students share aloud.

4. **Design Thinking Introduction (8 min)**:
   - *"Today's enrichment is a STEM design challenge. Let me teach you the steps real engineers use."*
   - Post the **Design Thinking Steps**:
     1. **Understand the problem** — What needs to be fixed or improved?
     2. **Brainstorm** — What are all the possible solutions? (No bad ideas!)
     3. **Plan** — Choose the best idea. Sketch your design.
     4. **Build** — Create a prototype (a first version).
     5. **Test** — Does it work? What happened?

### Math Block (9:20–10:20)

**Objective**: 4th graders will add and subtract fractions with like denominators using fraction strips and number lines, then transition to abstract notation. 3rd graders will deepen fraction comparison and begin measurement with rulers. (IEP Goal: Math Concepts — identify, compare, and compute with fractions using models and number lines with >=80% accuracy.)

**Materials**:
- Fraction strips (1 set per student)
- Fraction number lines (0–2, laminated)
- Dry-erase boards + markers
- Rulers (dual-unit: inches and centimeters, 1 per student)
- Objects to measure (pencils, books, scissors, folders, crayons — 10 items at each table)
- "Fraction Operations + Measurement" practice page (prepared — differentiated A/B versions)
- Chart paper for anchor chart
- Graph paper (for recording measurements)

#### Warm-Up/Activation (5 min)
- **Fraction Benchmark Quick Sort**: Teacher holds up a fraction card. Students write 0, 1/2, or 1 on boards (nearest benchmark).
- Flash: 1/6 (0), 3/4 (1), 4/8 (1/2), 7/8 (1), 2/5 (1/2), 1/10 (0).
- *"Yesterday we used benchmarks to compare. Today we're going to ADD and SUBTRACT fractions."*

#### Direct Instruction (15 min) — CRA Progression

1. **Concrete — Adding Fractions with Fraction Strips (8 min)**:
   - *"If you eat 1/4 of a pizza and your friend eats 2/4, how much pizza did you eat together?"*
   - Students place 1/4 strip and 2/4 strip end to end on their number line.
   - *"What do you notice? 1/4 + 2/4 = ?"* (3/4) Verify by placing a 3/4 strip on top.
   - Key teaching point: *"When we add fractions with the SAME denominator, we add the numerators and keep the denominator the same. The denominator tells us the size of the pieces — they're already the same size."*
   - Practice with strips: 2/6 + 3/6 = 5/6; 1/8 + 3/8 = 4/8 (= 1/2); 2/5 + 2/5 = 4/5.

2. **Concrete → Representational — Subtracting Fractions (4 min)**:
   - *"If you have 5/6 of a candy bar and you give away 2/6, how much do you have left?"*
   - Students place 5/6 strip, then remove 2/6. *"What's left?"* (3/6 = 1/2)
   - Draw on number line: Start at 5/6, jump back 2/6, land on 3/6.
   - Practice: 7/8 - 3/8 = 4/8 (= 1/2); 4/5 - 1/5 = 3/5.

3. **Abstract — Writing Equations (3 min)**:
   - Create anchor chart: **Adding & Subtracting Fractions with Like Denominators**
   - Rule: Same denominator → add (or subtract) the numerators → keep the denominator.
   - 1/4 + 2/4 = 3/4 (draw model next to each equation)
   - 5/6 - 2/6 = 3/6 = 1/2

   *Note: 3rd graders who are not ready for operations stay with fraction comparison using strips and benchmarks during this instruction.*

#### Guided Practice (15 min)

- **Split by readiness**:

  **Group 1 (3rd graders + students needing more comparison work)**:
  - Fraction comparison partner game: "Fraction War" — each student flips a fraction card, compare using strips, person with the larger fraction wins the round. Play 10 rounds.
  - Then: Measure 5 objects at their table using rulers (to the nearest inch for 3rd graders). Record: Object, Estimated Length, Actual Length. Compare estimate vs. actual.

  **Group 2 (4th graders + advanced 3rd graders)**:
  - Fraction addition/subtraction task cards (with teacher):
    - 2/8 + 5/8 = ___ (7/8)
    - 3/4 - 1/4 = ___ (2/4 = 1/2)
    - 1/6 + 4/6 = ___ (5/6)
    - 6/8 - 2/8 = ___ (4/8 = 1/2) — *"Can you simplify?"*
    - 2/5 + 2/5 = ___ (4/5)
    - Word problem: *"Maria read 3/8 of her book on Monday and 2/8 on Tuesday. How much has she read in all?"* (5/8)
  - Use fraction strips for the first 3, then try the rest without strips.

#### Independent Practice (10 min)

- **"Fraction Operations + Measurement" Practice Page**:

  **Version A (3rd grade / below level)**:
  - Section A (4 problems): Compare fractions — circle the larger fraction (with fraction strip pictures).
  - Section B (4 problems): Measure the line/object drawn and write the measurement in inches.
  - Section C (2 problems): Fraction word problems using comparison (no operations).

  **Version B (4th grade / on/above level)**:
  - Section A (4 problems): Add fractions with like denominators.
  - Section B (4 problems): Subtract fractions with like denominators.
  - Section C (2 problems): Measure objects drawn to the nearest 1/2 inch and 1/4 inch.
  - Section D (2 problems): Fraction word problems (add or subtract).

#### Closure (5 min)
- **Show Me**: On dry-erase boards:
  - 3rd graders: *"Which is larger — 3/8 or 5/8? Draw a model to prove it."*
  - 4th graders: *"2/6 + 3/6 = ? Show your work."*
- *"Tomorrow we'll use money and measurement together at the grocery store!"*

#### Differentiation
- **Below level (Group A)**: Stay with fraction comparison using strips only. Measurement in whole inches only. No fraction operations this day.
- **On level (Group B)**: Fraction comparison fluent. Begin adding fractions with like denominators using strips, then number line. Measurement to nearest inch.
- **Above level (Group C)**: Fraction addition/subtraction fluent. Simplify answers (4/8 = 1/2). Measurement to nearest 1/4 inch. Challenge: *"If I need 7/8 cup of flour and I already have 3/8 cup, how much more do I need?"*

#### Data Collection
- Collect practice pages (Version A or B). Score by section.
- During guided practice, note: Who can add fractions without strips? Who still needs concrete support?

#### Part 1: OG Lesson — Suffixes -ful and -less + Spelling Rules (10:30–10:50)

**Objective**: Students will identify and define the suffixes -ful (full of) and -less (without), apply them to base words, and learn the doubling and drop-e spelling rules when adding suffixes. (IEP Goal: Decoding — apply knowledge of common suffixes to decode and spell words with >=80% accuracy.)

**Materials**:
- Morpheme cards: base word cards (help, care, hope, use, thank, peace, harm, fear, pain, cheer, rest, power, taste, thought, color, home, joy, play) + suffix cards (-ful, -less) in red
- Spelling rules anchor chart (prepared on chart paper):
  - **Doubling Rule**: If a short vowel word ends in one consonant, double the consonant before adding a vowel suffix (hop → hopping, run → running)
  - **Drop-e Rule**: If the word ends in silent e, drop the e before adding a vowel suffix (hope → hoping, make → making)
  - **Note**: -ful and -less are consonant suffixes, so usually no change needed (hopeful, careless)
- Dry-erase boards + markers
- Colored pencils: black (base word), red (suffix)
- Word web graphic organizer (1 per student)

**OG Lesson Structure**:

1. **Cumulative Review (3 min)**:
   - Flash all 4 prefix cards: un-, re-, pre-, dis-. Students say meaning.
   - Teacher says 3 words. Students identify the prefix: "preview" (pre-), "disagree" (dis-), "unkind" (un-).
   - Flash red words: beautiful, different, important, probably.

2. **New Concept — Suffixes -ful and -less (10 min)**:
   - *"We've been learning about prefixes — parts that go at the beginning of words. Now we move to the end. A suffix goes at the END of a word and changes its meaning — and sometimes its part of speech."*
   - Hold up **-ful** (red card). *"-ful means 'full of.' If you're helpful, you're full of help. If something is beautiful, it's full of beauty."*
   - Build: **helpful, careful, hopeful, thankful, peaceful, cheerful, joyful, playful, powerful, colorful**.
   - Students build with cards, stating: *"-ful means full of. [base word]-ful means full of [base word]."*
   - Hold up **-less** (red card). *"-less means 'without.' If you're helpless, you're without help. If something is careless, it's without care."*
   - Build: **helpless, careless, hopeless, useless, harmless, fearless, painless, restless, tasteless, thoughtless, homeless**.
   - Students build with cards, stating: *"-less means without. [base word]-less means without [base word]."*
   - **Key connection**: *"Notice something amazing — 'helpful' and 'helpless' are OPPOSITES. The suffixes -ful and -less are antonyms!"*
   - Practice: Teacher says a word with -ful, students say the -less version (and vice versa): careful/careless, hopeful/hopeless, thankful/thankless, powerful/powerless.

3. **Spelling Rules Introduction (5 min)**:
   - Post the **Spelling Rules Anchor Chart**.
   - *"When we add a suffix, sometimes we need to change the spelling of the base word. Here are two important rules."*
   - **Doubling Rule**: *"If a short-vowel word ends in one consonant, and the suffix starts with a vowel, DOUBLE the consonant."*
     - hop + -ing = hopping (not hoping!)
     - run + -ing = running
     - sit + -ing = sitting
   - **Drop-e Rule**: *"If the word ends in silent e, and the suffix starts with a vowel, DROP the e."*
     - hope + -ing = hoping (not hopeing!)
     - make + -ing = making
     - use + -able = usable
   - *"But here's the trick: -ful and -less start with CONSONANTS (f and l). So we usually don't change the base word."*
     - hope + -ful = hopeful (keep the e because -ful starts with a consonant)
     - care + -less = careless (keep the e)
   - Practice on boards: Write the base word, add the suffix, check the spelling rule.
     - pain + -ful = painful (no change needed — no silent e, no doubling issue)
     - rest + -less = restless (no change)
     - beauty + -ful = beautiful (*"Wait — what happened to the y?"* Change-y rule preview: beauty → beauti + ful. We'll learn this rule Thursday.)

4. **Multisensory Practice (2 min)**:
   - On dry-erase boards, color-code 3 words: base word in black, suffix in red.
   - Words: thankful, fearless, cheerful.
   - Say the base word, say the suffix, say the meaning, write the whole word.

#### Differentiation
- **Below level (Group A)**: Focus on -ful only (introduce -less Thursday). Use only base words that don't require spelling changes (helpful, thankful, cheerful, painful, joyful). Skip spelling rules — just build words. Provide a suffix meaning card at desk.
- **On level (Group B)**: Full lesson — both suffixes. Practice the antonym pairs. Introduce doubling and drop-e rules with awareness, but no independent application yet.
- **Above level (Group C)**: Full lesson plus challenge — combine a prefix AND suffix on one word: unhelpful, unkindness (preview of -ness Thursday). Apply spelling rules to -ing words independently.

#### Part 2: Text Features Quick Lesson (10:50–11:00)

**Objective**: Students will identify and use text features (headings, bold words, captions, diagrams) to locate information in informational text. (IEP Goal: Comprehension — use text features to locate information with >=80% accuracy.)

**Materials**:
- Sample informational text page about recycling (with headings, bold words, a diagram, a caption, and a sidebar)
- Text features scavenger hunt sheet (list of features to find and label)

**Lesson**:

1. **Quick Teach (5 min)**:
   - Display the informational text page.
   - *"Informational texts are organized with special features that help us find information quickly — without reading every word."*
   - Point out and name each feature:
     - **Heading**: Tells you what the section is about
     - **Bold words**: Important vocabulary — usually defined nearby
     - **Caption**: Text under a picture that explains it
     - **Diagram/illustration**: A picture that shows how something works
     - **Sidebar**: Extra information in a box off to the side
   - *"If I want to know what 'compost' means, where do I look?"* (Bold word → definition nearby)
   - *"If I want to know what happens to recycled plastic, which heading do I look under?"*

2. **Scavenger Hunt (5 min)**:
   - Students complete the text features scavenger hunt on the sample text:
     - Find and circle 2 headings. What is each section about?
     - Find and underline 3 bold words. What do they mean?
     - Find the diagram. What does it show?
     - Find the caption. What picture does it describe?
   - Quick share: 2 students share one feature they found.

#### Differentiation
- **Below level**: Teacher does the scavenger hunt together with this group, pointing to each feature.
- **On level**: Complete independently, share with partner.
- **Above level**: After the hunt, answer: *"Why did the author include a diagram instead of just writing about it? When is a diagram more helpful than words?"*

#### Part 3: Opinion Essay Drafting (11:00–11:30)

**Objective**: Students will draft an opinion paragraph using the writing frame, incorporating transition words (first, also, another reason, because, in conclusion). (IEP Goal: Written Expression — write an opinion paragraph with a topic sentence, 2+ supporting reasons, and a closing sentence, scoring >=3/4 on rubric.)

**Materials**:
- Opinion writing frame template (in student folders)
- Transition words reference card (in student folders)
- Model paragraph (posted from Tuesday)
- Composition notebooks or lined paper
- Pencils + erasers
- "Stars and Wishes" peer feedback template (for end of session)

**Lesson**:

1. **Review & Model (5 min)**:
   - Reread the model paragraph from Tuesday (school gardens).
   - *"Today you write YOUR opinion paragraph. You have your topic and reasons from yesterday's brainstorm. Now we put it all together."*
   - Quick review of transition words: First, Also, Another reason, In addition, Most importantly, Because, Finally, In conclusion.
   - *"Your paragraph needs: (1) a clear opinion statement, (2) at least 2 reasons with transition words, (3) a closing that restates your opinion."*

2. **Drafting (20 min)**:
   - Students write their opinion paragraphs using the frame as a guide.
   - Teacher circulates with targeted support:
     - For stuck students: *"Tell me your opinion out loud. Now say 'because...' What's your first reason?"*
     - For students writing too little: *"You have a great reason here. Can you explain more? Why does that matter?"*
     - For students writing fluently: *"Read your opening sentence. Does it clearly state your opinion? Could a reader tell exactly what you believe?"*
   - Remind students to check their transition words reference card.
   - Students who finish early: reread for spelling (especially red words and pattern words from this week), then begin a second draft or add a third reason.

3. **Peer Feedback (5 min)**:
   - Swap papers with a partner.
   - Use "Stars and Wishes":
     - **Star**: Write one thing the writer did well. (e.g., "I like your reason about ___" or "Your transition words make it easy to follow.")
     - **Wish**: Write one suggestion. (e.g., "I wish you explained your second reason more" or "I wish you had a stronger closing.")
   - Partners return papers. Students read the feedback silently.
   - *"Tomorrow you'll revise based on this feedback and write your final portfolio piece."*

#### Differentiation
- **Below level**: Use the writing frame as a direct fill-in-the-blank. Teacher or aide conferences with each student during drafting to dictate or co-construct sentences. Goal: opinion + 2 reasons + closing. Accept shorter sentences.
- **On level**: Use the frame as a guide but write in full paragraphs (not fill-in). 2–3 reasons with transition words. Peer feedback as described.
- **Above level**: Write without the frame (use it only as a checklist afterward). 3 reasons with examples or evidence. Begin addressing a counterargument: *"Some people might say ___, but I think ___ because ___."* Peer feedback includes: *"What's the strongest reason and why?"*

### Exploration & Enrichment (12:00–1:00)

**Activity**: Design a Solution — STEM Challenge

**Overview**: Students apply the design thinking process to solve a real-world problem. They work in teams to design, build, and test a solution using simple materials.

**Materials**:
- Per group (3 groups of 3–4 students):
  - Cardboard pieces (assorted sizes)
  - Craft sticks (20)
  - Tape (masking and clear)
  - String (3 feet)
  - Recyclables (bottles, cups, cardboard tubes)
  - Foil (1 sheet)
  - Straws (10)
  - Rubber bands (5)
  - Scissors
- Design planning sheet (per group: Problem, Brainstorm ideas, Chosen design sketch, Materials list, Test results, What we'd change)
- Timer
- Rulers (for measuring — connects to math)

**The Challenge** (choose one or let groups choose):
- **Option A — "Water Carrier"**: Design a device that can carry a cup of water 3 feet without spilling.
- **Option B — "Egg Protector"**: Design a structure that protects a hard-boiled egg when dropped from table height.
- **Option C — "Bridge Builder"**: Design a bridge that spans 12 inches and holds at least 5 books.

**Schedule**:

1. **Challenge Introduction (5 min)**:
   - Present the challenge options. Each group selects (or is assigned) a challenge.
   - *"You have 25 minutes to design, build, and test. Use the design thinking steps we learned this morning."*
   - Distribute materials and planning sheets.

2. **Design Phase (8 min)**:
   - Groups discuss the problem and brainstorm solutions (Step 1–2).
   - Sketch their design on the planning sheet (Step 3).
   - List the materials they'll use.
   - Teacher circulates: *"What's your plan? How will this solve the problem? Have you thought about what could go wrong?"*

3. **Build Phase (12 min)**:
   - Groups build their prototypes (Step 4).
   - Teacher reminds about teamwork: *"Everyone has a job. If you disagree, use 'I think we should try ___ because ___' — just like persuasive writing!"*
   - 5-minute warning: *"Start finishing up. You need to be ready to test."*

4. **Test Phase (10 min)**:
   - Each group tests their design (Step 5).
   - Class watches and cheers.
   - After each test: *"Did it work? What happened? What would you change?"* (Step 6)
   - Groups record results on their planning sheet.
   - Math connection: If applicable, measure distances, heights, or weights. Record data.

5. **Reflection & Connection (15 min)**:
   - Groups share: *"Our problem was ___. We designed ___. It worked / didn't work because ___. Next time we would ___."*
   - Connect to real-world engineering: *"Real engineers fail many times before they succeed. That's called iteration."*
   - Connect to writing: *"In your opinion essay, you're building an argument. You plan it, draft it, test it (peer feedback), and improve it — just like this!"*
   - Clean up materials.

6. **Closing Circle (10 min)**:
   - *"What was the most challenging part of the design challenge? What strategy did your group use?"*
   - Self-monitoring checklist.
   - Preview: *"Tomorrow — grocery store math! We're budgeting a real meal."*
   - Dismissal at 1:00.

**Differentiation**:
- **Below level**: Assign Option A (simplest). Provide a "starter design" sketch they can modify. Teacher joins the group for scaffolded support during build phase.
- **On level**: Choose their challenge. Full design thinking process with planning sheet.
- **Above level**: Choose the hardest challenge. Add constraints: *"Your design must use no more than $5 worth of materials"* (assign prices to each item for a budgeting challenge). Must write a 3-sentence reflection explaining their design choices.
