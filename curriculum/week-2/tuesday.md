### Math Block (9:20–10:20)

**Objective**: Students will build, draw, and interpret arrays as a model for multiplication, identifying the number of rows and columns and writing corresponding multiplication equations. Students will discover that arrays show the commutative property (3 x 4 = 4 x 3). (IEP Goal: Math Computation — represent multiplication using arrays.)

**Materials**:
- Square tiles (50+ per group)
- Snap cubes / unifix cubes
- Grid paper (1-inch squares, 2 sheets per student)
- Dry-erase boards + markers
- Chart paper for anchor chart: "Arrays"
- Array recording sheet (1 per student)
- Counters (for students who prefer them to tiles)

#### Warm-Up/Activation (5 min)
- **"Egg Carton Question"**: Draw (or show) an egg carton on the board — 2 rows of 6 eggs.
- *"How many eggs are in a carton? How do you know?"*
- *"Yesterday we said '2 groups of 6.' But look at how they're arranged — they're in ROWS and COLUMNS. That arrangement is called an ARRAY."*
- *"An array is equal groups arranged in rows and columns. You see arrays everywhere — windows on a building, tiles on a floor, seats in a theater."*

#### Direct Instruction (15 min) — CRA Level: Concrete

1. **Building Arrays (8 min)**:
   - On the document camera or a flat surface visible to all, build an array with square tiles: **3 rows of 4**.
   - *"How many rows?"* (3) *"How many in each row?"* (4) *"We write: 3 x 4 = 12."*
   - Label: **rows x columns = product**
   - Build together: students use tiles at their desks to make the same 3 x 4 array.
   - *"Now turn your array sideways — without taking it apart, just rotate it."*
   - *"What do you see now?"* (4 rows of 3) *"4 x 3 = ?"* (12!)
   - *"WAIT — 3 x 4 and 4 x 3 both equal 12? That's called the COMMUTATIVE PROPERTY. The order doesn't change the answer!"*
   - Add to anchor chart: **3 x 4 = 4 x 3 (Commutative Property — flip the array!)**

2. **More Array Builds (7 min)**:
   - Build these arrays together:
     - **5 x 2** (5 rows of 2) = 10. Turn it: **2 x 5** = 10.
     - **1 x 7** (1 row of 7) = 7. Turn it: **7 x 1** = 7. *"When one factor is 1, it's just a single row or column!"*
     - **4 x 5** (4 rows of 5) = 20. *"Skip count the rows: 5, 10, 15, 20."*
   - For each, students build at their desks, then record on dry-erase boards: rows x columns = product.

#### Guided Practice (20 min)

**Activity: "Array Builders" — Partner Work**

- Each pair gets 40 square tiles and grid paper.
- **Round 1 — Build from equation (10 min)**:
  Teacher writes an equation on the board. Students build the array with tiles, then trace it on grid paper by coloring in squares.
  - 2 x 5 = ___ (build it, count it: 10)
  - 3 x 3 = ___ (build it: 9 — *"Hey, this one is a SQUARE! Same number of rows and columns."*)
  - 5 x 4 = ___ (build it: 20)
  - 2 x 10 = ___ (build it: 20 — *"Whoa, this one also equals 20! Different array, same product."*)
  - 6 x 2 = ___ (build it: 12)

- **Round 2 — Write from array (10 min)**:
  Teacher shows an array (drawn on chart paper or built with large tiles). Students write the equation on dry-erase boards.
  - Show 4 rows of 4: students write 4 x 4 = 16
  - Show 3 rows of 5: students write 3 x 5 = 15
  - Show 1 row of 8: students write 1 x 8 = 8
  - Show 5 rows of 5: students write 5 x 5 = 25
  - Show 2 rows of 7: students write 2 x 7 = 14

- After Round 2, discuss: *"Did anyone write the equation a different way?"* (e.g., 5 x 3 instead of 3 x 5). *"Both are correct! That's the commutative property."*

#### Independent Practice (10 min)
- **Array Recording Sheet** (10 problems):
  - Problems 1–4: Draw the array on grid paper and find the product.
    - 2 x 6, 4 x 3, 5 x 5, 3 x 7
  - Problems 5–7: An array picture is given. Write two multiplication equations for it (using commutative property).
    - Array of 3x4 → 3 x 4 = 12 AND 4 x 3 = 12
  - Problems 8–10: Word problems using arrays.
    - "A garden has 4 rows of tomato plants. Each row has 5 plants. How many tomato plants are there?" (Draw the array, write the equation.)
    - "A window has 3 rows and 2 columns of panes. How many panes of glass?" (Draw it, write it.)
    - "A parking lot has 5 rows with 10 cars in each row. How many cars?" (Draw it, write it.)

#### Closure (5 min)
- **Exit Ticket** (dry-erase boards):
  - Draw an array for 3 x 5. How many total? (15)
  - *"Write two equations for this array."* (3 x 5 = 15 and 5 x 3 = 15)
- Connection to enrichment: *"When you look at buildings and art today, watch for arrays — rows and columns of windows, bricks, tiles. They're multiplication in disguise!"*

#### Data Collection
- Collect Array Recording Sheet — score per section (draw arrays /4, write equations /3, word problems /3)
- Note who grasped the commutative property
- Note who still needs tiles vs. who can draw arrays on grid paper (moving toward representational)
- Exit ticket: photograph boards

#### Differentiation
- **Below level (Group A)**: Use only factors up to 5. Provide pre-drawn grid paper with rows/columns labeled. Keep tiles available for all problems. For word problems, highlight the key numbers and draw the groups.
- **On level (Group B)**: Complete all problems as described. Tiles available as a choice.
- **Above level (Group C)**: After finishing, challenge: *"Can you build all the arrays that equal 24? How many can you find?"* (1x24, 2x12, 3x8, 4x6, 6x4, 8x3, 12x2, 24x1) — This previews factors.

#### Part 1: OG Lesson — Vowel Teams ee/ea (10:30–11:00)

**Objective**: Students will read and spell words containing the vowel teams ee (feet) and ea (beach), understanding that both make the long /ē/ sound. Students will learn red words: again, because. (IEP Goal: Decoding — vowel team syllable type.)

**Materials**:
- OG phonogram cards (Week 1 deck + ai, ay, ee, ea)
- Red word cards: again, because (+ review: could, would, should)
- Sand/salt trays
- Dry-erase boards + markers
- Word list: 20 ee/ea words (printed, 1 per student)
- Decodable text with ai/ay and ee/ea words

**OG Lesson Structure**:

1. **Review — Phonogram Card Drill (5 min)**:
   - Rapid drill of all cards including ai and ay (taught yesterday).
   - When ai appears: students say "/ā/ as in rain — middle of the word."
   - When ay appears: students say "/ā/ as in play — end of the word."
   - Quick dictation review (3 words): **brain, spray, wait** — students write on dry-erase boards. Check for ai/ay spelling accuracy.

2. **New Concept — Vowel Teams ee and ea (10 min)**:
   - Hold up the **ee** card. *"Another vowel team! Two e's together say /ē/ — long e."*
   - Key word: **feet**. Students echo: *"e-e says /ē/ as in feet."*
   - Hold up the **ea** card. *"This one also says /ē/. Key word: **beach**."*
   - Students echo: *"e-a says /ē/ as in beach."*
   - *"Unlike ai/ay, there's no simple rule for when to use ee vs. ea. Both can appear in the middle of a word. You'll learn which words use which spelling through practice and reading."*
   - Write the generalization on chart paper:
     - **ee** = long /ē/ (feet, tree, sleep, green, wheel)
     - **ea** = long /ē/ (beach, read, team, clean, dream)
     - *"When in doubt, try ee first — it's more common. If the word doesn't look right, try ea."*

   **Multisensory Drill**:
   - **Sky writing**: Stand. Write e-e in the air with full arm while saying /ē/. Then e-a while saying /ē/. Repeat 3 times each.
   - **Sand tray**: Trace ee, smooth, trace ea. Say /ē/ each time.
   - **Phonogram card sort**: Give students a stack of 10 word cards (5 ee, 5 ea). Sort into two piles by spelling pattern.
     - ee pile: feet, tree, sleep, green, wheel
     - ea pile: beach, read, team, clean, dream

3. **Word List Practice (8 min)**:
   - **ee words** (read aloud):
     - see, tree, free, bee, feet, sleep, green, teeth, wheel, street
   - **ea words** (read aloud):
     - eat, beach, read, team, clean, dream, peach, teach, bead, cream
   - Finger-tap each word. For "sleep": /s/-/l/-/ē/-/p/ (4 sounds, 5 letters).
   - **Dictation practice** (dry-erase boards — choose ee or ea):
     - "green" (ee), "beach" (ea), "sleep" (ee), "dream" (ea), "teeth" (ee), "clean" (ea)
   - Students hold up boards. Teacher checks. Discuss any that were tricky.
   - **Mixed vowel team review** — read these words (ai/ay AND ee/ea mixed):
     - train, play, street, clean, rain, spray, dream, paint, teeth, clay

4. **Red Words — again, because (7 min)**:
   - Hold up **again**. *"This word looks like it should say /ă-gān/ but it says /ə-gĕn/. The 'ai' doesn't follow our ai rule here — that's why it's a red word!"*
   - Hold up **because**. *"This is a common word that's tricky to spell: b-e-c-a-u-s-e. The tricky part is the 'au' in the middle."*
   - For each word:
     - Teacher says it in a sentence: *"I want to play again." / "I like summer because it's sunny."*
     - Students repeat, spell aloud, write in sand tray 3 times.
     - Underline tricky parts: **again** (the ai says /ĕ/), **because** (the au and the se at the end).
     - Heart tap: spell from memory.
   - **Review all 5 red words** from Mon + Tue: could, would, should, again, because. Flash cards — students read and air-spell.
   - Memory sentence: *"I could play again because I would and should."* — Students say it, then write it on dry-erase boards. Check spelling of all 5 red words in context.

**Data Collection**:
- Tally ee/ea word reading accuracy (out of 20)
- Dictation accuracy: did students choose the correct ee or ea spelling?
- Mixed vowel team reading: accuracy on ai/ay words from yesterday (retained?) + new ee/ea
- Red word cumulative accuracy: all 5 words so far

#### Part 2: Shared Paragraph Writing (11:00–11:30)

**Objective**: Students will co-construct a paragraph about patterns with the teacher, applying the topic sentence / detail sentences / closing sentence structure. (IEP Goal: Written Expression — paragraph structure with guided support.)

**Materials**:
- Chart paper and marker
- Hamburger Paragraph anchor chart (from yesterday)
- Sticky notes (3 colors matching highlighter colors)
- Composition notebooks
- Paragraph checklist (laminated)

**Lesson**:

1. **Review Paragraph Structure (3 min)**:
   - Point to the Hamburger Paragraph anchor chart. *"Yesterday I wrote a paragraph and you labeled the parts. Today, WE write one together."*
   - Quick recall: *"What are the three parts?"* (Topic sentence, detail sentences, closing sentence.)
   - *"Our topic today: patterns in buildings or art. Think about the windows on a building, the tiles on a floor, the designs on a quilt."*

2. **Shared Writing — Co-Constructing the Paragraph (17 min)**:
   - Teacher holds the marker. Students provide the ideas. Teacher guides structure.

   **Step 1: Topic Sentence** (3 min)
   - *"We need a sentence that tells the reader what our paragraph is about. What should it say?"*
   - Take 3–4 suggestions. Vote on the best one or combine ideas.
   - Example outcome: **"You can find amazing patterns in buildings all around you."**
   - Write it on chart paper. *"Does this tell the reader what we're writing about? Yes! It's our topic sentence."*
   - Place a yellow sticky note next to it.

   **Step 2: Detail Sentences** (8 min)
   - *"Now we need 2–3 details. What are specific examples of patterns in buildings or art?"*
   - Take suggestions. Guide students to be specific:
     - Instead of "Buildings have patterns," push for: **"Many buildings have rows and columns of windows that look like arrays."**
     - *"Great! What else?"* → **"Some floors are covered in tiles that repeat the same shape, like squares or hexagons."**
     - *"One more detail."* → **"Artists in many cultures use geometric patterns — like circles, triangles, and stars — to decorate walls and ceilings."**
   - Write each on chart paper. Place green sticky notes next to them.
   - After each sentence, check: *"Does this sentence give a detail about our topic? Does it support our topic sentence?"*

   **Step 3: Closing Sentence** (3 min)
   - *"Now we need to wrap it up. Remember, we don't just copy the topic sentence. How can we say the main idea in a new way?"*
   - Take suggestions. Guide toward a satisfying ending.
   - Example: **"Once you start looking, you'll see that patterns are part of what makes buildings and art beautiful."**
   - Place a pink sticky note next to it.

   **Step 4: Read and Revise** (3 min)
   - Read the full paragraph aloud as a class.
   - *"Does it sound complete? Does every sentence connect to the topic? Is there anything we want to change?"*
   - Make any edits the class agrees on.

3. **Copy into Journals (10 min)**:
   - Students copy the shared paragraph into their notebooks under the heading: **"Our Paragraph About Patterns in Buildings"**
   - Remind: indent the first line, capital letters, end punctuation.
   - Below the paragraph, students use their paragraph checklist to self-assess (check off each item).
   - **Extension**: If students finish early, they add one more detail sentence of their own at the bottom (labeled "My extra detail").

**Data Collection**:
- Observe who contributed ideas during shared writing (participation)
- Note quality of suggestions: who offered specific details vs. vague statements?
- Check copied paragraphs: accuracy of copying, handwriting fluency, correct indentation
- Note who added an extra detail sentence and its quality

**Differentiation**:
- **Below level**: Teacher provides a partially filled paragraph with blanks: "You can find amazing patterns in ___. Many buildings have ___. Some floors are covered in ___. Once you start looking, ___." Students fill in the blanks.
- **On level**: Copy the shared paragraph and complete the checklist.
- **Above level**: After copying, write a second paragraph independently about a different type of pattern (in nature, in music, in food, etc.).

### Exploration & Enrichment (12:00–1:00)

**Activity**: Patterns in Art & Architecture — Virtual Museum Tour + Design Project

**Enrichment Category**: Museums & Culture (Tuesday theme)

**Overview**: Students explore patterns in art and architecture through projected images from diverse cultures and time periods. They identify the mathematical patterns (symmetry, repetition, arrays) and then create their own geometric pattern artwork. Connects directly to arrays (math) and descriptive writing.

**Materials**:
- Projected images (printed large on chart paper or shown on screen if technology available):
  1. Islamic geometric tile patterns (e.g., Alhambra palace in Spain)
  2. Quilt patterns (e.g., African American quilting traditions — Gee's Bend quilts)
  3. Greek temple columns (repeating columns = arrays)
  4. Gothic cathedral rose windows (symmetry, radial patterns)
  5. Modern skyscraper windows (arrays — rows and columns)
  6. Navajo weaving patterns (geometric repetition)
- Drawing paper (9x12, 1 per student)
- Rulers
- Colored pencils and markers
- Grid paper (optional, for students who want structure)
- Pencils for sketching

**Schedule**:

1. **Virtual Museum Tour (15 min)**:
   - Display each image for about 2 minutes. For each:
   - **Image 1 — Islamic Tile Patterns**:
     - *"These tiles are from a palace in Spain built almost 700 years ago. What patterns do you see?"* (Repetition, symmetry, geometric shapes.)
     - *"The artists used math to create these designs. Every shape fits together perfectly."*
   - **Image 2 — Gee's Bend Quilts**:
     - *"These quilts were made by African American women in Alabama. Each one tells a story through patterns. What shapes do you see repeating?"*
     - Math connection: *"If this quilt block has 4 rows of 4 squares, how many squares in one block?"* (4 x 4 = 16)
   - **Image 3 — Greek Temple Columns**:
     - *"Count the columns. If the front has 8 columns and the side has 17 columns, what's the total?"* (Skip counting or multiplication.)
     - *"The space between each column is exactly the same. That's a repeating pattern."*
   - **Image 4 — Rose Window**:
     - *"This window has symmetry — it looks the same no matter which way you turn it. How many lines of symmetry can you find?"*
   - **Image 5 — Skyscraper Windows**:
     - *"This is an array! How many rows of windows? How many columns? Write a multiplication equation."*
   - **Image 6 — Navajo Weaving**:
     - *"The Navajo people are master weavers. What geometric shapes do you see? Where is the symmetry?"*
   - For each image, students sketch one pattern observation in their nature/enrichment journal (quick 30-second sketch + 1 label).

2. **Design Your Own Pattern Art (25 min)**:
   - *"Now it's YOUR turn to be an artist and a mathematician."*
   - Task: Create a piece of art that uses at least ONE of these pattern types:
     - Repeating geometric shapes
     - Symmetry (fold your paper in half to plan both sides)
     - An array of shapes or colors
   - **Step 1** (5 min): Plan — sketch lightly in pencil. Decide on your pattern type and shapes.
   - **Step 2** (15 min): Create — use rulers for straight lines, colored pencils or markers for color. Fill the page.
   - **Step 3** (5 min): Label — on the back, write:
     - Your name
     - The type(s) of pattern you used
     - A multiplication equation that matches something in your artwork (e.g., "I have 4 rows of 3 stars = 4 x 3 = 12 stars")
   - Teacher circulates, asking: *"What pattern type is this? How does your design repeat? Can you write a multiplication equation for it?"*

3. **Gallery Walk & Share (10 min)**:
   - Students display artwork on desks.
   - Gallery walk: students walk around with a sticky note. Each student places their sticky note on one artwork they admire, writing one word to describe it.
   - 3–4 students share their artwork with the class, explaining their pattern choices.
   - *"You are pattern artists! These will go up on our 'Patterns Everywhere' wall."*

4. **Closing Circle (10 min)**:
   - *"What was the most surprising pattern you saw today?"* (3–4 shares)
   - Connect to tomorrow: *"Tomorrow we're going to find patterns in CODING — you'll learn how computers use patterns to do amazing things."*
   - Self-monitoring checklist: complete for the day.
   - Red word rapid review: flash all 5 red words (could, would, should, again, because). Students read and spell one on their dry-erase boards (teacher picks one at random).
   - Dismissal at 1:00.

**Academic Connection**:
- **Math**: Arrays in architecture, counting rows/columns, writing multiplication equations from real-world patterns.
- **Reading/Writing**: Descriptive vocabulary about visual patterns, observation notes in journals, labeling with mathematical language.
- **Social Studies/Culture**: Exposure to diverse artistic traditions; understanding that pattern-making is universal across cultures.
