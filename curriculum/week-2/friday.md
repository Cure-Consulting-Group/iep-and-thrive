#### Guided Practice — Review Stations (30 min total: 3 stations x 10 min)

Divide students into 3 groups. Rotate through all stations.

**Station 1: Equal Groups & Arrays Word Problems (Teacher-facilitated when possible)**
- Materials: Word problem task cards (8 cards), dry-erase boards, counters/tiles available
- Task cards:
  1. "A classroom has 5 rows of desks with 4 desks in each row. How many desks?" (Draw array, write equation: 5 x 4 = 20)
  2. "A baker puts 2 cookies on each plate. She fills 8 plates. How many cookies?" (Equal groups: 8 x 2 = 16)
  3. "A parking lot has 10 rows with 5 cars each. How many cars?" (10 x 5 = 50)
  4. "There are 3 teams. Each team has 0 points so far. What is the total score?" (3 x 0 = 0)
  5. "A garden has 4 rows of flowers. Each row has 5 flowers. How many flowers? Write TWO equations." (4 x 5 = 20, 5 x 4 = 20)
  6. "Grandpa has 1 gift for each of his 6 grandchildren. How many gifts?" (6 x 1 = 6)
  7. "A box holds 5 muffins. There are 7 boxes. How many muffins?" (7 x 5 = 35)
  8. "A wall has 10 rows of bricks with 10 bricks in each row. How many bricks?" (10 x 10 = 100)
- Students solve at least 4 cards. Must show a model (drawing, array, or equal groups) for each.

**Station 2: Multiplication Strategy Match Game**
- Materials: Memory/matching cards — 24 cards total (12 pairs). Each pair has a fact on one card and the product on the other.
  - Cards: 2x6/12, 5x4/20, 10x3/30, 0x8/0, 1x5/5, 2x9/18, 5x7/35, 10x6/60, 2x2/4, 5x10/50, 1x9/9, 0x4/0
- Game: Lay cards face down. Take turns flipping 2 cards. If a fact matches its product, keep the pair. Most pairs wins.
- Variation: If memory game is too easy, play "Speed Match" — lay all cards face up, race to find and match pairs.

**Station 3: Fact Fluency Practice — Beat Your Score**
- Materials: x0, x1, x2, x5, x10 fact cards (shuffled), timer, recording sheet
- Task: Student flashes cards to self (or partner). Sort into "know it" and "still learning" piles. Record counts.
- Goal: Beat yesterday's "Beat the Clock" score.
- After sorting, practice the "still learning" pile 3 times through.
- Record: "Today I know ___ out of ___ facts. My 3 trickiest facts are: ___."

#### Part 1: OG Cumulative Review — "Vowel Team Relay" (10:30–10:45)

**Objective**: Students will demonstrate knowledge of all 6 vowel teams and 7 red words through a fast-paced review game. (IEP Goal: Decoding — cumulative review.)

**Materials**:
- OG phonogram cards (full deck)
- Dry-erase boards + markers
- Sand/salt trays
- Word cards: 30 words containing ai, ay, ee, ea, oa, ow (5 from each team, shuffled)
- Red word cards (all 7)
- Team score chart on board

**Game: "Vowel Team Relay"**:

1. **Round 1 — Phonogram Speed Round (3 min)**:
   - Divide class into 2 teams.
   - Flash vowel team cards. Teams alternate answering (Team A, Team B, Team A...).
   - Correct sound + key word = 1 point. Example: Teacher shows "ai" → student says "/ā/, rain" = 1 point.
   - Go through all 6 vowel teams 3 times. Tally points.

2. **Round 2 — Word Reading Race (5 min)**:
   - Teacher holds up a word card. Both teams read silently. First team to have ALL members holding up the correct vowel team on their dry-erase boards (ai, ay, ee, ea, oa, or ow) earns a point.
   - Words: brain, display, sweep, cream, float, elbow, chain, relay, cheese, leaf, groan, pillow, stain, crayon, freezing, repeat, toaster, shadow
   - After identifying the vowel team, one team member reads the word aloud.

3. **Round 3 — Red Word Dictation Sprint (4 min)**:
   - Teacher says a red word. Both teams write it on dry-erase boards. Hold up. Correct spelling = 1 point per team (both can earn a point).
   - Words: could, would, should, again, because, people, friend
   - Go through all 7 words. Then dictate 2 sentences with multiple red words:
     - "My friend could play again." (could, friend, again)
     - "People should read because it is fun." (people, should, because)
   - Check sentence spelling carefully.

4. **Celebration (3 min)**:
   - Count total points. Both teams earn Thrive Bucks: winners get $3, other team gets $2.
   - *"You just reviewed an entire WEEK of phonics in 15 minutes. Your brains are incredible."*

**Data Collection**:
- Note team accuracy during Round 2 (vowel team identification) — which teams are most/least accurate as a class?
- Red word dictation: tally per student — all 7 correct? Which words are still missed?
- Sentence dictation: check red word spelling in sentence context (harder than in isolation)

#### Activity 1: Tessellation Art (12:00–12:20)

**Overview**: Students create tessellation artwork — patterns where shapes fit together with no gaps or overlaps. This is the ultimate "pattern everywhere" activity, connecting math (geometry, arrays, repetition) to art.

**Materials**:
- Tessellation templates (pre-cut cardboard shapes — squares, triangles, hexagons — each about 2 inches)
- Drawing paper (9x12, white)
- Colored pencils, markers, or crayons
- Scissors (if students are cutting their own templates from cardstock)
- Examples of tessellation art (M.C. Escher prints, Islamic tile patterns from Tuesday)
- Rulers
- Pencils for tracing

**Instructions**:

1. **Introduction (3 min)**:
   - Show an example of a tessellation. *"A tessellation is a pattern of shapes that fit together like puzzle pieces — no gaps, no overlaps."*
   - Show a simple example: a grid of squares (that's a tessellation!). Then a honeycomb of hexagons. Then an Escher-style tessellation.
   - *"You've seen tessellations all week — floor tiles, brick walls, honeycombs. Today you create your own."*

2. **Create (15 min)**:
   - **Option A — Template Tracing** (simpler):
     - Students choose a shape template (square, triangle, or hexagon).
     - Trace the shape across the entire page, fitting shapes edge to edge with no gaps.
     - Color with a repeating color pattern (e.g., alternate red-blue-red-blue, or create a 3-color pattern).
   - **Option B — Custom Shape** (advanced):
     - Start with a square template. Cut a shape from one side and tape it to the opposite side (classic Escher technique).
     - Trace the new custom shape across the page.
     - Add details inside each shape (e.g., if it looks like a bird, draw eyes and feathers).
   - Teacher circulates: *"Is your pattern repeating? Are there any gaps? What color pattern are you using?"*

3. **Label & Display (2 min)**:
   - On the back, write: name, date, type of tessellation, one sentence: "My tessellation uses ___ shapes in a ___ pattern."
   - Display on the "Patterns Everywhere" wall.
   - **Portfolio piece**: Teacher photographs each tessellation for the portfolio.

**Academic Connection**:
- **Math**: Geometry (shape properties), spatial reasoning, repeating patterns, arrays (a grid tessellation is an array!).
- **Art**: Design principles — repetition, rhythm, color theory.

#### Activity 2: Pattern Poetry (12:20–12:35)

**Overview**: Students write a short poem about patterns, using a structured poetry form that IS a pattern.

**Materials**:
- Poetry form templates (printed, 1 per student)
- Pencils
- Examples of pattern poems (posted on chart paper)
- Composition notebooks or separate paper

**Poetry Forms** (students choose one):

**Option 1 — Acrostic Poem** (spell PATTERN down the side):
> **P**etals on a flower, counting five by five
> **A**rrays of windows on every building
> **T**essellations covering the floor
> **T**rees with branches splitting and splitting
> **E**verywhere I look, I see them now
> **R**epeating, repeating, never stopping
> **N**ature's secret code, hiding in plain sight

**Option 2 — Cinquain** (2-4-6-8-2 syllable pattern):
> Patterns (2)
> Everywhere (4)
> Repeating shapes and sounds (6)
> Numbers, words, and colors dance in line (8)
> Beauty (2)

**Option 3 — Repeating Poem** (the structure itself is a pattern):
> I see a pattern in the ___,
> I see a pattern in the ___,
> I see a pattern in the ___,
> And now I see them everywhere.

**Instructions**:
1. Show the 3 examples (3 min). *"Poetry uses patterns too — the way lines are structured, the rhythm, the rhyme. Today, your poem IS a pattern."*
2. Students choose a form and write their poem (10 min). They may draft on scratch paper first, then write a final version in their notebook.
3. 2–3 students read their poems aloud (2 min). Snap fingers to appreciate (poetry snaps!).

**Portfolio piece**: The poem page from the notebook (or a separate paper) goes in the portfolio.
