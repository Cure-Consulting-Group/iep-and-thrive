### Math Block (9:20–10:20)

**Objective**: Students will apply the x5 (clock/nickel strategy) and x10 (place value strategy) multiplication facts using skip counting, number line hops, and beginning abstract notation. Students will solve multiplication word problems using equal groups and array strategies. (IEP Goal: Math Computation — multiplication fact strategies x5 and x10.)

**Materials**:
- Dry-erase boards + markers
- Number lines (desktop, 0–100)
- Grid paper
- Analog clocks (student-size, 1 per student or pair)
- Play money (nickels and dimes)
- Chart paper for anchor chart (continued): "Multiplication Strategies"
- "x5 and x10 Facts" practice sheet (1 per student)
- Multiplication fact cards (x5, x10 sets + review of x0, x1, x2)

#### Warm-Up/Activation (5 min)
- **"Nickel Jar"**: Draw a jar on the board. *"Every time I drop a nickel in, tell me how much money is in the jar."*
- Drop nickels one at a time (draw circles): 5, 10, 15, 20, 25, 30, 35, 40, 45, 50.
- *"I dropped in 8 nickels. 8 groups of 5 cents = 8 x 5 = 40 cents."*
- *"What if they were dimes?"* (80 cents — 8 x 10 = 80.)
- *"Today you'll learn tricks for multiplying by 5 and by 10."*

#### Direct Instruction (15 min) — CRA Level: Representational → Abstract

1. **The x5 Strategy — Clock and Skip Count (8 min)**:
   - **Number Line Hops** (representational): Draw a number line 0–50 on chart paper.
   - *"Let's make hops of 5."* Draw arcs: 0→5→10→15→20→25→30→35→40→45→50.
   - Count the hops: *"3 hops of 5 = 3 x 5 = 15. 7 hops of 5 = 7 x 5 = 35."*
   - Students practice on their desktop number lines: *"Show me 4 hops of 5. Where do you land?"* (20 — 4 x 5 = 20)

   - **Clock Strategy**: Give each student (or pair) a student clock.
   - *"Every number on the clock face is a x5 fact!"*
   - Point to the 1: *"1 x 5 = 5 minutes."* Point to 2: *"2 x 5 = 10."* Continue around.
   - Students write x5 facts on dry-erase boards as teacher points to clock numbers:
     - Point to 6 → 6 x 5 = 30
     - Point to 9 → 9 x 5 = 45
     - Point to 4 → 4 x 5 = 20
     - Point to 12 → 12 x 5 = 60

   - **Pattern observation**: *"Look at all the x5 products. What do you notice about the ones digit?"* (They all end in 0 or 5!)
   - Add to anchor chart: **x5: skip count by 5s, use the clock, products always end in 0 or 5**

2. **The x10 Strategy — Place Value (7 min)**:
   - *"Multiplying by 10 is the easiest multiplication trick."*
   - Write: 3 x 10 = ? Students predict. (30)
   - Write: 7 x 10 = ? (70) 5 x 10 = ? (50) 9 x 10 = ? (90)
   - *"What's the trick?"* (Just add a zero! Put the number in the tens place.)
   - *"WHY does this work?"* Use place value: 3 x 10 means 3 groups of 10. Three tens = 30. The 3 moves from the ones place to the tens place.
   - Show on a place value chart:
     - 4 x 10: four groups of ten = 4 in the tens column, 0 in the ones = 40
   - Students practice on dry-erase boards — rapid fire:
     - 6 x 10 (60), 2 x 10 (20), 8 x 10 (80), 1 x 10 (10), 10 x 10 (100)
   - *"10 x 10 = 100! That's a whole hundred. One hundred is just 10 groups of 10."*
   - Add to anchor chart: **x10: add a zero (move the digit to the tens place). Products always end in 0.**

   - **Compare x5 and x10**: Write side by side:
     - 4 x 5 = 20 | 4 x 10 = 40
     - 6 x 5 = 30 | 6 x 10 = 60
     - *"What do you notice?"* (x10 is always double x5!)
     - *"So if you know 7 x 5 = 35, then 7 x 10 must be DOUBLE that: 70."*

#### Guided Practice (20 min)

**Activity: "Fact Strategy Match" — Whole Group then Partner Practice**

- **Round 1 — Strategy Identification (8 min)**: Teacher shows a fact card. Students hold up a finger to indicate the strategy:
  - 1 finger = Zero Property (x0)
  - 2 fingers = Identity Property (x1)
  - 3 fingers = Doubles (x2)
  - 4 fingers = Clock/Skip Count (x5)
  - 5 fingers = Place Value (x10)

  Then they solve on dry-erase boards:
  - 5 x 10 (place value) → 50
  - 3 x 5 (clock) → 15
  - 0 x 8 (zero) → 0
  - 2 x 9 (doubles) → 18
  - 7 x 5 (clock) → 35
  - 10 x 4 (place value) → 40
  - 1 x 6 (identity) → 6
  - 5 x 8 (clock) → 40
  - 10 x 10 (place value) → 100
  - 2 x 5 (doubles OR clock — both work!) → 10

- **Round 2 — Partner Fact Drill (12 min)**:
  - Each pair gets a shuffled deck of x0, x1, x2, x5, and x10 fact cards (about 50 cards total).
  - **Game: "Beat the Clock"**
    1. Set a timer for 2 minutes.
    2. One student flashes cards. Partner answers.
    3. Correct answers go in the "got it" pile. Wrong answers go in the "practice" pile.
    4. Count the "got it" pile. Record the number.
    5. Switch roles. Repeat.
    6. Each student's goal: beat their OWN score from the first round in a second round (growth mindset — competing against yourself).
  - After the game, each student writes their 3 trickiest facts on a sticky note and puts it inside their folder (personal practice targets).

#### Independent Practice (10 min)
- **"x5 and x10 Facts" Practice Sheet** (15 problems):
  - Section A — x5 Facts (5 problems): Solve and show your strategy (number line, clock, or skip count).
    - 3 x 5, 6 x 5, 9 x 5, 5 x 5, 8 x 5
  - Section B — x10 Facts (5 problems): Solve and explain the pattern.
    - 4 x 10, 7 x 10, 10 x 10, 2 x 10, 9 x 10
  - Section C — Mixed Review (5 problems): All strategies (x0, x1, x2, x5, x10).
    - 5 x 0 (0), 2 x 7 (14), 10 x 6 (60), 1 x 8 (8), 5 x 7 (35)

#### Closure (5 min)
- **Exit Ticket** (dry-erase boards):
  - *"What is 6 x 5?"* (30) *"How did you know?"* (Clock: 6 on the clock = 30 minutes)
  - *"What is 8 x 10?"* (80) *"How did you know?"* (Add a zero / 8 in the tens place)
- *"You now know strategies for x0, x1, x2, x5, and x10. That's 50 facts! Tomorrow we'll see how fast you've gotten."*
- Connect to enrichment: *"In cooking today, we'll use x2 (doubling a recipe) and you might even see x5 and x10 when measuring."*

#### Data Collection
- Collect x5 and x10 Practice Sheet — score per section
- Record "Beat the Clock" scores (both rounds) — look for growth within the session
- Note students' personal "3 trickiest facts" — common trouble spots across the class?
- Exit ticket: photograph boards

#### Differentiation
- **Below level (Group A)**: Focus on x5 only (x10 as extension). Provide clocks and number lines throughout. For the practice sheet, allow students to draw hops on a number line to solve. Reduce mixed review to 3 problems.
- **On level (Group B)**: Complete all sections. Clocks and number lines available as a choice.
- **Above level (Group C)**: After completing the sheet, introduce x5 and x10 with larger numbers: *"What is 12 x 5? 15 x 10? Can you use the same strategies?"* Also: *"If 6 x 5 = 30 and 6 x 10 = 60, what is 6 x 15?"* (6 x 10 + 6 x 5 = 60 + 30 = 90 — preview of distributive property.)

#### Part 1: OG Cumulative Vowel Team Review + Connected Text (10:30–10:50)

**Objective**: Students will read and spell words containing all six vowel teams (ai/ay, ee/ea, oa/ow) with ≥80% accuracy, and read a connected text passage containing these patterns fluently. (IEP Goal: Decoding — cumulative vowel team review and application.)

**Materials**:
- OG phonogram cards (full deck including all 6 vowel team cards)
- Dry-erase boards + markers
- All 7 red word cards
- Decodable text passage: "A Day at the Beach" (printed, 1 per student — see below)
- Highlighters (6 colors or 3 colors with underlining)

**OG Lesson Structure**:

1. **Phonogram Card Rapid Drill (3 min)**:
   - Speed round through all cards. Students respond chorally.
   - Emphasis on vowel teams: ai (/ā/ — middle), ay (/ā/ — end), ee (/ē/), ea (/ē/), oa (/ō/ — middle), ow (/ō/ — end).
   - *"You've learned 6 vowel teams in 3 days. Let's see how sharp you are."*

2. **Mixed Vowel Team Dictation (5 min)**:
   - Teacher says a word. Students write it on dry-erase boards, choosing the correct vowel team spelling.
   - Words (one from each team, then mixed):
     - "brain" (ai), "spray" (ay), "green" (ee), "dream" (ea), "toast" (oa), "throw" (ow)
     - "preach" (ea), "snail" (ai), "speed" (ee), "display" (ay), "float" (oa), "follow" (ow)
   - After each word, students hold up boards. Teacher checks. Quick correction if needed.
   - *"Remember: ai and oa go in the MIDDLE. ay and ow go at the END."*

3. **Red Word Cumulative Review (2 min)**:
   - Flash all 7: could, would, should, again, because, people, friend.
   - Students read and air-spell each. Any word missed gets 3 extra reps.

4. **Connected Text — "A Day at the Beach" (10 min)**:

   > **A Day at the Beach**
   >
   > It was a bright, clean day and the rain from last week was finally over. "We should go to the beach," said my friend. "People say the waves are big today because of the storm."
   >
   > We loaded the boat with snacks in a green tray and a big yellow float. My friend would always bring her painting supplies — she liked to paint the ocean spray.
   >
   > At the beach, we could see the foam on the waves glow in the sunlight. Seagulls would swoop and float on the breeze. Again and again, the waves would crash, then slowly flow back out to sea.
   >
   > "I could stay here all day," I said. My friend smiled. "Me too. This is a dream."

   - **First read**: Teacher reads aloud while students follow along. Students underline or highlight vowel team words as they hear them.
   - **Second read**: Choral reading — class reads together.
   - **Third read**: Partner reading — students take turns reading one sentence each. Partner listens for accuracy.
   - **Word hunt**: Students circle all the vowel team words they can find and sort them by team:
     - ai: rain, brain, painting, snacks (wait — snacks doesn't have a vowel team! Good catch!)
     - ay: day, say, spray, stay, always, tray
     - ee: beach (ea), see (ee), breeze (ee), green (ee)
     - ea: beach, sea, dream, clean
     - oa: boat, float, foam, loaded, coach (not here — just testing!)
     - ow: glow, flow, slowly, yellow
   - Also find the red words: should, friend, people, because, could, would, again
   - *"How many vowel team words did you find?"* (Count as a class — there are approximately 20+)

**Data Collection**:
- Dictation accuracy: score out of 12 words, note which vowel teams are most frequently misspelled
- Connected text: during partner reading, listen to 3–4 students. Note vowel team decoding accuracy and fluency
- Red word recognition in context: could students read them fluently in the passage?

#### Part 3: Independent Paragraph Writing — "A Pattern I Discovered" (11:00–11:30)

**Objective**: Students will independently write a paragraph with a topic sentence, 2–3 detail sentences, and a closing sentence about a pattern they have observed. (IEP Goal: Written Expression — independent paragraph writing with structure.)

**Materials**:
- Paragraph Planning Graphic Organizer (same as Wednesday — optional, students may choose to use it or not)
- Composition notebooks
- Paragraph checklist (laminated, at each desk)
- Word bank posted on board: pattern, symmetry, repeat, design, nature, multiplication, equal, observe, discover, amazing
- Modeled paragraph (Monday) and shared paragraph (Tuesday) visible on the wall for reference
- Pencils, erasers

**Lesson**:

1. **Introduction & Topic Selection (5 min)**:
   - *"This is the moment you've been building toward all week. Monday, I wrote a paragraph. Tuesday, we wrote one together. Wednesday, you wrote one with my help. Today — you fly solo."*
   - *"Your topic: A PATTERN I DISCOVERED. Think about all the patterns we've explored this week:"*
     - Patterns in nature (Monday): symmetry in leaves, spirals, branching, repetition
     - Patterns in art and architecture (Tuesday): tiles, windows, cultural designs
     - Patterns in code (Wednesday): sequences, loops, repeating instructions
     - Patterns in words: vowel teams, red word families (could/would/should)
     - Patterns in numbers: skip counting, multiplication facts
   - *"Choose ONE pattern to write about. Take 1 minute to think."*
   - Students turn and tell a partner their topic. Partner asks one question about it: *"What details will you include?"*

2. **Planning (5 min)**:
   - Students may use the graphic organizer OR plan directly in their notebook.
   - Planning must include: topic sentence idea, at least 2 details, closing sentence idea.
   - Teacher circulates quickly: *"What's your topic sentence going to say? Good. What are your details?"* Brief check-ins, not extensive help.

3. **Writing (15 min)**:
   - Students write independently in their composition notebooks.
   - Heading: **"A Pattern I Discovered"** and today's date.
   - Expectations posted:
     - Indent the first line
     - Topic sentence first
     - 2–3 detail sentences
     - Closing sentence
     - Capital letters and end punctuation
     - Spell red words correctly
     - Use your checklist when you're done
   - Teacher circulates silently for the first 5 minutes (let students work without interruption). Then begin quiet individual conferences (30 seconds each):
     - *"Read me your topic sentence."* (Is it clear?)
     - *"How many details do you have so far?"* (At least 2?)
     - *"What will your closing sentence say?"* (Planned ahead?)
   - Students who finish early: illustrate their pattern below the paragraph, or write a second paragraph about a different pattern.

4. **Self-Assessment & Peer Share (5 min)**:
   - Students complete the paragraph checklist (check off each item).
   - Partner share: read your paragraph to your partner. Partner listens and gives one specific compliment: *"I liked the detail about ___."*
   - 1–2 volunteers read to the whole class. Brief applause.

**Data Collection**:
- Collect all paragraphs. Score using the paragraph checklist rubric:
  - Topic sentence present and clear (0–2 points)
  - 2+ detail sentences that support the topic (0–3 points)
  - Closing sentence present and distinct from topic sentence (0–2 points)
  - Indentation (0–1 point)
  - Conventions: capitals, punctuation, spelling (0–2 points)
  - Total: /10 points
- **This is the Week 2 writing portfolio piece**: "A Pattern I Discovered"
- Compare to Week 1 writing baseline: growth in structure, length, and conventions?
- Note common areas for re-teaching next week

**Differentiation**:
- **Below level**: Provide the graphic organizer with the topic sentence partially written: "One interesting pattern I noticed is ___." Provide sentence starters for details: "For example, ___." / "Another thing I noticed is ___." / "This pattern is special because ___." Closing starter: "Patterns like this show that ___." Accept 4+ sentences total.
- **On level**: Write independently using the checklist and word bank. Graphic organizer available but not required. Target: 5+ sentences.
- **Above level**: Write 7+ sentences. Include at least one vocabulary word from the word bank. Add a transition word between details (also, in addition, another example). No graphic organizer.

### Exploration & Enrichment (12:00–1:00)

**Activity**: Recipe Patterns — Doubling & Halving Ingredients

**Enrichment Category**: Life Skills / Cooking (Thursday theme)

**Overview**: Students explore how recipes use patterns (measurements repeat, ratios stay consistent) and apply multiplication (doubling) and division (halving) to adjust a recipe. If possible, students prepare a simple no-bake recipe. If cooking is not feasible, students work with recipe math worksheets.

**Materials**:
- Recipe cards (printed large, 1 per group):
  - **Simple Trail Mix Recipe** (for 4 people):
    - 2 cups cereal
    - 1 cup raisins
    - 1 cup pretzels
    - ½ cup chocolate chips
    - ½ cup peanuts (check allergies — substitute sunflower seeds if needed)
- Measuring cups and spoons (1 set per group)
- Ingredients (if cooking): cereal, raisins, pretzels, chocolate chips, peanuts/substitute
- Large mixing bowls (1 per group)
- "Recipe Math" worksheet (1 per student)
- Dry-erase boards + markers
- Ziplock bags (for individual trail mix portions)
- Paper towels, wipes

**ALLERGY CHECK**: Before this activity, confirm all food allergies with parents. Substitute ingredients as needed. Have a non-food alternative ready for students with multiple allergies.

**Schedule**:

1. **Introduction: Patterns in Recipes (10 min)**:
   - Display the trail mix recipe on chart paper.
   - *"A recipe is a pattern — a set of instructions that repeats the same way every time. If you follow the pattern, you get the same result."*
   - *"But what if we need to make this recipe for MORE people? Or fewer people?"*
   - **Doubling** (x2): *"This recipe serves 4. We have 8-10 students. We need to DOUBLE it — multiply every measurement by 2."*
   - Work through the doubling together on chart paper:
     - 2 cups cereal x 2 = 4 cups cereal
     - 1 cup raisins x 2 = 2 cups raisins
     - 1 cup pretzels x 2 = 2 cups pretzels
     - ½ cup chocolate chips x 2 = 1 cup chocolate chips
     - ½ cup peanuts x 2 = 1 cup peanuts
   - *"See the pattern? Every number gets doubled — that's x2 multiplication!"*
   - **Halving** (÷2): *"What if we only needed to serve 2 people? We'd HALVE the recipe."*
   - Work through:
     - 2 cups cereal ÷ 2 = 1 cup cereal
     - 1 cup raisins ÷ 2 = ½ cup raisins
     - ½ cup chocolate chips ÷ 2 = ¼ cup chocolate chips
   - *"Halving is the opposite of doubling. It's like dividing by 2 — we'll learn more about division next week!"*

2. **Recipe Math Worksheet (10 min)**:
   - Students complete the "Recipe Math" worksheet:
   - **Problem 1**: Double this cookie recipe (serves 6 → serves 12):
     - 3 cups flour → ___
     - 2 eggs → ___
     - 1 cup sugar → ___
     - ½ cup butter → ___
     - 1 tsp vanilla → ___
   - **Problem 2**: A recipe calls for 5 cups of water for every 1 cup of rice. Complete the table:

     | Cups of Rice | 1 | 2 | 3 | 4 | 5 |
     |-------------|---|---|---|---|---|
     | Cups of Water | 5 | ___ | ___ | ___ | ___ |

     *"What multiplication pattern do you see?"* (cups of water = cups of rice x 5)

   - **Problem 3**: A smoothie recipe serves 1 person and uses 2 strawberries, 1 banana, and 5 ice cubes. How much do you need for 5 people? For 10 people?
   - **Problem 4**: Write your own recipe problem. Create a recipe for something (real or silly) and show how to double it.

3. **Cooking Activity — Trail Mix (20 min)** (or extended worksheet if no cooking):
   - Divide into groups of 4–5 students.
   - Each group gets a mixing bowl, measuring cups, ingredients, and the doubled recipe card.
   - **Jobs** (rotate within the group):
     - Reader (reads each step of the recipe)
     - Measurer (measures the ingredients)
     - Pourer (pours ingredients into the bowl)
     - Mixer (stirs the mix)
     - Quality checker (verifies measurements are correct before pouring)
   - Students follow the doubled recipe, measuring each ingredient.
   - Teacher circulates:
     - *"How did you know to use 4 cups of cereal?"* (Because 2 x 2 = 4)
     - *"What measuring cup did you use for 1 cup of chocolate chips?"* (The 1-cup — because ½ x 2 = 1)
   - When mixed, scoop into individual ziplock bags. Students may eat during closing circle.
   - *If no cooking*: Students draw pictures of each step and write the doubled measurements. They design a "dream recipe" on the back of the worksheet with all measurements and then double it.

4. **Closing Circle (10 min)**:
   - Students eat trail mix during discussion.
   - *"What math did you use today during cooking?"* (Multiplication — doubling, x2, skip counting)
   - *"What patterns did you notice in the recipe?"* (Every measurement doubled, the ratio between ingredients stayed the same)
   - Self-monitoring checklist: complete for the day.
   - Red word practice: *"Write the sentence 'My friend could eat this again because people would love it.' Check your spelling of every red word."* (All 7 red words in one sentence!)
   - Preview: *"Tomorrow is Friday — our pattern art and poetry day! Plus, you'll take your first weekly assessment. Don't worry — it's just to see how much you've grown."*
   - Dismissal at 1:00.

**Academic Connection**:
- **Math**: Doubling (x2), halving (÷2), measurement, ratio patterns, x5 multiplication in the rice problem.
- **Reading**: Following procedural text (recipe as instructions), vocabulary (ingredients, measurements, portions).
- **Writing**: The recipe format is a type of procedural writing — connection to structure and sequence.
- **Life Skills**: Measuring, following multi-step instructions, teamwork, food preparation.
