### Math Block (9:20–10:20)

**Objective**: Students will represent multiplication as equal groups using concrete manipulatives (counters and snap cubes), connecting skip counting to equal groups. Students will write multiplication expressions for concrete models. (IEP Goal: Math Computation — understand multiplication as equal groups.)

**Materials**:
- Two-color counters (20+ per student)
- Snap cubes / unifix cubes (50+ per group)
- Paper plates or small circles cut from construction paper (to represent "groups")
- Dry-erase boards + markers
- Chart paper for anchor chart: "Multiplication = Equal Groups"
- "Equal Groups" recording sheet (1 per student)
- Number lines (desktop, 0–100)

#### Warm-Up/Activation (5 min)
- **"Groups Around Us"**: Display an image (drawn on chart paper) of 3 baskets, each holding 4 apples.
- *"How many apples are there? How did you figure it out?"*
- Accept all strategies: counting one by one, skip counting, adding 4+4+4.
- *"Some of you counted by 4s. Some of you added. Some of you counted every apple. Today I'm going to show you a FAST way to think about groups — it's called multiplication."*

#### Direct Instruction (15 min) — CRA Level: Concrete

1. **What Is Multiplication? (7 min)**:
   - Write on chart paper: **"Multiplication means EQUAL GROUPS."**
   - Set out 3 paper plates on the document camera or table. Place 5 counters on each plate.
   - *"How many groups do I have?"* (3) *"How many are in each group?"* (5)
   - *"We write this as 3 x 5. The first number tells me HOW MANY GROUPS. The second number tells me HOW MANY IN EACH GROUP."*
   - Write: **3 groups of 5 = 3 x 5 = 15**
   - *"The x symbol means 'groups of.' So 3 x 5 means '3 groups of 5.'"*
   - Build 2 more examples together:
     - 4 plates, 2 counters each: *"4 groups of 2 = 4 x 2 = 8"*
     - 2 plates, 6 counters each: *"2 groups of 6 = 2 x 6 = 12"*
   - For each, have students count the total by skip counting on their number lines.

2. **Vocabulary Anchor Chart (3 min)**:
   - Add to the anchor chart:
     - **Factor** — the numbers we multiply (3 and 5)
     - **Product** — the answer (15)
     - **Equal groups** — every group has the SAME number
   - *"If I had 3 plates and one had 5 apples, one had 3 apples, and one had 4 apples — is that multiplication?"* (No — the groups aren't equal.)

3. **Skip Counting Connection (5 min)**:
   - *"Skip counting is multiplication in disguise!"*
   - Place 5 groups of 2 counters. Skip count together: 2, 4, 6, 8, 10.
   - Show on the number line: hop from 0 to 2, 2 to 4, 4 to 6, 6 to 8, 8 to 10.
   - *"5 hops of 2 = 5 x 2 = 10."*
   - Repeat with 4 groups of 5: skip count 5, 10, 15, 20. Show 4 hops of 5 on the number line. **4 x 5 = 20.**
   - *"When you skip count, you're already multiplying. You just didn't know it had a name!"*

#### Guided Practice (20 min)

**Activity: "Build It, Count It, Write It"** — Partner Work

- Each pair gets: 20 counters, 6 paper plates, a dry-erase board, a number line.
- Teacher calls out a multiplication expression. Students build it with plates and counters, skip count to find the total, and write the equation.
- Expressions to build (progressive difficulty):

| Teacher Says | Students Build | Students Write |
|-------------|---------------|----------------|
| "2 groups of 3" | 2 plates, 3 counters each | 2 x 3 = 6 |
| "5 groups of 2" | 5 plates, 2 counters each | 5 x 2 = 10 |
| "3 groups of 4" | 3 plates, 4 counters each | 3 x 4 = 12 |
| "4 groups of 5" | 4 plates, 5 counters each | 4 x 5 = 20 |
| "6 groups of 1" | 6 plates, 1 counter each | 6 x 1 = 6 |
| "3 groups of 0" | 3 plates, 0 counters each | 3 x 0 = 0 |

- After "6 groups of 1": *"What do you notice? When you multiply by 1, what happens?"* (You get the same number.) Write: **Anything x 1 = itself. This is the Identity Property.**
- After "3 groups of 0": *"What happened? Why is the answer 0?"* (If every group has nothing in it, you have nothing.) Write: **Anything x 0 = 0. This is the Zero Property.**

- Then reverse it — teacher shows a concrete model, students write the equation:
  - Show 4 plates with 3 counters → students write 4 x 3 = 12
  - Show 2 plates with 5 counters → students write 2 x 5 = 10

#### Independent Practice (10 min)
- **"Equal Groups" Recording Sheet** (10 problems):
  - Problems 1–4: A picture of plates with counters is shown. Write the multiplication equation and the product.
  - Problems 5–7: A word problem in "groups of" format. Draw a picture and write the equation.
    - "Mia has 3 bags. Each bag has 5 shells. How many shells does Mia have?"
    - "There are 4 tables. Each table has 2 books. How many books are there?"
    - "A farmer has 5 pens. Each pen has 10 chickens. How many chickens?"
  - Problems 8–10: Write your own "groups of" word problem and solve it.

- Teacher circulates. Look for:
  - Are students making equal groups (same number in each)?
  - Can they connect the groups to a multiplication expression?
  - Who is skip counting vs. counting all?

#### Closure (5 min)
- **Exit Ticket** (dry-erase boards):
  - *"Show me 3 groups of 4 with a drawing. Write the equation."* (3 x 4 = 12)
  - *"If I have 5 groups of 0, what is the answer?"* (0)
- *"Today you learned that multiplication is just equal groups. Tomorrow, we'll learn another way to show multiplication — using ARRAYS. It's like a pattern you can see."*

#### Data Collection
- Collect the Equal Groups recording sheet — score per section (pictures /4, word problems /3, create-your-own /3)
- Note who is still counting all (one by one) vs. skip counting
- Note who grasped the zero and identity properties
- Exit ticket: photograph boards for records

#### Differentiation
- **Below level (Group A)**: Use only factors of 1, 2, and 5. Provide pre-drawn circles on the recording sheet so students only need to place counters and count. Keep counters available throughout independent practice.
- **On level (Group B)**: Work with all factors as described. Use number line for skip counting support.
- **Above level (Group C)**: After completing the recording sheet, challenge: *"Can you find two different multiplication expressions that have the same product? For example, what two expressions both equal 12?"* (3x4, 2x6, 4x3, 6x2)

#### Part 1: OG Lesson — Vowel Teams ai/ay (10:30–11:00)

**Objective**: Students will read and spell words containing the vowel teams ai (rain) and ay (play), understanding the generalization that ai appears in the middle of a word/syllable and ay appears at the end. Students will learn red words: could, would, should. (IEP Goal: Decoding — vowel team syllable type.)

**Materials**:
- OG phonogram cards (full Week 1 deck + new cards: ai, ay)
- Red word cards: could, would, should
- Sand/salt trays (1 per student)
- Dry-erase boards + markers
- Word list: 20 ai/ay words (printed, 1 per student)
- Decodable text with ai/ay words
- Sky writing space (stand up, use arm in the air)

**OG Lesson Structure**:

1. **Review — Phonogram Card Drill (5 min)**:
   - Rapid drill of Week 1 cards: all consonants, short vowels, blends, digraphs.
   - Students respond chorally with the sound.
   - Speed round: go through the full deck. Note any lingering hesitations.
   - *"Great — you remembered all of those. This week, we're adding something brand new."*

2. **New Concept — Vowel Teams ai and ay (10 min)**:
   - Hold up the **ai** card. *"This is a VOWEL TEAM. Two vowels that work together to make ONE sound."*
   - *"When two vowels go walking, the first one does the talking — and it says its NAME, not its sound."*
   - **ai says /ā/** (long a). Key word: **rain**.
   - Hold up the **ay** card. *"This one also says /ā/. Key word: **play**."*
   - *"So how do we know when to use ai and when to use ay?"*
   - Write the generalization on chart paper:
     - **ai** = middle of a word or syllable (rain, train, paint, brain)
     - **ay** = end of a word or syllable (play, day, stay, spray)
   - *"Think of it this way: ai needs a consonant after it — it's shy, it hides inside the word. ay is brave — it stands at the end."*

   **Multisensory Drill**:
   - **Sky writing**: Students stand. Teacher says "ai." Students write a-i in the air with their whole arm while saying /ā/. Repeat 3 times. Then "ay" — same process.
   - **Sand tray**: Students trace ai in the sand while saying /ā/, then smooth and trace ay while saying /ā/.
   - **Phonogram card tap**: Students tap the ai card and say "a-i says /ā/ as in rain." Tap the ay card and say "a-y says /ā/ as in play."

3. **Word List Practice (8 min)**:
   - **ai words** (students read aloud, teacher points):
     - rain, train, brain, chain, paint, stain, snail, trail, plain, wait
   - **ay words**:
     - day, play, stay, pray, spray, tray, clay, gray, relay, display
   - For each word, students finger-tap the sounds: for "train" → /t/-/r/-/ā/-/n/ (4 taps — the ai makes one sound).
   - *"How many sounds in 'train'?"* (4) *"How many letters?"* (5) *"Why is there a difference?"* (Because ai is a team — two letters, one sound.)
   - Mix the lists: teacher says a word, students write on dry-erase boards. They must choose ai or ay based on position:
     - "brain" (ai — middle), "stay" (ay — end), "paint" (ai — middle), "clay" (ay — end), "chain" (ai — middle), "pray" (ay — end)
   - Students hold up boards after each word. Teacher checks spelling.

4. **Red Words — could, would, should (7 min)**:
   - *"These three words are a family — they rhyme and they're all tricky the same way."*
   - Hold up **could**. *"The 'ou' makes /ʊ/ and the 'l' is silent. That's the tricky part."*
   - For each word:
     - Teacher says the word and uses it in a sentence: *"I could run fast." / "I would like pizza." / "You should study."*
     - Students repeat the word.
     - Spell it aloud together.
     - Students write it in the sand tray 3 times.
     - Underline the tricky part: the silent **l** in all three.
     - Heart tap: hand on heart, spell from memory.
   - Memory hook: *"Could, would, should — they all have 'ould' in them. O-U-L-D. The L is silent but it's always there."*
   - Quick drill: flash all 3 cards randomly. Students read and spell on dry-erase boards.

**Data Collection**:
- Tally ai/ay word reading accuracy on class roster (out of 20 words)
- Note who grasped the ai-middle / ay-end generalization during spelling dictation
- Red word accuracy: who learned all 3? Who still confuses could/would/should?
- Sand tray observations: any letter reversals or formation issues?

#### Part 2: Modeled Paragraph Writing (11:00–11:30)

**Objective**: Students will identify the three parts of a paragraph (topic sentence, detail sentences, closing sentence) by observing a teacher-modeled paragraph and labeling its parts. (IEP Goal: Written Expression — paragraph structure.)

**Materials**:
- Chart paper and marker
- "Hamburger Paragraph" anchor chart (pre-drawn outline — fill in during lesson)
- Highlighters (3 colors per student: yellow, green, pink)
- Printed model paragraph (1 per student)
- Composition notebooks
- Paragraph structure checklist (laminated, 1 per student)

**Lesson**:

1. **Introduction: What Is a Paragraph? (5 min)**:
   - *"Last week, you wrote sentences. This week, you're going to learn how sentences work TOGETHER to build something bigger — a paragraph."*
   - Hold up a hamburger image (drawn on chart paper):
     - Top bun = **Topic sentence** (tells the reader what the paragraph is about)
     - Fillings = **Detail sentences** (give examples, facts, descriptions — 2 or 3)
     - Bottom bun = **Closing sentence** (wraps it up, restates the main idea in a new way)
   - *"A paragraph is like a hamburger. The buns hold it together, and the filling is the good stuff."*
   - Post the **Hamburger Paragraph** anchor chart on the wall.

2. **Teacher Models a Paragraph (10 min)**:
   - *"I'm going to write a paragraph right in front of you. Watch how I think out loud."*
   - Topic: A pattern in nature (connects to today's enrichment).
   - Think aloud while writing on chart paper:

   > *"My topic is patterns on leaves. So my topic sentence needs to tell the reader what my paragraph is about..."*

   Write: **"Leaves are full of interesting patterns."**

   > *"Now I need details — specific things I've noticed about leaf patterns..."*

   Write: **"Many leaves have veins that branch out from the center like a tree."**
   Write: **"Some leaves are perfectly symmetrical — if you fold them in half, both sides match."**
   Write: **"Other leaves have wavy or jagged edges that repeat the same shape over and over."**

   > *"Now I need a closing sentence that wraps it up. I don't just copy my topic sentence — I restate the idea in a new way."*

   Write: **"Next time you pick up a leaf, look closely — nature's patterns are everywhere."**

   - Read the full paragraph aloud.

3. **Label the Model (8 min)**:
   - Distribute the printed copy of the model paragraph to each student.
   - Students use highlighters:
     - **Yellow** = topic sentence
     - **Green** = detail sentences
     - **Pink** = closing sentence
   - Discuss: *"How many detail sentences did I write?"* (3) *"Could I have written 2? Could I have written 4?"* (Yes — 2 to 3 is the target for now.)
   - *"What makes the closing sentence different from the topic sentence?"* (It says the same idea but in new words; it feels like an ending.)
   - Introduce the **Paragraph Checklist**:
     - [ ] My paragraph has a topic sentence.
     - [ ] I included 2–3 detail sentences.
     - [ ] My closing sentence wraps it up.
     - [ ] I indented the first line.
     - [ ] Every sentence starts with a capital and ends with a period.

4. **Notebook Work (7 min)**:
   - Students copy the anchor chart labels into their journals under the heading: **"Paragraph Structure — Week 2"**
     - Topic sentence = tells what the paragraph is about
     - Detail sentences (2–3) = give examples, facts, or descriptions
     - Closing sentence = wraps up the main idea in a new way
   - Below that, students copy the model paragraph into their journals (handwriting practice + internalizing the structure).
   - If time permits, students write one sentence about a pattern they've noticed: *"I see a pattern in/on ___."* (This will become the seed for their own paragraph later this week.)

**Data Collection**:
- Did students correctly highlight the three parts of the paragraph?
- Note who understands the distinction between topic sentence and closing sentence
- Note handwriting fluency: who copied the paragraph quickly vs. who struggled with the volume
- Collect notebooks at end of day — check journal entries

**Differentiation**:
- **Below level**: Provide the paragraph pre-printed with blanks for highlighting only (no copying required). Sentence starters for the "pattern I noticed" sentence.
- **On level**: Complete the highlighting and copying as described.
- **Above level**: After copying, write 2–3 sentences about a pattern they've noticed. Try to include a topic sentence and at least one detail.

### Exploration & Enrichment (12:00–1:00)

**Activity**: Nature Patterns Walk — Symmetry in Leaves, Spirals in Shells

**Enrichment Category**: Nature & Patterns (Monday theme)

**Overview**: Students explore the outdoor space with a "pattern hunter" lens, searching for symmetry, spirals, repetition, and tessellation in natural objects. They document findings in their nature journals with sketches and descriptive notes. This directly connects to the week's reading/writing (pattern paragraph) and math (equal groups as a repeating pattern).

**Materials**:
- Nature journals (from Week 1, continued)
- Pencils + colored pencils in ziplock bags
- Magnifying glasses (1 per student or pair)
- Small hand mirrors (for symmetry testing)
- Paper bags for collecting natural objects
- Clipboards (1 per student)
- "Pattern Hunt" checklist (pre-printed, 1 per student — see below)
- Camera or tablet (teacher — for documentation)

**"Pattern Hunt" Checklist** (students carry this on their clipboard):

> Find and sketch at least ONE example of each:
> - [ ] **Symmetry** — something that looks the same on both sides (leaf, butterfly wing, flower)
> - [ ] **Spiral** — something that curls outward from a center point (snail shell, fern frond, pinecone)
> - [ ] **Repetition** — something that repeats the same shape or color over and over (fence slats, petals on a flower, bark texture)
> - [ ] **Branching** — something that splits into smaller and smaller parts (tree branches, leaf veins, cracks in pavement)
> - [ ] **BONUS: A pattern you name yourself** — find something and describe the pattern in your own words

**Schedule**:

1. **Introduction (5 min, inside)**:
   - *"Last week, we went outside to observe nature. This week, we're going back — but this time we're looking for PATTERNS."*
   - Show 3 images on chart paper (drawn or printed): a symmetrical butterfly, a spiral seashell, a repeating honeycomb.
   - Name the 4 pattern types: symmetry, spiral, repetition, branching.
   - *"Scientists and mathematicians study these patterns. They're not random — there are reasons they exist in nature."*
   - Distribute Pattern Hunt checklists and review expectations: stay together, sketch in journals, check off patterns as you find them.

2. **Nature Walk (20 min, outside)**:
   - Walk a planned route. At 4 stopping points, pause and focus:
     - **Stop 1 — Symmetry Station**: Find a leaf. Hold the mirror along the center vein. *"Does the reflection match the other side? If yes, it's symmetrical!"* Students sketch the leaf in their journal and draw the line of symmetry.
     - **Stop 2 — Repetition Station**: Look at a fence, sidewalk, or row of plants. *"What repeats? How many times does the pattern repeat?"* Math connection: *"If one section of the fence has 4 slats, and there are 5 sections, how many slats total?"* (5 x 4 = 20 — multiplication!)
     - **Stop 3 — Spiral & Branching Station**: Examine a tree or bush closely. *"Look at how the branches split. Big branches become medium branches become small branches. That's called branching."* Look for spirals: curled ferns, snail shells, or the spiral arrangement of seeds on a pinecone.
     - **Stop 4 — Free Hunt**: 5 minutes for students to find their bonus pattern. *"Find any pattern and be ready to describe it."*
   - Teacher circulates, takes photos, asks prompting questions:
     - *"What type of pattern is this?"*
     - *"How would you describe this pattern to someone who can't see it?"*
     - *"Where else have you seen a pattern like this?"*

3. **Journal Documentation (15 min, inside)**:
   - Back at tables, students complete their nature journal pages:
     - At least 2 detailed sketches of patterns found (with labels: type of pattern, where found)
     - 2–3 descriptive sentences about their favorite pattern discovery
     - Math connection at the bottom of the page: *"Draw an equal groups picture using something from nature. Example: 3 flowers with 5 petals each = 3 x 5 = 15 petals."*
   - Students check off items on their Pattern Hunt checklist.

4. **Share & Connect (10 min)**:
   - **Gallery Walk**: Students place their open journals on their desks. Class walks around silently, looking at each other's patterns.
   - Whole group: 3–4 students share their favorite discovery. Teacher records pattern types found on chart paper under the heading "Patterns We Discovered."
   - Connect to the week: *"You found patterns in nature today. Tomorrow we'll find patterns in art and architecture. By Friday, you'll be pattern experts!"*
   - Connect to reading/writing: *"By Thursday, you'll write your OWN paragraph about a pattern you discovered — just like the one I modeled today."*

5. **Closing Circle (10 min)**:
   - Each student shares one word to describe today (quick whip-around).
   - Self-monitoring checklist: complete for the day. NEW item: *"Did I look for a pattern today?"*
   - **Red word practice (rapid)**: Flash could, would, should. Students respond chorally. Spell each one in the air (sky writing).
   - *"Tonight, practice your red words: could, would, should. Look for patterns at home — patterns on your plate at dinner, patterns on your clothes, patterns in your neighborhood. Come back tomorrow ready to share one."*
   - Dismissal at 1:00.

**Academic Connection**:
- **Reading/Writing**: Descriptive vocabulary, observation-to-writing pipeline, model paragraph topic (patterns in nature) directly feeds Thursday's independent writing.
- **Math**: Equal groups in nature (petals on flowers, segments on insects, sections on fruit) — reinforces multiplication as a real-world concept.
