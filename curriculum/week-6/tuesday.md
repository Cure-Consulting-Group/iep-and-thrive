### Math Block (9:20–10:20)

**Objective**: Students will review cumulative math skills through games and begin assembling their math portfolio (selecting best work samples, graphing fact fluency growth). (IEP Goal: All math domains — cumulative demonstration of skills.)

**Materials**:
- Math review game stations (prepared):
  - Station 1: "Fraction War" (fraction cards — compare, larger fraction wins)
  - Station 2: "Multiplication Bingo" (caller reads a problem, players cover the product)
  - Station 3: "Money Match" (price cards + coin/bill cards — match exact amounts)
  - Station 4: "Word Problem Challenge" (10 multi-step problems on task cards, answer key for self-check)
- Weekly probe data sheets (all 5 weeks — for each student)
- "My Math Growth Story" poster template (large paper with sections)
- Graph paper
- Rulers, colored pencils, markers
- All manipulatives available as choice tools

#### Math Review Games (30 min — 2 rotations of 15 min)

- **Station Setup**: 4 stations, 2–3 students per station. Rotate after 15 minutes.

  **Station 1 — Fraction War**:
  - Each player flips a fraction card. Compare fractions (use benchmarks or strips to verify).
  - Larger fraction wins the round. If equal, war (flip again).
  - Self-differentiating: weaker students rely on strips; stronger students reason mentally.

  **Station 2 — Multiplication Bingo**:
  - 5x5 bingo card with products. Caller (student or teacher) reads a multiplication problem (e.g., "7 times 8"). Students find and cover the product (56).
  - 5 in a row = Bingo!
  - Differentiation: Some cards have products only within x5 and x10; others include all facts through x12.

  **Station 3 — Money Match**:
  - 15 price cards ($1.50, $3.25, $7.80, etc.) and corresponding sets of coin/bill combinations.
  - Students match each price card to the correct coin/bill combination.
  - Extension: Find a DIFFERENT combination that makes the same amount.

  **Station 4 — Word Problem Challenge**:
  - 10 task cards with multi-step problems covering all operations, fractions, measurement, time, and money.
  - Students solve on dry-erase boards or scratch paper.
  - Answer key available for self-checking.
  - Problems range from 1-step (Level 1) to 3-step (Level 3) — students choose their level.

#### Math Portfolio Assembly (20 min)

1. **Select Math Work Samples (10 min)**:
   - Students go through their math folders and select 2–3 work samples that show growth:
     - One from early weeks (Weeks 1–2)
     - One from later weeks (Weeks 4–5)
     - One they're most proud of
   - For each selection, write a sticky note: *"I chose this because ___."*

2. **Graph Your Growth (10 min)**:
   - Distribute weekly probe data sheets (teacher has recorded scores for each student across 5 weeks).
   - Students plot their math computation scores on a bar graph:
     - X-axis: Week 1, 2, 3, 4, 5
     - Y-axis: Number correct (scale based on individual data)
   - *"What do you notice about your graph? Is it going up, staying the same, or going down?"*
   - Most students will see an upward trend. Celebrate this.
   - *"This graph goes on your Math Growth Story poster — it's PROOF of your hard work."*

#### Closure (5 min)
- *"Tomorrow and Thursday we'll create the full Math Growth Story poster with your graph, your best work, and your reflection."*
- Quick share: *"Name one math skill you can do now that you couldn't do 6 weeks ago."*

#### Differentiation
- **Below level (Group A)**: During games, pair with a supportive peer. At Station 1 (Fraction War), use fraction strips for every comparison. At Station 4, work Level 1 problems only. For graphing, teacher provides pre-drawn axes; student just fills in bars. Sticky note annotations may be verbal (teacher writes what student says).
- **On level (Group B)**: Full game rotation and portfolio assembly. Graph independently. Write sticky note annotations.
- **Above level (Group C)**: At Station 4, work Level 3 problems and create one original problem for a classmate. Graph includes both computation and concept scores. Write a full reflection paragraph about math growth (not just sticky notes).

#### Data Collection
- Observe during games: Which skills are students confident with? Which still need support?
- Record game performance informally (especially word problem station).

#### Part 1: OG Lesson — Silent Letters + Homophones (10:30–10:50)

**Objective**: Students will read words with silent letter patterns (kn, wr, gn, mb), distinguish between common homophones, and identify homographs. (IEP Goal: Decoding — apply knowledge of advanced phonics patterns; Comprehension — use context to determine meaning of homophones/homographs.)

**Materials**:
- Silent letter word cards:
  - kn: know, knee, knit, knot, knock, knife, knight, kneel, knob, knew
  - wr: write, wrap, wrist, wreck, wrong, wring, wrestle, wrote
  - gn: gnat, gnaw, gnome, sign, design, align
  - mb: climb, lamb, thumb, numb, comb, bomb, crumb, limb, plumb
- Homophone pair cards: there/their/they're, to/too/two, write/right, no/know, hear/here, sea/see, flour/flower, knight/night, wrap/rap, knot/not
- Homograph cards: lead (metal vs. guide), wind (air vs. turn), tear (rip vs. cry), close (near vs. shut), read (present vs. past), live (alive vs. event), bow (ribbon vs. bend)
- Dry-erase boards + markers
- Sand/salt trays

**OG Lesson Structure**:

1. **Cumulative Review (3 min)**:
   - Quick phonogram flash: 10 cards (mixed patterns from Weeks 1–5).
   - Quick morpheme flash: un-, -ful, re-, -less, pre-, -ness, dis-, -ly.

2. **New Concept — Silent Letters (8 min)**:
   - Write on the board: **know, write, gnat, climb**
   - *"Read these words. What do you notice?"* (Each has a letter that's silent.)
   - **kn**: *"In words that start with 'kn,' the K is silent. We only hear the /n/. This comes from Old English — hundreds of years ago, people DID pronounce the K!"*
   - Read the kn word list together. Students circle the silent k.
   - **wr**: *"In words that start with 'wr,' the W is silent. We only hear /r/."*
   - Read the wr list. Circle the silent w.
   - **gn**: *"When G and N are together, the G can be silent. At the beginning: gnat, gnaw. At the end: sign, design."*
   - Read the gn list.
   - **mb**: *"At the end of a word, when M and B are together, the B is silent: climb, lamb, thumb."*
   - Read the mb list.
   - **Memory trick**: *"The silent letter is there for spelling — even though you don't hear it. When you spell these words, remember: 'the K is silent but not invisible!'"*
   - Sand tray practice: Write 4 words (know, write, sign, thumb). Circle the silent letter.

3. **Homophones (7 min)**:
   - *"Homophones are words that SOUND the same but have DIFFERENT spellings and DIFFERENT meanings."*
   - Show 3 key sets:
     - **there / their / they're**: there = a place ("over there"), their = belonging to them ("their dog"), they're = they are ("they're coming")
     - **to / too / two**: to = direction ("go to school"), too = also or too much ("me too"), two = the number ("two cats")
     - **write / right**: write = put words on paper, right = correct or direction
   - Practice: Read sentences on the board. Students write the correct homophone on boards:
     - "_____ going to the park." (They're)
     - "I need ___ pencils." (two)
     - "Please ___ your name." (write)
     - "Is this the ___ answer?" (right)
     - "Put the book over ___." (there)
     - "I want to come ___." (too)

4. **Homographs — Quick Introduction (2 min)**:
   - *"Homographs are the opposite problem — same spelling, different meaning, sometimes different pronunciation."*
   - Show 3 examples:
     - "The wind blew hard." / "Please wind the clock." (different pronunciation + meaning)
     - "A tear rolled down her face." / "Don't tear the paper." (different pronunciation + meaning)
     - "Close the door." / "She sat close to me." (different pronunciation + meaning)
   - *"Context — the words around it — tells you which meaning and pronunciation to use."*

#### Differentiation
- **Below level (Group A)**: Focus on silent letters only (skip homophones/homographs for today — cover Wednesday). Focus on kn and wr patterns (most common). Provide a silent letter reference card for desk. Sand tray practice with 3 words only.
- **On level (Group B)**: Full lesson — silent letters + the three main homophone sets. Practice sentences. Skip homographs (cover Wednesday).
- **Above level (Group C)**: Full lesson including homographs. After the lesson, write 3 original sentences using homophones correctly. Challenge: *"Can you find a word that has BOTH a silent letter AND a prefix or suffix?"* (e.g., unknowing, rewriting)

**Students work on portfolio assembly while teacher pulls for assessments.**

**Activities** (students choose or rotate):

1. **Portfolio Assembly Continued (15 min)**:
   - Continue selecting and organizing pieces from Monday.
   - Add sticky note annotations to each selected piece.
   - Arrange pieces in order (Week 1 → Week 6).

2. **Comprehension Strategy Reflection (10 min)**:
   - Students open their reading response journals.
   - On a fresh page, write: **"My Reading Strategy Toolkit"**
   - List every comprehension strategy they remember:
     - Predicting, Monitoring (does this make sense?), Retelling, Main Idea + Details, Story Elements, Sequencing, Inferring ("I read... I know... So I think..."), Author's Purpose (PIE), Summarizing (Topic + Details + So What), Text Features
   - Circle the 2–3 strategies they use most. Write 1–2 sentences about each: *"I use ___ when ___ because ___."*
   - This page becomes a portfolio piece.

3. **Reading Response: Favorite Text (15 min)**:
   - Students write about their favorite book or text from the summer.
   - Prompt: *"What was your favorite text we read this summer? What was it about? Why was it your favorite? How did it connect to something in your life?"*
   - Write at least 5 sentences (below level: 3 sentences with sentence frames).
   - This becomes a portfolio piece.

#### Differentiation
- **Below level**: Portfolio assembly with teacher aide support. Strategy reflection uses a checklist format (check the strategies you know, circle your favorites). Reading response uses sentence frames: "My favorite text was ___. It was about ___. I liked it because ___."
- **On level**: Full portfolio assembly. Strategy reflection and reading response written independently.
- **Above level**: Portfolio includes detailed annotations. Strategy reflection includes specific examples from texts read. Reading response includes text-to-self, text-to-text, or text-to-world connection.

### Exploration & Enrichment (12:00–1:00)

**Activity**: Create a Mini-Museum Exhibit of Summer Learning

**Overview**: Each student creates a small exhibit display showcasing their summer learning journey. These exhibits become part of the Friday showcase.

**Materials**:
- Tri-fold display boards or large poster boards (1 per student)
- Index cards (for labels and descriptions)
- Markers, colored pencils, glue sticks, tape
- Scissors
- Students' work samples (from portfolio folders — they'll select pieces to display)
- "Museum Exhibit Checklist":
  - [ ] Exhibit title
  - [ ] 3–5 items on display (work samples, projects, artwork)
  - [ ] Written label for each item (what it is, when you made it, what it shows)
  - [ ] A brief "About the Artist/Scholar" card (name, grade, 2 sentences about yourself as a learner)
  - [ ] Neat, organized, visually appealing

**Schedule**:

1. **Introduction (5 min)**:
   - *"In a museum, every exhibit tells a story. YOUR exhibit tells the story of YOUR learning this summer."*
   - Show a teacher-made example: a tri-fold board with sections labeled "Reading Growth," "My Best Writing," "Math Journey," with work samples attached and index card labels.
   - Review the checklist.

2. **Planning (10 min)**:
   - Students sketch a layout plan on scrap paper: Where will each piece go? What labels do they need?
   - Select 3–5 work samples or projects to display (these can be COPIES — originals stay in portfolio).
   - Teacher or aide helps photocopy any pieces students want both in their portfolio and on their display board.
   - Write an exhibit title: "My Summer of Learning" or "Growing as a Reader and Mathematician" or student's choice.

3. **Creation (25 min)**:
   - Students build their exhibits.
   - Attach work samples, write labels on index cards, decorate.
   - Labels should include: What the item is, When it was created, and What skill it demonstrates.
   - *"Write your labels clearly — your family needs to be able to read them on Friday."*
   - Teacher circulates with encouragement and practical support (cutting, gluing, organizing).

4. **"About the Scholar" Card (5 min)**:
   - Each student writes an index card:
     - Name and grade
     - *"This summer I learned ___."*
     - *"I am most proud of ___."*
   - Attach to the exhibit.

5. **Gallery Preview (5 min)**:
   - Quick walk around the room to see everyone's exhibits in progress.
   - Positive feedback only: each student gives one compliment to a neighbor's exhibit.
   - *"These will be finished and displayed on Friday for your families."*
   - Store exhibits carefully — lean against walls or stack flat.

6. **Closing Circle (10 min)**:
   - *"What was it like to see all your work from the summer in one place?"*
   - *"What surprised you about how much you've done?"*
   - Self-monitoring checklist.
   - Preview tomorrow: *"Tomorrow is a big day — writing post-assessment in the morning, final STEM challenge in the afternoon, and we start making your Math Growth Story poster."*
   - **Remind about assessments**: *"Tomorrow we finish our post-assessments. These are the last tests — and they're the SAME tests from Week 1. Just do your best."*
   - Dismissal at 1:00.

**Academic Connections**:
- **Writing**: Descriptive labels, exhibit descriptions, "About the Scholar" card.
- **Reading**: Reviewing and reflecting on texts read during the summer.
- **Math**: Organizing and displaying data (growth graphs may be included on exhibit).
- **Executive Function**: Planning, organizing, multi-step project execution.

**Differentiation**:
- **Below level**: Provide a pre-made exhibit template with labeled sections (just fill in). Teacher selects 3 work samples for the student. Labels can be dictated to teacher/aide. Focus on 3 items only.
- **On level**: Full activity. 4–5 items with written labels. Independent creation with checklist.
- **Above level**: 5+ items with detailed written descriptions (2–3 sentences per label, explaining what the piece shows about their growth). Add a "Growth Timeline" section showing progression from Week 1 to Week 6. Write captions as if for a real museum.
